# Alone on the Bosphorus

---

### Chapter 7: The Game at Karapınar

The village had no particular reason to be celebrating.

This was, Cornelis would learn later, not unusual. The plateau people did not require occasions in the way that Europeans required occasions — a saint's day, a harvest, a royal anniversary, something with a name and a sanction. What was required was simpler: an evening with no rain, a reasonable fire, someone with an instrument, and enough people who had finished their day's work. The rest followed.

They had stopped at Karapınar — a small village, a well, a *han* of sorts that was really just a wide courtyard with a roof on one side — because one of the pack animals had thrown a shoe and Mehmet, who handled these things, required an hour with the village's farrier. The hour became two. By the time the shoe was sorted the light was going amber and Yusuf made the decision without discussion: they would stay.

Cornelis was sitting outside the *han* with his ledger when he heard the drumming start.

Not distant — three streets away, if streets was the right word for the packed-earth lanes between the stone houses. A single drum at first, then a second finding the same rhythm, and then something stringed underneath it, lower, a sound he felt in his sternum before he properly heard it.

He looked up from the ledger.

A boy was standing ten feet away watching him with the frank assessment that children apply to foreigners. Perhaps eight years old. He said something in Turkish.

Cornelis caught the word for *come* — *gel* — and not much else.

"What is it?" Cornelis asked, in Turkish.

The boy switched without hesitation to a mime of someone playing a drum, then pointed in the direction of the sound, then beckoned again with the full-arm gesture of someone for whom half-measures were unnecessary.

Cornelis looked at his ledger. The accounts were current. The light was failing anyway.

He closed it.

---

The celebration was in the wide space before the village mosque — not inside, not religious in any formal sense, just using the open ground where the lanes converged. There were perhaps sixty people, which was probably most of the village. A fire. Three musicians: the two drummers he had heard, and a man with a *saz* — the long-necked lute of the plateau — who played with his eyes closed and the expression of someone who had gone somewhere else and left his hands behind to manage.

Nobody announced Cornelis. Nobody needed to. He was simply there, the pale foreigner in the coat, standing at the edge of the firelight with the boy who had collected him, and the village registered his presence and returned its attention to the music, which was the courteous response and also the correct one.

He stood and listened.

The music was nothing like the hornpipe in the *Altın Ay* — nothing like anything he had heard. The rhythms were in places he didn't expect them, the melody of the *saz* moving in intervals that his ear tried to resolve into something familiar and couldn't quite. It was not unpleasant. It was disorienting in the way that a room with one wall at a slight angle is disorienting — nothing obviously wrong, but the sense of something the eye keeps trying to correct.

Then the dancing started and he stopped thinking about the music structurally.

It was the men who danced first, which surprised him — in Amsterdam dancing was a mixed affair, formal, partnered. Here it was different: a line of men, arms around each other's shoulders, moving in a pattern that was complex enough to require attention but communal enough that the attention was shared. The line grew as men joined it. The pattern changed without announcement, the whole group shifting at once as if the decision had been made collectively and below the level of speech.

He watched.

And then someone grabbed his arm.

---

He was not the boy — the boy had drifted toward the fire and was eating something. This was a young man of perhaps twenty, broad-shouldered, with the look of someone who worked with his hands and had decided, with complete goodwill and no particular consultation, that the foreigner standing at the edge needed to be somewhere else.

Cornelis said, in Turkish, that he didn't know the steps.

The young man — whose name, produced cheerfully and immediately, was Tarık — said something that Cornelis's Turkish was not quite equal to but whose meaning was apparent from the gesture: that not knowing the steps was not a reason, it was a starting point.

He was pulled into the line.

The arm around his shoulder was Tarık's, solid and matter-of-fact. On the other side an older man whose name he didn't catch simply adjusted his grip and continued dancing as if a Dutchman slotting in beside him was unremarkable. The pattern continued. Cornelis's feet did what feet do when the body is committed before the mind has consented — they found something, not the right steps but a reasonable approximation, and the line moved and he moved with it and twice he went wrong and the grip on his shoulder corrected him without comment, the way you correct a door that is swinging slightly off true.

The music got faster.

He stopped trying to think about the steps. This was not a decision so much as a surrender — his mind simply ran out of processing capacity and his body was left to manage alone. What happened then was unexpected: he got better. Not good, but better. The feet finding the rhythm not through counting but through something underneath counting, the same thing that kept a man upright on a moving ship without calculating balance, the thing that was in the body before education got to it.

He was breathing hard. He was also, he realized, grinning.

---

The dancing gave way to the game at some point without a clear transition.

It was called — Tarık explained this with the assistance of demonstration and the two words of Italian he turned out to possess — *güreş*, which meant wrestling, though what Cornelis witnessed was not quite what he understood wrestling to mean. The formal Ottoman kind was an ancient sport, Cornelis would later learn, with rules and tradition and greased leather trousers, but this was its village cousin: two men, a circle of onlookers, the object to put the other man on the ground.

It was done with laughter. This was the thing that surprised him most. The wrestlers grinned at each other. The crowd offered commentary that was clearly comic from the responses it produced. A man was thrown and landed badly and came up laughing at himself. The whole thing had an atmosphere less of competition than of shared experiment — what happens when these two particular bodies try to solve this particular problem.

Cornelis was watching with genuine absorption when Tarık appeared at his elbow and said something.

"No," Cornelis said, in Turkish. Then, because this seemed insufficient: "Absolutely not."

Tarık grinned. Said something to the crowd. The crowd, which had not previously been paying attention to Cornelis, paid attention to Cornelis.

"I am a factor," Cornelis said, to Tarık, to the crowd, to the night in general. "I do accounts."

This was translated — by Tarık, approximately — and produced a response from the crowd that required no translation.

He looked around for Yusuf. Yusuf was across the fire talking to two older men, a cup in his hand, and when his eyes met Cornelis's his expression was of a man who is choosing, deliberately, not to intervene. The ghost of the almost-laugh.

Cornelis took off his coat.

---

His opponent was not Tarık — that would have been unfair in the wrong direction, Tarık being built like a warehouse. It was a man roughly his own size, perhaps a farmer, who shook his hand before they began with the businesslike warmth of a man who has nothing against you personally and intends to put you on the ground.

What Cornelis knew about wrestling: nothing useful.

What he had: a low center of gravity that he had not known about before this evening, the result of years at a desk giving him a certain groundedness that he had never had occasion to discover. And — more surprisingly — the ledger-keeper's instinct for imbalance. He had spent years finding where weight was distributed incorrectly, where a column didn't add up, where something was heavier on one side than it should be. This was, it turned out, not entirely unlike finding the moment when a man's weight shifted past his center.

He was thrown twice. Both times quickly, cleanly, and to general approval. He came up from the second fall with his mouth full of dust and the crowd making a sound that he identified, after a moment, as encouragement rather than mockery.

The third time he didn't think. His opponent moved in and something in Cornelis's body — the part that had learned the dance without being taught — registered the shift of weight and moved with it rather than against it, and then the man was going sideways and Cornelis was not, and there was a moment of surprised suspension and then the farmer was on the ground and Cornelis was standing over him with absolutely no idea how he had gotten there.

The crowd made a different sound.

The farmer looked up at him from the ground with an expression of genuine surprise and then laughed — a real laugh, the laugh of a man who finds the universe occasionally capable of humor — and took Cornelis's offered hand and got up, and shook it again, and said something that Tarık translated into a single word of Italian:

"*Fortunato.*"

Lucky.

"Yes," Cornelis agreed, in Turkish. "Very."

---

Later — much later, the fire burned low, most of the village gone home — he sat with Tarık and two others whose names he had and would retain, drinking something that was not wine and was considerably more direct in its intentions than wine, and they talked in the way that men talk when language is partially shared and the gaps are filled by gesture and goodwill, and it was slow and sometimes wrong and twice produced laughter from errors he couldn't locate, and he did not mind any of it.

Tarık asked him, with patience and repetition, where he was from.

Cornelis told him. The Netherlands. The sea. The ship. The quay.

Tarık absorbed this. Asked how long ago.

Cornelis counted backward. "Four months," he said. "Perhaps five."

Tarık looked at him for a moment, then at the plateau darkness beyond the fire, then back. He said something slow enough to follow: *you are far from the sea.*

"Yes," Cornelis said.

Tarık said something else. Cornelis missed it and asked him to repeat it. He repeated it twice and Cornelis still couldn't parse it and in the end Tarık mimed it — a man walking, then stopping, then looking around, then shrugging, then sitting down as if he had decided the place he'd stopped was fine.

Cornelis looked at him.

"Yes," he said, after a moment. "Something like that."

He did not write in the ledger that night. He slept instead, on a mat in the *han* courtyard, with the plateau stars overhead in their impossible quantities and the last of the fire a red suggestion at the edge of his vision, and his body — bruised in two specific places from the falls, warm in a way that was not just the drink — already becoming the body of a man who had been thrown and gotten up and thrown someone else, which was a different body than the one that had boarded a ship in Amsterdam.

In the morning he wrote one line:

*Karapınar. I don't know how I did it. I think that was the point.*

### Chapter 8: Kayseri

The road east of Karapınar was the same road it had been before, which was the strange thing.

The same plateau light, the same wind with its plateau indifference, the same pale thread running southeast through the long grass and occasional stands of poplar that marked where water was underground. *Amsterdam* had his opinions about the wind as always. The pack animals moved at their pace. Mehmet rode at the front with Yusuf and occasionally looked back at Cornelis with an expression that was not quite respect and not quite amusement but occupied the territory between them.

What was different was Cornelis.

He could not have specified it precisely — he was a man who liked to specify things precisely, and the imprecision bothered him mildly in the way that an unbalanced column bothers an accountant even when the sum is close enough for practical purposes. But something had shifted in Karapınar that was not reversible. The body that had been thrown twice and thrown a man once was not the body that had ridden out of Istanbul four months ago, and it was not only the bruises, which had faded to yellow at the edges and would be gone by Kayseri. It was something in the posture. The way he sat *Amsterdam* — Mehmet's corrections had accumulated, the door was becoming a rider. The way he looked at the road rather than across it.

He rode one morning without opening the ledger. This had not happened before.

---

Yusuf noticed. Yusuf noticed most things and said something about perhaps a third of them, which was a ratio Cornelis had come to understand as deliberate.

"You are quieter," Yusuf said, on the third morning out of Karapınar, riding alongside.

"I am thinking," Cornelis said.

"About what?"

Cornelis considered the honest answer. "About the difference between knowing a thing and having done it." He paused. "I knew what the plateau was before we crossed it. I had read accounts. I had seen maps. I thought this was knowledge."

Yusuf said nothing, which was his way of indicating that Cornelis should continue.

"It wasn't knowledge," Cornelis said. "It was the shape of knowledge. The container without the thing inside."

Yusuf rode for a moment. "In Turkish," he said, "we have a word — *tecrübe.* It means experience, but not quite. It means the knowledge that comes through the body, through doing. It is considered a different category from what is learned from books."

"We have no such word in Dutch," Cornelis said.

"I know," said Yusuf.

They rode on. The plateau went about its plateau business around them, enormous and unhurried.

"Yusuf," Cornelis said, after a while.

"Yes."

"The man in Kayseri. The accounts that require care." He had been carrying this since Konya. "Tell me what I'm riding into."

Yusuf was quiet long enough that Cornelis thought he might decline. Then: "His name is Adem Şahin. He has been trading on the eastern routes for thirty years — beyond Kayseri, into the passes, toward Tabriz when the roads allow and the politics allow, which is not always simultaneously." He paused. "He is very wealthy and not entirely comfortable with what being very wealthy requires him to do. His accounts reflect this discomfort. They are not dishonest — Adem is not dishonest, this is important to understand — but they are the accounts of a man who has made many arrangements that he would prefer not to examine too closely."

"And you want me to examine them."

"I want you to organize them. Make them legible. There is a difference."

Cornelis thought about this. "What is he afraid he'll find?"

Yusuf almost laughed. "He is afraid he will find that he is richer than he thinks, because some of that richness has obligations attached that he has been successfully not thinking about."

"And if he is?"

"Then he will need to decide what to do about the obligations. That is not your problem. Your problem is the ledger." He glanced at Cornelis. "Can you do that? Look at accounts and report what is there, without advising what should be done about it?"

Cornelis thought about the Leiden professor who had taught him to read Rumi. About the Dutch Reformed church's very specific opinions about usury and obligation. About nine coins and a ledger on a quay in Istanbul.

"Yes," he said. "I can do that."

---

Kayseri arrived on the ninth day as a dark mass against the improbable backdrop of Mount Erciyes — a volcanic cone, snow-capped even now in early summer, rising from the plain with the clean geometric confidence of something that had decided to be a mountain and seen no reason to be modest about it. The city at its foot was old in the way that plateau cities were old: not loudly, not ornamentally, but with a settled permanence that made Istanbul feel, by comparison, almost recent.

This had been the Hittites. This had been the Persians. Alexander had passed through. The Romans had fortified it. The Byzantines had held it until they hadn't. The Seljuks, then the Mongols, then the Ottomans — the city had been claimed by so many successive certainties about how the world was organized that it had developed, Cornelis sensed on entering it, a quality of patient scepticism about all of them. History had happened here and would continue to happen here, and Kayseri's job was to outlast it.

The stonework was the dark volcanic basalt of the mountain — whole streets of it, the buildings absorbing the light rather than reflecting it, giving the city a density that Bursa's warm sandstone and Istanbul's imperial marble did not have. The market was smaller than Konya's but more concentrated, the goods from further east present in a way he had not seen before: spices whose names he didn't know, textiles in patterns that did not come from the plateau, a smell in certain alleys that he associated with his reading about the silk road rather than anything he had actually encountered.

He was further from Amsterdam than he had allowed himself to think about directly.

---

Adem Şahin's house was in the old quarter, built against the city wall — a substantial house, the kind that announced its owner's wealth through quality rather than display. The wooden screens on the upper windows were finely made. The courtyard garden, glimpsed through the gate, had a fountain that worked. These were the details, Cornelis had learned, that meant something on the plateau.

Adem himself was not what Cornelis had constructed from Yusuf's description.

He had expected — the word *uncomfortable* had lodged in his mind — someone visibly burdened, a man with the look of accounts-anxiety that he recognized from certain Amsterdam merchants who had grown faster than their record-keeping. What he found instead was a man of perhaps sixty, lean and upright with a white beard trimmed close and eyes that were simultaneously warm and measuring, who greeted Yusuf with the embrace of a genuine old friendship and turned to Cornelis with an attention that was immediate and specific.

"The Dutchman," he said, in Arabic-inflected Turkish that Cornelis caught most of.

"The factor," Yusuf said, using the word again.

Adem looked at Cornelis for a moment, then said, in careful and surprising Italian: "Can you keep a confidence?"

Cornelis met his eyes. "I keep accounts," he said. "Accounts are by nature confidential."

Something in Adem's expression settled, the way a balance settles when the weights are correctly placed. He nodded once, stepped back, and gestured toward the interior of the house.

"Come then," he said. "We will eat first. The accounts have waited two seasons. They can wait another hour."

---

They ate in the courtyard beside the fountain, the mountain visible above the roofline in the long evening light, the water sound underneath the conversation. The meal was elaborate in a way that Cornelis had not encountered since Istanbul — course following course, each announced by a servant who seemed to take the production personally. Adem ate with the appetite of a man who had been hungry enough times in his life to take abundance seriously.

He asked Cornelis questions through the meal — not about the accounts, not about trade, but about the Netherlands. The sea. The VOC and its methods. The way European merchants organized their companies, their joint-stock arrangements, the separation of investors from operators. He asked with the focused curiosity of a man who had been thinking about these questions for some time and had not previously had a useful interlocutor.

Cornelis answered honestly, including the parts that were unflattering to the VOC, which were several. Adem listened to all of it and said nothing evaluative, only asked the next question.

Yusuf ate and said almost nothing and watched them both.

"Your company left you here," Adem said eventually, not unkindly.

"By accident," Cornelis said.

"Yes." Adem looked at the mountain. "And yet here you are in Kayseri, nine days from Konya, eating my food and about to look at my accounts." He refilled Cornelis's cup himself, which was a gesture of something. "Accident is an interesting word. I have been a merchant for thirty years and I am not sure I believe in it."

Cornelis thought about the *Altın Ay* and the hornpipe and the tide. "What do you believe in instead?"

Adem smiled — the first full smile of the evening. "Readiness," he said. "Some men are ready when the moment comes and some men are not. The moment does not choose. The man chooses, beforehand, by how he has lived." He set down his cup. "You were ready to be left behind. You did not know it. The ship knew."

Cornelis had no answer for this. He was not sure he agreed with it. He was not sure he disagreed.

After dinner Adem led him to a room off the courtyard — a study, the walls lined with the kind of organized disorder that meant a man spent real time here. On the central table were the accounts.

They were considerable. Four bound ledgers and a stack of loose correspondence, the whole thing covering perhaps a decade of trade. Cornelis sat down, looked at the topmost page, and felt the specific settling of attention that good work produces in a person suited to it — not excitement exactly, but a kind of alignment, the right instrument applied to the right problem.

He looked up. Adem was in the doorway.

"How long?" Adem asked.

Cornelis assessed the stack. "A week. Perhaps ten days, if there are things I need to ask you about."

Adem nodded. "You will stay here. Yusuf also." He paused. "Ask me whatever you need to ask. I have decided that I want to know."

He left.

Cornelis turned back to the ledgers. Opened the first one. Found the earliest date — twelve years ago, a caravan manifest from a road east of Kayseri, somewhere called Sivas — and began.

Outside the courtyard fountain maintained its steady account of the water moving through the city, and the mountain held its position above the roofline, and the evening deepened around the house of Adem Şahin while the Dutchman with the ledger did what he had always been made to do, in a place he had never imagined being, on a night that no one had planned.

### Chapter 9: What the Ledgers Said

The accounts told a story that Adem had not entirely meant to write.

This was not unusual — in Cornelis's experience, accounts always told a truer story than the man who kept them intended, because numbers did not have the social grace to omit the uncomfortable parts. What made Adem's ledgers unusual was their texture. Most merchants' accounts were dry things, functional, the minimum notation required to track what was owed and what was paid. Adem's were annotated. Margins full of small observations — a note about the weather on a particular crossing, a comment about a contact's health, once a brief digression about the quality of the bread at a *han* near Sivas that had no commercial relevance whatsoever and had been written apparently for the pleasure of recording it.

Cornelis recognized this immediately.

He turned to the back pages of his own ledger, where his Dutch observations lived alongside the Turkish words and the sketch of the *Koza Han.* Then back to Adem's margins. A man who wrote in the margins of his accounts was a man who understood that the accounts were not the whole truth — that the numbers were a skeleton and the life was somewhere in the connective tissue around them.

He worked through the first ledger in two days. It was the oldest, covering the years before Adem's trade had become what it now was, and it had the quality of a young man's record-keeping: energetic, slightly chaotic, the categories shifting as he figured out what mattered. A carpet trade out of Kayseri, modest. Then a connection east toward Sivas, then further — Erzincan, Erzurum, the roads toward the Persian border that opened and closed with the wars and the seasons. The carpets giving way to other things: raw silk from further east than the Bursa workshops, dyes, metalwork, once a consignment of manuscripts that was recorded with a discretion suggesting their provenance was not straightforward.

On the third evening Adem appeared in the doorway of the study with two cups of tea and sat across the table without asking permission, which was his house and his right.

"You have questions," he said.

"Several," Cornelis said. He turned the first ledger to a page he had marked with a strip of cloth. "This consignment. Year six of your records. Listed as *miscellaneous goods, eastern origin.* The value is recorded but not the buyer, which is the only instance in this ledger where the buyer is absent."

Adem looked at the page. Something moved briefly in his expression and was managed. "The buyer was a Venetian agent. He preferred not to be named."

"And the goods?"

"Manuscripts. As you have inferred." He picked up his tea. "Byzantine, mostly. From collections that had become — dispersed — in the years after the conquest. There was a period when such things moved in quantity. I was young and the margin was significant."

Cornelis wrote *Venetian agent, unnamed — manuscripts, Byzantine origin* in his notation column and moved to the next mark. "This is not a moral accounting," he said, without looking up. "I am not here to judge the provenance."

"I know," Adem said. "That is why I can answer."

They worked through the evening this way — Cornelis asking, Adem answering, the answers going into the notation column in the careful hand that Cornelis had been developing since he was twelve years old in a Leiden schoolroom. The fire in the brazier burned down and was replenished by the servant without either man noticing.

What emerged, slowly, was a portrait of a merchant who had been extraordinarily good at finding the moment before a trade route became known — arriving at the junction of supply and demand just before the junction became obvious to everyone else, making his margin in the gap between discovery and competition, and then moving on before the route became crowded. He had done this six or seven times in thirty years. Each time the margins had been significant. The accumulated result was the house, the fountain, the mountain view, and four ledgers of careful discomfort.

"You knew about the joint-stock companies," Cornelis said, near midnight.

"I read about them," Adem said. "The Portuguese model first, then what the Dutch have done. The idea that risk can be distributed—" he turned his cup in his hands— "it solved a problem I had been carrying alone."

"You needed partners."

"I needed partners who would not need to know everything." He looked at the ledgers. "The eastern routes beyond Erzurum — the Persian trade, the further connections — they require capital that one man cannot hold alone and discretion that most partners cannot maintain." He paused. "I made arrangements that were practical and that I am not comfortable with in retrospect."

"The obligations you mentioned."

"Yes."

Cornelis looked at the stack of remaining ledgers. "They are in there?"

"The later ones. Yes."

"Then I will find them."

Adem looked at him for a moment with the expression Cornelis was coming to know — not the merchant's assessment of the first evening, but something more considered, something that took its time. "How old are you?" he asked.

"Twenty-six," Cornelis said.

Adem shook his head slowly, not in negation but in something more like wonder at the arithmetic of things. "Twenty-six," he said. "In Kayseri. With a company ledger and a book you cannot read." He stood, picking up his cup. "Come tomorrow after the noon prayer. I want to show you something before you go further into the accounts."

---

What Adem wanted to show him was the carpet workshop.

It occupied a long building two streets from the house, backing onto the city wall — the sound of the looms audible from outside, a rhythmic wooden percussion that Cornelis had been hearing as background since his arrival without identifying its source. Inside: a dozen weavers working at vertical looms, mostly women, the carpets growing upward from the base in the slow accumulation of individual knots, each one placed by hand, the pattern emerging from the accumulated decisions of thousands of small acts.

Cornelis stood and watched.

He had seen carpets. Istanbul was full of them, Bursa had its workshops, the *bedesten* markets in both cities traded them seriously. He had learned to assess them in the way he had learned to assess silk — weight, density of knot, quality of dye. He had filed them under *known.*

This was different. This was carpets being *made.*

The weaver nearest him was a woman of perhaps forty who worked with a speed that made the individual movements invisible — her hands moving across the warp threads and back, the small knife for trimming the pile used with a reflexive precision that was almost casual, the pattern in her head or in her hands or somewhere between the two, producing itself without apparent consultation with the conscious mind.

Adem said, beside him: "The pattern she is working — it is three hundred years old. Her mother knew it. Her mother's mother." He watched her hands. "It exists nowhere written down. It lives in the hands."

"*Tecrübe,*" Cornelis said.

Adem looked at him. "Yes. Exactly."

They walked the length of the workshop. Adem named the patterns — their origins, their symbolic content, the tribes and regions they belonged to, the way a pattern from east of the Euphrates was different from one from the plateau not because anyone had decreed it but because the hands that made them had grown up seeing different things and the eyes had told the hands and the hands had remembered. He spoke about it with a precision and warmth that was different from his manner in the study — less guarded, more alive, the way a man speaks about the thing he actually loves as opposed to the thing that made him wealthy.

"This is what started it," Cornelis said. "The trade. The carpets."

"My father sold carpets at the Kayseri market. Small scale, local." Adem touched the edge of a loom with one finger, lightly. "I took the first one to Istanbul when I was nineteen. Sold it for three times what my father believed it was worth." He paused. "That margin — that gap between what something is worth where it is made and what it is worth where it is desired — it is the whole of trade, really. Everything else is detail."

Cornelis thought about the door, and what was behind it. Yusuf had said the same thing in different words on the road from Bursa.

"Why did you stop?" he asked. "The carpets."

"I did not stop," Adem said. "The workshop still works. But the margins—" he made a gesture that suggested compression, the narrowing of space— "when the Venetians learned the routes, when the Ottoman traders from Istanbul came in volume, the gap closed. You cannot live forever in the same gap." He looked at Cornelis. "So you find the next gap."

"Further east."

"Further east."

---

He finished the accounts on the ninth day.

The final summary occupied four pages in his own ledger — the structure of Adem's trade laid out clearly, the obligations named, the liabilities that had been successfully not-thought-about given their actual figures. He had been scrupulously neutral in the notation, as promised: this exists, this is the number, this connects to that. No recommendations. No judgment in the margins.

He presented it to Adem after the evening meal, in the study, with Yusuf present.

Adem read it slowly. All four pages, once, then the second page again, then the third. His expression throughout was the expression of a man receiving news he already knew but had needed to hear spoken aloud before he could decide what to do about it.

He set it down.

"It is as I thought," he said.

"Yes," Cornelis said.

"Worse in two places. Better in one."

"Yes."

Adem looked at the summary for another moment, then at Cornelis. "You have a gift," he said. "Not for numbers — numbers are just patience, any careful man can do numbers. You have a gift for making the shape of a thing visible without changing the thing." He tapped the pages. "These are my accounts. Nothing in them is different from what was there before you came. But now I can see them."

Cornelis said nothing.

"I need this," Adem said. "Ongoing. Not once — the trade does not stop, the accounts do not stop." He looked at Yusuf, briefly, then back. "I would like you to stay in Kayseri. Through the winter at minimum. In the spring, if you are willing, I have a journey east that requires exactly this."

The room was quiet except for the fountain in the courtyard.

Cornelis thought of the *Noord-Holland,* which had sailed without him in October. He thought of Pauw's letter, unresponded to now for six weeks. He thought of the letter he had written to his father in Leiden — or had not yet written, had been composing in his head since Karapınar and not yet set down.

He thought of the road east of Kayseri, which Adem knew and he did not yet, and what Yusuf had said about readiness, and what the weaver's hands knew that could not be written down.

"Through the winter," he said. "And we will speak about the spring."

Adem nodded once, satisfied. Yusuf picked up his tea. The fountain continued its account.

Outside, Mount Erciyes held the last of the evening light on its snow-cap — pale and patient above the dark city, above the volcanic plain, above the roads that ran in every direction from Kayseri toward everything the world contained.

Cornelis opened his ledger to the back pages and wrote, in Dutch:

*Staying. Don't know until when. The spring journey east — I have not said yes. I have not said no. I notice I am not afraid of either answer.*

He paused. Added:

*Write to Father.*
