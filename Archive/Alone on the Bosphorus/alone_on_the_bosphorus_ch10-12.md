# Alone on the Bosphorus

---

### Chapter 10: The Winter Accounts

The plateau winter arrived without ceremony.

One morning in late October the mountain was there as it always was, snow on the upper cone, the lower slopes brown with the end of the grazing season. The next morning everything below the snow line was also white, and the sky had become the low grey ceiling that it would maintain, with occasional exceptions, until March. The fountain in Adem's courtyard slowed and was covered by the servant with a wooden board and a layer of straw, and that was that. Winter.

Cornelis had experienced cold. The Netherlands in January was a serious matter — the canals freezing, the wind coming off the North Sea with nothing between it and you for a considerable distance. But Dutch cold was wet cold, cold that got into wool and stayed there. Plateau cold was different: dry and absolute, the air so clear on the days when the sky opened that the mountain appeared close enough to touch, the shadows on the snow a blue so precise it looked painted. It was cold that did not negotiate.

He acquired a second coat through Adem's intervention — a quilted thing, locally made, that was not elegant and was extremely effective — and learned to move through the city in the way that Kayseri people moved through it in winter: purposefully, without the wandering that warmer months permitted, the body knowing where it was going and getting there.

The city contracted. The plateau roads became difficult and then impassable, and the traffic that had moved through Kayseri in the trading season slowed to a trickle of local commerce, and the market stalls that had offered eastern goods retreated to the basics of a city feeding and warming itself. What remained was interior: the workshops, the houses, the *han* where men sat around fires and talked with the particular quality of attention that comes when there is nowhere else to be.

Cornelis had never had a winter like it. He had not known this was something a person could have.

---

Adem's teaching was nothing like Yusuf's.

Yusuf had taught by proximity — put the student near the thing and let observation do the work, correct errors without explaining the principle, trust the road to deliver what lecture could not. It was the teaching of a man who had learned the same way, who trusted the body's accumulation of *tecrübe* over the mind's organization of theory.

Adem taught with language. With dinner, and argument, and the kind of question that had no comfortable answer.

It began with the carpets. In the first weeks of winter, when the accounts were current and the spring journey was not yet close enough to plan, Adem took him back to the workshop and began — systematically, with the patience of a man who had been thinking about these things for thirty years and had rarely found an interlocutor worth the full explanation — to teach him to read what was woven into the patterns.

Not aesthetically. Adem had no interest in Cornelis developing an eye for beauty, which he seemed to regard as a byproduct rather than a purpose. What he wanted to teach was the pattern as information — what a particular motif indicated about the tribe that made it, the region, the period, the quality of the dye indicating the trade connections of the workshop, the density of the knot telling you not just the quality but the economic conditions of the labor that made it, whether the weavers had been working quickly under pressure or slowly with care.

"Every carpet is an account," Adem said one afternoon, unrolling a piece across the workshop floor — older than anything Cornelis had seen in a market, the colors faded to a beauty that was different from what they had been originally. "It records what was available, what was valued, who was connected to whom. You can read a century of trade history in this room if you know the language."

Cornelis knelt and looked at the pattern. He was learning, slowly, to see what Adem saw — the difference between a vegetable dye and a mineral one in the way color aged, the particular knot structure of the workshops east of Kayseri versus those on the plateau, the motifs that indicated origin the way a watermark indicated a paper mill. "This one," he said, touching a border motif. "I've seen this repeated. It's not from the plateau."

"Further east," Adem said. "Sivas, or beyond. The pattern is older than the piece — it's been copied from a carpet that was copied from another carpet. Three generations of weavers reproducing something none of them have seen the original of."

Cornelis sat back. "Like a manuscript tradition."

Adem looked at him with something that was not quite surprise but was adjacent to it. "Yes," he said. "Exactly like that."

These were the mornings. The afternoons were accounts — not just Adem's, as the winter progressed, but those of two other Kayseri merchants who had heard, through the small-city transmission of reputation, that Adem Şahin had a Dutch factor with an unusual ability to make the shape of things visible. Cornelis took the work because Adem suggested it and because the work was interesting and because the additional income had begun to seem, in some part of his mind that he was not yet fully acknowledging, like the income of a man who was staying rather than the income of a man who was waiting.

---

The evenings were Adem's study, and they were the education he had not expected.

Adem read. Not unusually for a merchant — literacy was a professional necessity — but with an appetite that went well past the professional. His shelves held Arabic texts and Persian texts and Ottoman commentaries and things in languages Cornelis couldn't identify, and he moved between them with the ease of a man who had been doing it for decades. His Arabic was classical and his Persian was fluent and his Ottoman Turkish sat atop both of them like a layer of sediment that had not entirely covered what was underneath.

He read Rumi. Of course he read Rumi — they were a day's ride from the tomb, which Cornelis had not forgotten — but not reverently, not with the careful distance of the religious. He argued with the text. He would read a passage and set the book down and tell Cornelis what he thought was right about it and what he thought was incomplete and what he thought the commentators had gotten wrong, with the comfortable combativeness of a man disagreeing with a friend.

"You have the book," Adem said one evening, meaning the copy Cornelis had bought outside the shrine in Konya, which lived in his coat pocket still.

Cornelis produced it.

Adem took it, looked at the cover, opened it to the first page. His expression did the thing it occasionally did — a warmth that crossed his face and was managed back to neutrality before it fully arrived. He handed it back. "I will teach you enough Arabic to read the opening," he said. "Just the opening. It will take the winter."

"I'm already learning Turkish," Cornelis said. "And I have the Arabic letters from Yusuf."

"Then you have the beginning," Adem said. "The letters are the door. What is written behind the door is the point."

Cornelis thought about Yusuf saying *you are what is behind the door.* He was beginning to think that everything in this part of the world organized itself around the image of the door and what was on either side of it.

---

His Turkish became, across those winter months, genuinely functional.

Not elegant — Mehmet, who appeared at Adem's house with irregular frequency since Yusuf had taken up semi-permanent residence there, continued to find his errors instructive. But functional in the way that mattered: he could negotiate, could follow a conversation between two native speakers if they were not being deliberately obscure, could make himself understood on topics more complex than the weather and the road.

What he noticed, as the language opened, was that the world around him became more densely populated. When his Turkish had been limited, the people he encountered had been, to some degree, types — the merchant, the weaver, the guard — because he could not hear enough of what they said to let them be individuals. Now he could hear them, and what he heard was the ordinary specificity of people: opinions about the quality of this season's supply of madder root, arguments about a boundary between two properties, a joke that required knowledge of a local figure he didn't have and had to ask Mehmet to explain, which Mehmet did with the patient condescension of a seventeen-year-old explaining the world to an adult.

He wrote to his father in December.

The letter took three attempts across as many evenings. The first attempt was factual and read like an account — a ledger of locations and transactions that said nothing true. The second attempt went too far in the other direction and became, halfway through, something he did not want to send to a schoolmaster in Leiden who had last seen his son boarding a ship with a clerk's trunk and a letter of employment from the VOC. He burned both.

The third letter was shorter than either. He described Kayseri, the mountain, the carpet workshop, the winter. He described Adem briefly — *a merchant of considerable experience and unexpected depth, who has provided both employment and instruction.* He said that he was well and that the return date was uncertain but that he was not in difficulty. He did not say that he had been thrown in a wrestling match in a village on the plateau and had not thought about Amsterdam for three days afterward. He did not say that he was learning to read Arabic with a sixty-year-old Persian carpet trader by lamplight while the plateau winter pressed against the shutters. He did not say that he was beginning to suspect that whatever he was becoming had no name in Dutch yet and possibly no name in any language he currently possessed.

He said: *I am learning a great deal. I hope this finds you well. I will write again in the spring.*

He sealed it and gave it to Adem's man to put with the next westbound courier, whenever the roads permitted.

Then he sat for a while in the lamp light, listening to the wind work on the shutters, and thought about what the letter had not said — all the *tecrübe* that had no container in the language of a Leiden schoolmaster's son, all the back pages of the ledger that were not for sending.

---

In February, Adem showed him the maps.

They came out of a chest in the study — hand-drawn, some of them, on good paper that had been rolled and stored carefully, others printed, one a Portuguese chart of the eastern trade routes that was old enough to predate certain currently inconvenient Ottoman-Persian border arrangements. Adem spread them across the table without preamble and stood back.

Cornelis looked.

The roads east of Kayseri were marked in varying quality of detail — the route to Sivas well-drawn, the road north toward Trabzon on the Black Sea coast clear enough, the passes through the mountains toward Erzurum sketched in a hand that suggested someone had described them from memory rather than walked them with a measuring cord. Beyond Erzurum the map became more approximate, more opinionated — the Persian border a line that was clearly more political than geographical, the roads beyond it trailing off into an optimism about the eastern trade that the map's maker had expressed in dotted lines.

"Tabriz," Adem said, pointing. "In a good year — when the Safavid-Ottoman situation is quiet, which it is not always — the road from Erzurum to Tabriz is open. Persian silk, direct. Not through the Ottoman middlemen in Istanbul, not through the Venetian agents. The primary source."

Cornelis looked at the dotted line. He thought about the gap between where a thing was made and where it was wanted, and the margin in the space between.

"This is the spring journey," he said.

"If the roads allow," Adem said. "If the politics allow. If we can put together the capital." He paused. "If I have a factor I trust with the accounts on a road where mistakes are expensive."

The lamp between them threw the map into relief — the mountains raised by shadow, the roads pale threads across the dark plateau. Outside, the February wind came off Erciyes with a cold that was theoretical in its purity.

Cornelis looked at the dotted line to Tabriz for a long time.

"The *Noord-Holland* sailed in October," he said. "The next reliable VOC passage from Istanbul will be—" he calculated— "spring. April at the earliest."

"Yes," said Adem, who had clearly already calculated this.

"The spring journey east would begin when?"

"March. Before the passes close again with the late snow."

Cornelis looked up from the map. "So I would miss it," he said. "The April passage."

"Yes," said Adem. "You would."

The wind. The lamp. The dotted line.

"Tell me about Tabriz," Cornelis said.

Adem sat down, folded his hands on the map, and began.

### Chapter 11: What Adem Knew About Tabriz

The city had been burning for two hundred years.

Not literally — not always — but Tabriz had the quality of a place that fire understood well, that had been taken and retaken and burned and rebuilt often enough that the architecture had developed a certain philosophical relationship with impermanence. The Mongols had sacked it. Tamerlane had sacked it. The Safavids had made it their capital and the Ottomans had taken it twice and given it back twice and the border between the two empires had moved back and forth across the surrounding plain like a man unable to find a comfortable position in bed.

"It is the most contested city in the world that is still worth contesting," Adem said. He had poured something — not tea this time, a darker liquid that arrived in small glasses and was not discussed — and settled into his chair with the manner of a man who had been waiting for the right evening to tell this particular story. "Which tells you something about what is there."

What was there: the Persian silk trade at its source, before the Ottoman intermediaries took their margin, before the Venetian agents added theirs, before the Istanbul market added the final layer of markup that made Bursa silk expensive and Persian silk — if you could get it directly — the kind of margin that Adem had spent thirty years looking for.

"How direct?" Cornelis asked.

"The workshops are in Gilan, on the Caspian coast — that is another journey entirely, one I have not made. But Tabriz is where the Gilan silk comes to be traded, and Tabriz is reachable." Adem turned his glass in his hands. "In a good year."

"How often is it a good year?"

"Since the last Ottoman-Safavid war ended — perhaps half the years. The Peace of Nasuh Pasha held longer than most expected. It may hold through this spring. It may not." He looked at Cornelis steadily. "This is the nature of that road. Everything east of Erzurum is conditional."

Cornelis processed this. "You have been before."

"Twice. The first time twenty years ago — that was when I understood what the margin could be. The second time eight years ago, when the road opened briefly between conflicts." Adem set down his glass. "Both times I came back with enough to fund the next several years of trade. Both times the road closed again within the season."

"And the accounts — the obligations you mentioned. The arrangements you were not comfortable with in retrospect."

Adem was quiet for a moment. "Financing the Tabriz journey requires capital that one man cannot hold alone. Twenty years ago I borrowed from sources whose terms seemed reasonable in the excitement of the margin." He looked at his hands. "The terms were not reasonable. I paid them — eventually, fully — but the payment required further arrangements that required further arrangements." He gestured at the ledgers on the shelf. "You have read what that produces."

"Yes," Cornelis said.

"This time I want to go differently." Adem looked at him directly. "Smaller. Better capitalized. With a factor who knows the accounts so well that nothing accumulates in the margins without being seen." He paused. "The risk is not primarily financial. It is the road, the politics, the conditional nature of everything east of Erzurum. I cannot eliminate that risk. I can manage the financial structure so that if something goes wrong on the road, the damage is bounded."

Cornelis thought about the dotted line on the map. He thought about what Yusuf had said about danger and care, which were not the same thing. He thought about the wrestling match in Karapınar, the two falls before the throw, the body learning from what the mind could not prepare it for.

"Tell me the road," he said.

---

Adem told it over three evenings.

The first evening: Kayseri to Sivas, six days on a road he knew well, the last substantial Ottoman city before the landscape became serious. Sivas was a junction — the road north to Trabzon and the Black Sea, the road east toward Erzincan and the mountains. It had a *han* Adem trusted, contacts who could be relied upon for information about road conditions further east, a market where final supplies could be acquired at reasonable prices.

The second evening: Sivas to Erzurum, eight days through country that was no longer the plateau. The mountains of eastern Anatolia were different from the Taurus visible from Kayseri — older, less organized, the valleys deep and the passes genuine obstacles rather than the gradual elevations of the western plateau. The Euphrates had its headwaters somewhere in this territory, which Cornelis found difficult to reconcile with the modest river he had encountered on maps labeled as the great boundary of the ancient world. Snow in the passes as late as April. Bandits, occasionally, though Adem said this with the same neutrality he applied to weather — a condition to be managed, not a drama.

"The guards from the Kayseri journey," Cornelis said.

"Not sufficient for that road," Adem said. "We would need to join a larger caravan out of Sivas. There is usually a significant movement east in March — merchants, pilgrims heading eventually toward Mecca via the southern route, soldiers rotating to the eastern garrisons. Safety in numbers is not a metaphor on the Erzurum road. It is the operating principle."

The third evening: Erzurum to Tabriz.

Adem was quieter about this section. Not evasive — Cornelis had learned to distinguish Adem's genuine reticences from his managed ones — but careful, in the way that a man is careful about a country he loves and has complicated feelings about.

"Erzurum is the last Ottoman city," he said. "It feels like a last city — it has that quality. Large garrison, serious walls, the sense of a place that knows it is a boundary and has organized itself around that knowledge." He paused. "East of Erzurum the road crosses into territory that is sometimes Ottoman and sometimes Safavid and sometimes neither, depending on the year and the mood of both empires. There are Kurdish tribal territories whose relationship with both empires is — pragmatic. There are Armenian villages that have survived every successive wave of authority by being useful to whoever is currently in charge."

"And the road itself?"

"Good, in places. Maintained when the Safavids hold it, because the Tabriz trade is important to them. Neglected when the Ottomans hold it, because the Ottomans are less interested in what the road leads to." He turned his glass. "Four days from Erzurum to the border, if border is the right word. Three more to Tabriz, if the roads are open. If they are not—" he made the gesture that Cornelis associated with the open-endedness of the eastern trade— "we wait, or we turn back."

"And in Tabriz itself?"

Adem's expression changed.

It was the change Cornelis had seen in the carpet workshop — the warmth that crossed his face when he spoke about something he genuinely loved. "Tabriz," he said, "is the most alive market I have encountered. More alive than Istanbul, which is larger but older in itself, settled into its own importance. Tabriz knows it might be taken again tomorrow and conducts its commerce accordingly — everything faster, more vivid, the merchants more direct because there is no tradition of permanence to be polite about." He shook his head slowly. "The silk market is in the *qaysariyya* — their word for the covered market. The silk from Gilan arrives in bales and is graded and traded in a single day sometimes, the whole year's production moving in a week's burst of commerce. The prices are—" he stopped. "You would need to see it."

Cornelis looked at the map on the table between them, the dotted line going east from Erzurum, the city of Tabriz marked with a confidence the mapmaker clearly felt and the road clearly didn't always justify.

"The people," Cornelis said. "In Tabriz. Language."

"Persian primarily. Azeri Turkish, which is different enough from Ottoman Turkish to be its own learning. Arabic for trade and religion. Armenian in the merchant quarters." Adem looked at him. "Your Ottoman Turkish will be understood but not native. You would need me to conduct the primary negotiations. Your role would be the accounts and the assessment — the same as here, but faster. In Tabriz you cannot take ten days to organize a decade of records. You have the duration of the market."

"Which is?"

"Two weeks. Perhaps three if we arrive at the right moment in the trading cycle." He paused. "Then the road back, before the politics change."

Cornelis was quiet for a moment, doing the arithmetic he always did — not just the financial arithmetic, though he was doing that too, the margins Adem had described against the costs of the journey, the capital required, the risk-adjusted return. He was doing a different arithmetic as well, harder to express in columns: the weight of what he would see against the weight of what he was moving away from, the April passage against the dotted line, the letter to his father that had not said what was true against the thing that was becoming true.

"The capital," he said. "How are you financing it differently this time?"

"I have enough of my own," Adem said. "For the smaller operation I described. No borrowed money, no arrangements with terms that will require further arrangements." He looked at Cornelis carefully. "What I do not have is a factor I can trust with the full picture. The Tabriz merchants — they are sophisticated, they are fast, they will find any weakness in the accounting structure and exploit it with complete professionalism and no malice. It is simply how the market works." He spread his hands. "I need someone who knows my accounts as well as I do, who can see a proposed transaction in the context of everything else, who will tell me when something is wrong before I have signed it."

"You have described the job I have been doing here all winter," Cornelis said.

"Yes," said Adem. "I have."

---

Yusuf was in the courtyard when Cornelis came out of the study on the third evening, sitting on the edge of the covered fountain in his winter coat, apparently engaged in the contemplation of nothing in particular.

Cornelis sat beside him.

They were quiet for a while. The mountain was invisible behind the cloud cover but present — you could feel the mass of it even when you couldn't see it.

"You knew," Cornelis said. "When we left Istanbul. Not the details, but the direction."

Yusuf was quiet long enough that Cornelis thought he might not answer. Then: "I knew Adem needed someone. I knew you needed a road." He paused. "I did not know you would be what he needed. I hoped."

"And if I hadn't been?"

"Then you would have organized his accounts for ten days and ridden back to Istanbul with fair wages and a good story." Yusuf almost laughed. "The road provides what it provides. You cannot plan the specifics."

Cornelis thought about this. "Tabriz," he said.

"Yes."

"You've been."

"Once. Twelve years ago." Yusuf looked at the courtyard wall, at something Cornelis couldn't see. "It is a remarkable place. And the road to it—" he paused— "the road to it is the road I have been meaning to take again. There is something further east that I have been meaning to return to."

Cornelis looked at him.

"Further east than Tabriz?" he said.

Yusuf said nothing. Which was, Cornelis had learned, not the same as no.

He sat in the courtyard while the February cold did what February cold did, and felt the spring journey taking shape around him the way the plateau had taken shape on the morning the forest ended — enormous, clear-edged, already decided, waiting only for him to ride into it.

He was not afraid.

He noted this, as he noted things, for the record.

### Chapter 12: The Last Morning, and the Road to Sivas

He found Adem in the carpet workshop at first light.

Not unusual — Adem was often there in the early hours, before the weavers arrived, in the particular quiet of a room that spent its days full of rhythmic industry. He was standing at the far end with a lamp, examining a carpet that had come off the loom the previous week, and he did not look up when Cornelis entered, which meant he had heard the footsteps and identified them.

Cornelis stood beside him and looked at the carpet.

It was a large piece — the biggest loom in the workshop, the one reserved for commissions of significance. The ground was a deep indigo that had taken three separate dyeing sessions to achieve, the pattern a medallion design from further east, one of the pieces in Adem's collection that the weavers had been reproducing from careful study across the winter months. The border carried a motif that Cornelis now recognized — had recognized since January, when Adem had shown him the original in the chest in the study — as coming from a workshop tradition east of Tabriz. Someone had made a carpet with this border two hundred years ago and the pattern had traveled west by being copied, generation to generation, the hands teaching the hands.

"It's good," Cornelis said.

"The indigo is slightly uneven in the third quadrant," Adem said. "You have to know to look."

Cornelis looked. Found it — a barely perceptible variation in the depth of color, a place where the dye had not fully penetrated. "I wouldn't have seen that in January," he said.

"No," Adem agreed. "You wouldn't have."

They stood with it for a moment. Outside, Kayseri was doing what Kayseri did at first light — the calls to prayer finished, the city finding its feet, the smell of bread already present in the cold air. The mountain would be clear today. It had been clear for three days, which Adem had said meant the passes east were open, which meant the road to Sivas was ready, which meant this was the last morning.

"The workshop," Cornelis said. "When you're east — who manages it?"

"My sister's husband. He knows the commercial side. The weavers know the rest." Adem set down the lamp on a workbench, looked at the carpet a final time. "The workshop has been here longer than I have. It will be here after."

Cornelis thought about the pattern traveling west from beyond Tabriz, copied by hands that had never seen the original. He thought about what survived and what didn't, and what the difference was between the two.

"Adem," he said.

"Yes."

"The role you have described for me — on the Tabriz journey, and after. It is not a factor's role. Not in the VOC sense."

"No," Adem said. "It is not."

"I am trying to understand what it is."

Adem was quiet for a moment, in the way he was quiet when the question deserved a considered answer rather than a ready one. "In the Persian tradition," he said finally, "there is a word — *dalāl.* A broker. But not simply a man who stands between buyer and seller for a fee. A *dalāl* is a man who knows the value of a thing independently of what either party believes it to be worth. He carries the true price in his head." He looked at Cornelis. "You are not there yet. But you are learning to carry it."

Cornelis said nothing.

"The Tabriz journey will teach you the other end," Adem said. "You already know what silk is worth in Bursa, in Istanbul, in the European market. After Tabriz you will know what it is worth at the source. Very few men hold both numbers simultaneously." He picked up the lamp. "What a man does with that knowledge is his own business."

He walked toward the workshop door, paused. "The horses are ready at the second hour. Eat something first. The road to Sivas does not improve on an empty stomach."

He left.

Cornelis stood alone in the workshop for a moment, the indigo carpet before him in the grey morning light, the slight imperfection in the third quadrant invisible now without the lamp. He thought about *dalāl* — the word, its weight, the shape of what it named. He turned it in his mind the way he turned new Turkish words, testing its edges.

He thought it might be the first word in any language he had encountered that described what he was becoming.

He went to eat something.

---

The departure had the quality of all departures from places that have held you — slightly unreal, the familiar rendered strange by the knowledge that you are about to stop seeing it. The courtyard fountain, uncovered now that the frosts had passed, resuming its account. The study window, the lamp Adem worked by not yet lit at this hour. The smell of the city's bread and basalt and the cold coming off the mountain.

Mehmet had the horses ready in the street. He had grown across the winter — not just in height but in the way a young man grows when the people around him are worth watching: a new attentiveness in his eyes, a quality of assessment borrowed from Yusuf and not yet fully his own but clearly on its way to being so. He handed Cornelis *Amsterdam*'s reins without comment.

Yusuf was already mounted, looking east.

Adem stood in the doorway of the house. He had said what he had to say in the workshop. He raised one hand now — not a wave exactly, something more economical — and Cornelis raised his in return, and that was sufficient.

They rode out through the basalt streets as the sun cleared the mountain's shoulder and threw long shadows west behind them.

---

The plateau in March was a different country from the plateau in April.

The grass was not yet green — it was the color of old straw, flattened by winter, pressed close to the earth as if still not entirely convinced that the cold was finished. The road was soft in places where the snowmelt had not yet drained, *Amsterdam* picking his way with a care he hadn't needed in the drier months. The sky was the high pale blue of early spring, the light strong but not yet warm, the shadows long until midday and long again by afternoon.

There were more travellers than Cornelis expected.

He had imagined the spring opening of the eastern roads as a gradual thing — a trickle becoming a flow. What he found instead was that everyone who had been waiting out the winter had apparently decided simultaneously that March was sufficient. The road to Sivas was populated: merchants, pilgrims, soldiers rotating to eastern postings, a group of dervishes travelling in a silence so complete it seemed to alter the air around them, two Armenian traders from Kayseri who recognized Adem and fell into conversation beside him for the better part of a morning.

Cornelis rode and listened and caught what he could.

His Turkish was good enough now that travel had become aurally rich in a way the early months had not been. He heard a merchant complaining to his companion about prices in the Kayseri spring market — the specific numbers, the comparison to last year, a theory about why the Venetian buyers had been less present than expected. He heard a soldier explaining something about the garrison at Erzurum to a younger man who was going there for the first time. He heard the dervishes not speaking and understood that this too was a kind of communication.

He rode alongside Yusuf when the road allowed it and they talked — more than they had talked since the early days out of Istanbul, the winter in Kayseri having created a new ease between them. Yusuf asked about the winter's work, the accounts, what Adem had taught. Cornelis answered honestly, including the parts he was still working out.

"*Dalāl,*" he said, on the second afternoon. "Adem used this word."

Yusuf rode for a moment. "Yes," he said. "That is what he is making you."

"Is it what you are?"

Yusuf considered. "I am a merchant. I buy and sell. A *dalāl* does not buy and sell — he knows the price and advises. It is a different relationship to the goods." He glanced at Cornelis. "A *dalāl* needs to trust no one's valuation but his own. This requires knowing more than either party in a transaction, which requires—"

"Having been to both ends," Cornelis said.

"Yes."

The road curved through a shallow valley where a stream ran fast with snowmelt, the sound of it loud enough to require a pause in conversation. When they came out the other side Yusuf said: "Adem was a *dalāl* once. Before the capital accumulated and the arrangements became necessary. He misses it, I think."

"He's making me what he was," Cornelis said.

"He is giving you what he had before he had too much," Yusuf said. "There is a difference."

Cornelis thought about the obligations in the ledgers, the arrangements that had required further arrangements, the discomfort Adem had named clearly and carried quietly. The workshop that had been there before him and would be there after. The carpet traveling west by being copied, the pattern surviving because the hands kept teaching the hands.

What survived was the knowledge. Not the capital, which accumulated and created its own problems. The knowledge of value, carried independently.

He wrote it in the back of the ledger that evening, in Dutch, which he was still saving for the things worth keeping:

*A dalāl. A man who carries the true price. Adem was one before he became something larger and more complicated. He is teaching me to be what he was before the complications. I am not sure he knows he is doing this. I think perhaps he does.*

---

On the fourth day the terrain changed.

The plateau's long swells gave way to something more broken — the land lifting and folding as the road climbed toward the watershed between the Kızılırmak basin and the upper Euphrates country. The horizons that had been the plateau's constant generosity contracted, the road threading through valleys that required attention in a way the open ground had not. The snow on the higher ground was still substantial, the meltwater running in channels beside the road that hadn't been channels in October.

Sivas appeared on the sixth day in the late afternoon, in a valley broad enough to have recalled the plateau's scale — a substantial city, the old Seljuk architecture visible in the tile work of the medrese facades, the minarets different in proportion from the Ottoman style Cornelis had become accustomed to. Older. The Seljuks had built here three hundred years before the Ottomans consolidated the region, and the stonework remembered.

Yusuf's contact in Sivas was a man named Kemal — a lean, sardonic figure of perhaps fifty who ran a *han* and appeared to regard the world with the amusement of someone who had seen enough of it to find its repetitions entertaining. He embraced Yusuf with the warmth of long friendship and assessed Cornelis in approximately four seconds with the accuracy, Cornelis suspected, of a man who had been making such assessments for decades.

"The Dutchman," Kemal said, in Turkish.

"The factor," Yusuf said.

"Adem's work?"

"All winter."

Kemal looked at Cornelis with a slight upward revision of whatever he had initially concluded. "The road east," he said. "You know what you're going into?"

"I'm learning," Cornelis said, in Turkish.

Kemal made a sound that was somewhere between Yusuf's almost-laugh and something more pointed. "Good answer," he said. He switched to the trading Italian of the caravan routes, slower and more deliberate than Yusuf's, shaped by different influences. "Honest men say they are learning. Fools say they know. The road east has no patience for fools."

"What does it have patience for?" Cornelis asked.

Kemal thought about it. "Men who pay attention," he said. "And men who can tell the difference between a problem and a condition."

"What's the difference?"

"A problem can be solved." Kemal turned toward the *han* interior. "Come. Eat. Tomorrow we talk about the road to Erzurum and what the conditions are this season."

Cornelis followed him in, and behind him the door of the *han* swung on its hinge in the Sivas wind, and the road east waited outside it with its conditions and its distances, patient as the plateau, indifferent to whether he was ready or not.

He was, he thought, nearly ready.
