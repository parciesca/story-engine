# Alone on the Bosphorus

---

### Prologue: The Hornpipe and the Tide

The tavern sat where it had always sat, wedged between a rope merchant's warehouse and the smell of low tide, its sign — a painted crescent that had once been a moon — swinging in the evening wind off the Bosphorus. Inside, the tallow candles had burned low enough that the faces around the tables were more shadow than man.

He was the one with the ledger.

Not open — it would have been ruined by the wine — but there beside his cup, a habitual gravity, the way a priest keeps a hand near his breviary even when he is not praying. The other men from the *Zeeland Ster* had been sailors long enough to leave their work on the ship. He had not. Junior factors carry their counting in their heads even when their heads are unwise.

The conversation around him was the loose talk of men whose contracts were nearly done — Batavia, the return leg, what they'd spend it on. Someone was playing a hornpipe badly in the corner. A Levantine trader at the bar spoke in rapid Turkish to the proprietor, gesturing at something outside, and both men laughed.

He understood none of it. He did not find this remarkable. He understood Dutch, Latin, some French, the rudiments of Portuguese. He had assumed — the way educated young men from good families assume things — that this would be sufficient.

A cup was placed in front of him by a girl who did not look at him. He drank. Someone refilled it. He drank again.

Later — how much later was a question that would matter, then not matter, then matter enormously — he put his head down on the table beside the ledger. Just for a moment. The hornpipe played on.

Outside, the *Zeeland Ster* rode the tide at anchor, her departure logged for first light.

### Chapter 1: What the Straits Keep

The light came first as a bruise along the Asian shore — purple going amber, the minarets of Üsküdar emerging from darkness like fingers reaching from the water. It was, by any honest accounting, a beautiful morning. He was in no condition to appreciate it.

His name was Cornelis Bos. He had discovered this again some minutes ago, patting his coat for the ledger and finding it still there, his name inked on the inside cover in his own careful hand — *C. Bos, Junior Factor, V.O.C.* — as if he had anticipated needing the reminder. The coat was damp. The cobblestones beneath him were damp. The dog regarding him from a careful distance was, at least, dry.

He stood. This took longer than it should have.

The street that had seemed, in darkness, to be near the waterfront was in fact several streets removed from it, which meant he had walked in the wrong direction at some point in the night, which meant — he turned, got his bearings from the minarets, turned again — the quay was that way. He walked.

Istanbul in the early morning was not yet fully itself. The great covered markets were shut. The water-sellers had not yet taken their positions. But the waterfront never entirely slept — there were always men moving cargo in the grey hours, always small boats crossing to the Asian side, always the smell of fish and tar and the particular mineral cold that came off the Bosphorus regardless of season. Cornelis followed his nose and the sound of water and came out onto the quay at the precise moment the sun cleared the hills of Anatolia and threw its light across the straits.

He stood there and looked at the water for a long time.

There was no ship.

He looked left, where the Bosphorus opened toward the Golden Horn and the crowded harbor beyond. He looked right, where the straits narrowed and curved toward the Black Sea. He looked straight across at the Asian shore, in case he had somehow confused his directions entirely and the *Zeeland Ster* was anchored there for reasons he could not fathom.

There was no ship.

A fisherman was mending a net twenty feet away, paying him no attention. Cornelis walked to him and said, in Dutch, "The ship. The Dutch ship. Was it here?" The fisherman did not look up. Cornelis said it in French, then in the approximate Portuguese he'd learned in Lisbon two years ago. The fisherman continued mending his net with the patience of a man who had seen Europeans make noise on this quay before and expected to see it again.

Cornelis sat down on a bollard.

The *Zeeland Ster* had been bound for Batavia via Alexandria, and then home. The return cargo was already negotiated — he had done some of the negotiating himself, his first real responsibility, and he had been proud of it in the quiet way he allowed himself small prides. His passage home was on that ship. His employment was on that ship. His trunk, containing his spare coat, his books, his letters from his mother, and every guilder he had saved across fourteen months of the eastern trade, was on that ship.

What he had: one ledger. One coat. A small knife he kept from habit. Whatever remained in his purse, which he now counted — eleven silver coins of uncertain denomination, two of which turned out, on closer inspection, to be buttons.

Nine coins, then. And a ledger. And the morning.

He watched a caïque — one of the long, elegant rowboats the Ottomans used to cross the straits — glide past with four passengers seated in silence, the oarsman standing at the stern and working his single oar with an ease that seemed almost contemptuous of effort. The boat was painted red and green, the wood worn smooth. It disappeared around a point of land and was gone.

Cornelis opened the ledger to the first blank page and, from force of habit more than any useful purpose, wrote the date. Then he wrote: *Ship departed. Circumstances unclear.* He stared at this. He added: *Position: the quay below Galata. Assets: as above. Liabilities: considerable.*

He closed the ledger.

Around him the city was waking up properly now — the calls to prayer had come and gone while he slept on the cobblestones, but the streets were filling, the noise building, the particular layered chaos of a city that had been the center of the world for two hundred years and had not forgotten it. A spice merchant was opening his stall twenty yards away, arranging small mountains of color — ochre, crimson, a yellow so bright it almost hurt. Two women passed in conversation, their voices quick and low. A boy led a donkey loaded with copper pots that rang softly against each other with each step.

None of it was for him. He was scenery here. He was a pale, somewhat crumpled young Dutchman sitting on a bollard with a ledger, and the city moved around him like water around a rock, indifferent, unhurried, already full of its own business.

He had two options, as best he could determine. He could make his way to the Dutch trading house in Galata and throw himself on their mercy — they would have heard of the *Zeeland Ster*, might even know what had happened, might arrange passage home eventually. This was the sensible option. This was what he would do.

He did not move.

The spice merchant caught him staring and stared back, not unfriendly, assessing. The man said something in Turkish, then in what Cornelis slowly recognized as a version of Italian, simplified, the trading-port Italian that was nobody's native tongue and everybody's second language along this coast.

"You are lost," the man said. It was not quite a question.

Cornelis considered the various ways this was true.

"I have missed my ship," he said, in the same simplified Italian.

The merchant nodded slowly, as if this were a reasonable thing to have done. "Where was it going?"

"Home," said Cornelis.

The merchant made a small sound — not quite sympathy, not quite amusement — and went back to arranging his spices. But he did not close the conversation. He left it open, the way a door is left open on a warm day, without obligation, just the possibility of air.

Cornelis sat on the bollard a little longer. The sun was fully up now. The Bosphorus glittered. Somewhere in the city, someone was baking bread, and the smell of it reached him on the wind and made his stomach remind him forcefully of its existence.

He stood. He picked up his ledger. He looked once more at the empty water where his ship had been.

Then he walked toward the spice merchant.

### Chapter 2: The Consul's Ledger and the Road East

The Dutch trading house in Galata smelled like home — pine resin, old wool, the faint ghost of Gouda — and Cornelis stood in the doorway for a moment before entering, breathing it in like a man surfacing from water.

The factor who received him was named Hendrick Pauw, a heavyset man in his fifties who had been in Istanbul long enough that his Dutch had taken on a faint Ottoman lilt without him noticing. He listened to Cornelis's account with the expression of a man who has heard variations of this story before — not unsympathetic, but not surprised either. Ships left men behind. The Bosphorus had a long memory for such things.

"The *Zeeland Ster* made the morning tide," Pauw confirmed, not unkindly. "The harbourmaster's log has her cleared before the sixth hour. I'm told there was some confusion about your whereabouts." He paused. "You were at the *Altın Ay*?"

"I was," Cornelis admitted.

Pauw nodded. Said nothing further on the subject.

The news was not good, though it was delivered with the smooth efficiency of a man accustomed to managing disappointment on behalf of the company. The next VOC vessel expected in Istanbul was the *Noord-Holland*, but she was running the Alexandria route first and would not make the Bosphorus before October at the earliest — possibly November, if the winds off Cyprus ran contrary. It was currently late April.

Six months. Perhaps seven.

"We have contacts," Pauw said, producing a small ledger of his own — smaller than Cornelis's, and considerably more worn. "Local merchants who occasionally require a literate hand. Translation work, correspondence in Latin or French. The French ambassador's secretary sometimes has overflow. The pay is—" he found the page, ran a thumb down a column of figures— "adequate. For modest lodgings and one meal a day."

Cornelis looked at the numbers. His face did something careful and controlled.

Pauw read it accurately. "Istanbul is not a cheap city," he said. "But you will survive until November. We've seen men through worse."

That evening Cornelis sat in the small room Pauw had arranged for him — a clean room, a narrow bed, a window that looked onto a courtyard where a fig tree grew — and opened his ledger to the page he had started on the quay. He added two columns: income projected, expenses projected. He ran the sums twice. The arithmetic was unambiguous. He would arrive in Amsterdam, assuming no unexpected costs, with less than he had left with fourteen months ago. Fourteen months of the eastern trade, and he would return poorer in money and possessed of nothing more than a story about sleeping on cobblestones.

He closed the ledger. He looked at the fig tree.

---

He lasted eleven days on Pauw's list of contacts before the caravan merchant found him.

Or rather — before he found the caravan merchant, which was also, in a sense, the city finding him, which was perhaps how it had always been going to work.

The work Pauw had arranged was real and he did it conscientiously — copying correspondence for a Flemish cloth dealer, translating a legal document from Latin into passable Italian for a Greek Orthodox bishop who seemed faintly embarrassed to need the help. But the money was precisely as meager as Pauw had suggested, and Cornelis, who had been trained to notice discrepancies between what a thing was worth and what it was being sold for, could not stop noticing them.

The discrepancy that caught him was in the *bedesten* — the inner market of the grand bazaar, where the serious trading happened, locked behind iron gates each night. He had gone in looking for nothing in particular, which is the only safe way to enter a bazaar, and had found himself watching a transaction that seemed, by his accounting, to make no sense.

A merchant — Anatolian, from the look of him, travelling clothes still dusty from the road — was selling a consignment of beeswax to a Venetian buyer who was not paying nearly enough attention to what he was buying. The price being agreed was the Istanbul price. The wax had come from somewhere east, somewhere the price was considerably lower, and Cornelis did not need to know where exactly to understand that the margin between those two numbers was significant.

He stood there for a moment doing sums in his head.

Then he did what he had been trained to do: he made himself useful.

His Italian was better than the Anatolian merchant's — a low bar, as it turned out — and when the negotiation stumbled on a question of weights and the two men reached for their respective versions of the same gesture of exasperated incomprehension, Cornelis stepped in and said, in his careful trading Italian, "Perhaps I can assist."

The Venetian buyer was annoyed. The Anatolian merchant — whose name was Yusuf, and who had been on the road for nineteen days and was tired in the way that only long travel produces — looked at this pale young foreigner with an expression that was not quite hope but was adjacent to it.

They sorted out the weights. The transaction concluded. The Venetian departed.

Yusuf looked at Cornelis for a long moment, then said something in Turkish. Cornelis spread his hands apologetically. Yusuf switched to his Italian, which was indeed limited but serviceable for the present purpose.

"You know numbers," he said.

"I am a factor," Cornelis said. "Or I was."

Yusuf absorbed this. "I go back east in three days," he said. "To Bursa first, then into the interior. I have goods to sell and goods to buy and—" he searched for the word— "accounts that become confused." He looked at Cornelis with the particular attention of a practical man assessing a practical problem. "You need money. Yes?"

Cornelis thought of the arithmetic in his ledger. "Yes," he said.

"I pay fairly," said Yusuf. "I have a reputation for this."

"How long?" Cornelis asked.

Yusuf considered. "Bursa is ten days. After Bursa—" he made a gesture that suggested the interior of Anatolia, its distances, its open-endedness— "it depends what we find. What the roads are. What the season does." He looked at Cornelis. "You have somewhere you need to be?"

Cornelis thought of the *Noord-Holland*, arriving in November, maybe, if the winds off Cyprus cooperated.

"Not until October," he said. "At the earliest."

Yusuf nodded, as if this settled something. He named a figure — weekly, in silver — and Cornelis, who had been trained never to accept the first number, almost forgot to negotiate. He caught himself, made a mild counter, and Yusuf accepted it so quickly that Cornelis suspected he had not gone nearly high enough.

They shook hands in the Ottoman fashion, which Cornelis got approximately right.

---

He told Pauw the following morning. Pauw received the news with the mild disapproval of a man whose carefully arranged solution was being discarded, but he was too experienced to argue with the arithmetic.

"Yusuf ibn Malik," he said, when Cornelis gave the name. "Yes, I know him. Reliable. Moves between here and Konya, sometimes further east." He studied Cornelis with the look of a man recalibrating. "You'll need a horse."

"Can I—"

"Against your wages," Pauw said, before the question was finished. "We'll sort it. And Bos—" Cornelis was already turning to go— "be back before the *Noord-Holland*. Don't get distracted."

Cornelis said he wouldn't.

He was, even then, looking at the map on Pauw's wall — the interior spreading out east of the Bosphorus, city names he half-recognized from classical history, trade routes marked in faded ink, mountain ranges that looked, on paper, like the wrinkles in an old man's hand. Bursa, which had been the Ottoman capital before Constantinople fell. Konya, where the whirling dervishes kept their house. Beyond that, the map grew quieter and more approximate.

He picked up his ledger from under his arm. Opened it to the arithmetic page. Drew a single line through the column of small Istanbul wages and replaced it with the new figure.

The numbers, for the first time, looked like something.

Three days later, in the early morning, the city still blue-grey and cool, he rode out through the Anatolian side on a compact bay horse he had named — privately, and with some embarrassment at the sentimentality — *Amsterdam*, and followed Yusuf ibn Malik east.

### Chapter 3: The Road to Bursa

The ferry crossing took less than an hour.

Cornelis stood at the bow of the flat-bottomed *vapur* with his horse — *Amsterdam*, who was indifferent to water in a way that suggested good sense — and watched Istanbul recede across the Bosphorus. The city from the water was something else entirely: the domes of Hagia Sophia and the Sultan Ahmet mosque rising in a kind of geological patience above the skyline, the minarets thin and exact as compass needles, the whole of it reflected in the straits with a wobbling, impermanent beauty that he understood he would not see again for some time. He felt something that was not quite homesickness — he had not been home in fourteen months, and home was in the other direction — but was perhaps its cousin. The loss of something known.

Then the Asian shore came up beneath the hull and there was the business of unloading, and the city was behind him.

---

They were seven in the caravan: Yusuf and his nephew Mehmet, who was sixteen and handled the pack animals with a competence that made Cornelis quietly ashamed of his own relationship with *Amsterdam*; two hired guards whose names Cornelis could not yet pronounce reliably and who regarded him with a professional neutrality that was neither welcoming nor hostile; a cloth merchant named Ibrahim who was travelling as far as İznik and seemed to find Cornelis mildly amusing; and a woman of indeterminate age who Ibrahim referred to only as *the widow* and who travelled in a covered cart and spoke to no one.

Yusuf rode at the front and set the pace, which was unhurried. This surprised Cornelis, who had imagined — he saw now that he had imagined the whole enterprise from the outside, as a series of transactions rather than an experience — that a merchant would be anxious about time. But Yusuf seemed to understand something about roads that Cornelis did not yet, which was that the road rewarded patience and punished urgency, that a horse pushed too hard arrived lame, that a man who did not sleep enough made bad decisions in the market.

They rose before light and rode through the cool of the morning. By midday they stopped at whatever shade presented itself — a stand of plane trees, a ruined caravanserai, once simply the shadow side of a long hill — and waited out the worst of the sun. In the late afternoon they rode again, the light going amber and long across the countryside, and stopped for the night at villages when they could, in the open when they couldn't.

The landscape east of Üsküdar surprised him.

He had expected — again, he had expected the thing before the thing, the idea of Asia Minor rather than Asia Minor itself — something barren, or strange, or dramatically foreign. What he found instead in those first days was a countryside that looked, in its rhythms if not its particulars, oddly like the inland parts of any civilized land: cultivated fields coming green in the April warmth, olive trees on south-facing slopes, villages of whitewashed stone where dogs came out to regard the caravan with suspicion and children came out to regard it with uncomplicated delight. Women carried water jars on their heads. Men guided wooden plows behind patient oxen. The smell was thyme and horse dung and somewhere ahead, always, the faint cool promise of water.

What was different was harder to name. A quality of light, perhaps — sharper than Dutch light, which was always softer, filtered through clouds and the particular grey generosity of northern skies. Here the shadows were definite. Things stood out from their backgrounds with an almost aggressive clarity. The white of a minaret against a blue sky was not the white of anything he had seen before.

And the sounds. The call to prayer reaching them across a valley just before dusk, the muezzin's voice wavering and then steadying into something that was not music exactly but was not not music either, and the caravan stopping — not formally, just slowing, the men who prayed moving to the side of the road to do so — and Cornelis sitting on *Amsterdam* and not quite knowing where to put himself.

He wrote in his ledger in the evenings. He had begun, without deciding to, to use the blank pages at the back for something other than accounts. Small observations. The Turkish word Mehmet had taught him that day. The name of a plant Ibrahim had pointed out. The way the road curved south through a valley of fig trees and then climbed sharply and suddenly presented, over a ridge, a view of Bursa's distant plain that made him stop his horse without meaning to.

He was recording something. He was not sure yet what.

---

Yusuf began talking to him on the third day.

Before that there had been functional communication — the accounts Cornelis was supposed to maintain, questions about the pack animals' loads, decisions about the route — but on the third evening, at a *han* outside a village whose name Cornelis could not catch, Yusuf sat down across the fire and asked him, in their shared Italian, what he had been doing in Istanbul.

Cornelis explained. The VOC. The trading voyage. The *Altın Ay* and its consequences.

Yusuf listened without interrupting, which was a quality Cornelis had already noticed in him, and then said: "And before the ship?"

"I was a clerk in Amsterdam. For a merchant house. Wool, mostly."

"Your father?"

"A schoolmaster. In Leiden."

Yusuf considered this. "And you chose trade."

"I was good at numbers," Cornelis said. "And the VOC offered—" he gestured vaguely at the night sky, at the road, at Anatolia in general— "this."

Yusuf made the sound that Cornelis was beginning to recognize as his version of a laugh — not a laugh exactly, more a brief exhalation of acknowledgment. "Yes," he said. "It does offer this."

Silence. The fire. Somewhere in the darkness a nightingale was producing an amount of sound disproportionate to its size.

"You keep notes," Yusuf said. He had seen Cornelis writing.

"A habit."

"What do you write?"

Cornelis thought about how to explain this in Italian. "What I see," he said. "What I don't understand yet."

Yusuf looked at him steadily. In the firelight his face was all shadow and plane, the face of a man who had spent decades reading road and weather and the intentions of other merchants. "That is a good habit," he said. "Most men write only what they already understand."

He stood, picked up his cup, and walked to the other side of the fire to talk to one of the guards, and the conversation was over, though Cornelis continued to think about it for some time.

---

The trade itself he learned in pieces.

Yusuf dealt primarily in what he called *kuru mal* — dry goods, the unglamorous backbone of the caravan trade: beeswax, dried figs, leather, raw wool, blocks of alum from the mines near Bursa that the European cloth merchants needed for their dyes. He had contacts at each stop, men who had known his father and now knew him, relationships maintained across years by the consistent honoring of agreed prices and the occasional small gift brought from the road.

"The Venetians come to Istanbul," he explained one afternoon, riding side by side on a stretch of road wide enough for it, "and they think they are buying from Istanbul. But Istanbul does not make the wax. Istanbul does not grow the figs. Istanbul is just the door." He gestured back, roughly west. "We are what is behind the door."

Cornelis thought about this in accountant's terms: the difference between the price at origin and the price at the door. He had spent his career so far on the Istanbul side of that equation, which meant he had understood only the door.

"How far back does it go?" he asked. "The supply."

Yusuf made his almost-laugh. "How far east do you want to go?"

It was not quite an invitation. Cornelis filed it under *things not yet understood* and wrote it in the back of the ledger that evening.

---

On the eighth day they came over a ridge in the late afternoon and Bursa was below them.

The city lay against the long flank of Uludağ — the Great Mountain, Ibrahim told him, which the ancient Greeks had called Olympus of Bithynia — in a way that suggested it had grown there rather than been built, the minaret clusters rising from the green of fruit orchards and the silver-grey of olive groves, the old city walls still visible, the silk market's *bedesten* somewhere in the center of it all sending up a thin haze of commerce even at this distance. Beyond the city the plain stretched south and west, flatlands going hazy in the late light.

Mehmet said something in Turkish, and even without the words Cornelis understood it as the thing a young man says when he sees home from the road. Yusuf smiled — a real smile, the one he reserved for things that were not transactions.

Cornelis opened his ledger. Looked at the figure at the top of his account column. Looked at Bursa.

He wrote: *Day 8. City of silk and old empire. The road here felt longer than the numbers say.*

He closed the ledger and rode down the hill toward the city.
