# Alone on the Bosphorus

---

### Chapter 4: The Language of Silk

Yusuf did not take him to the silk market immediately.

This was, Cornelis would understand later, deliberate — the same patience that governed the road governing everything else. First there was the *han* where Yusuf always stayed in Bursa, a substantial caravanserai in the old quarter whose proprietor embraced Yusuf with the warmth of a man greeting a brother and regarded Cornelis with the polite blankness of a man who had learned not to be surprised by whatever the road brought in. First there was the unloading, the animals watered and fed, the goods inventoried and locked — this part Cornelis did, working from the manifest he had maintained since Istanbul, and Yusuf checked his figures once, found them correct, and did not check them again. First there was a meal that appeared without being ordered, lamb and flatbread and a yogurt dish that Cornelis ate carefully and then, when no one was watching, ate more of.

Only then, the following morning, did Yusuf say: "Come."

---

The *Koza Han* — the cocoon market — sat at the center of Bursa like a heart.

It was a double-storied Ottoman structure built around a courtyard, a small mosque at its center, the arched porticos running around the inner and outer galleries lined with the workshops and offices of the silk merchants. Cornelis had seen grand buildings before — in Amsterdam, in Lisbon, once briefly in Venice — but there was something about the *Koza Han* that was different from European commercial architecture. European buildings announced themselves, declared their purpose through façade and ornament. This one turned inward. The exterior was plain, almost austere. You passed through the gate and the courtyard opened around you, and the activity — the merchants, the samples, the weighing, the negotiation conducted in low voices across small tables — had a quality of the interior, of something protected and self-contained, like a conversation you were privileged to overhear.

Yusuf walked through it with the ease of a man who had walked through it a thousand times. He greeted people as he went — a touch of the hand to the chest, a word, sometimes a longer exchange that he did not translate and Cornelis did not ask him to. He was collecting something as he moved, Cornelis realized. Information. The temperature of the market. Who was buying, who was selling, what had come in from the villages, what the Venetian agents were paying this season.

"Watch," Yusuf said quietly, stopping at the edge of a negotiation between two merchants whose goods Cornelis could not immediately identify. "Don't listen. Watch."

Cornelis watched.

The two men sat across a low table. Between them was a small sample of silk — raw, still in the gum, a skein of it that caught the light from the courtyard in a way that the word *yellow* did not adequately describe. One man held it. The other man did not reach for it, which seemed to Cornelis a choice. The man holding the silk said something. The other man looked at the courtyard archway above his companion's head, as if mildly interested in the stonework.

"What do you see?" Yusuf asked.

"The seller is eager," Cornelis said quietly. "He's holding the goods. The buyer is pretending not to want them."

"Yes. What else?"

Cornelis looked more carefully. The buyer's hands were in his lap, relaxed. The seller's were not. "The seller needs to move the goods today. Something about his position requires it — cash, or a debt, or he's leaving tomorrow."

Yusuf almost laughed. "He is leaving tomorrow. He comes from Bilecik, three days east, and he has a full consignment outside. He cannot take it back." He paused. "The buyer knows this."

"How does the buyer know?"

"Because the buyer was at the *han* last night when the Bilecik caravan came in." Yusuf glanced at Cornelis. "The market is not only in this courtyard. The market begins the night before."

They moved on. Yusuf stopped at a stall where a merchant greeted him with particular warmth — this was Selim, an older man whose family had been in the silk trade for three generations and who spoke, to Cornelis's surprise, a careful and formal Italian learned from decades of dealing with Venetian agents.

"Your Dutchman," Selim said, looking at Cornelis with undisguised assessment.

"My factor," Yusuf said, which was the first time he had used that word, and Cornelis felt something shift slightly in his understanding of his own position.

Selim showed them silk. Not the raw cocoon silk of the market floor but processed cloth — bolts of it, stored in a back room behind the stall that was dim and cool and smelled of something Cornelis associated, inexplicably, with old libraries. Selim unrolled a length of deep crimson across the table and Yusuf ran his thumb along the weave in a gesture so practiced it was nearly unconscious.

"He's checking the thread count," Cornelis said, almost to himself.

Both men looked at him.

"The warp threads," Cornelis said. "The density. It determines the weight per length, which determines the true price per unit." He had seen this done in the wool trade in Amsterdam, the same thumb-across-fabric motion. "And that edge—" he pointed to where the selvedge was slightly uneven— "suggests it was woven on a narrower loom than the width implies. They've joined it."

Silence.

Selim looked at Yusuf. Said something in Turkish.

Yusuf replied without taking his eyes off Cornelis. Then, in Italian: "He asks where you learned this."

"Wool," Cornelis said. "In Amsterdam. The principle is the same."

Selim studied him for a moment longer, then laughed — a genuine laugh, the kind that came from being caught — and began rolling up the bolt. He and Yusuf negotiated briefly. The joined bolt was set aside. Selim produced another.

Afterward, walking back through the courtyard, Yusuf said: "That bolt — at the price he was asking — would have cost me eight percent."

"I know," Cornelis said.

"You saved it by seeing."

"It was obvious."

"It was obvious to you," Yusuf said. "Because you know wool." He stopped walking. "What else do you know?"

It was not a casual question. Cornelis understood it as the kind of question that, answered honestly, determined something.

"Wool," he said. "Basic cloth. Alum and dye quality — I learned that in the Amsterdam trade. Spices I can assess by smell and weight but not by origin. Leather I know poorly. Metals I know from the accounts side only, not the physical." He paused. "I know numbers well. I know when a contract has a hidden liability. I know when a quantity doesn't match the invoice."

Yusuf listened to all of this. "And languages?"

"Dutch. Latin. French. This Italian. I have thirty words of Turkish."

"You will have more," Yusuf said, as if this were simply a matter of time and road. "Turkish, and also some Arabic — for the markets further east, if we go further east." He began walking again. "You should also learn to read the silk. Not just the weave — the origin. Bursa silk and İznik silk look the same to most eyes. They do not perform the same in the dye."

"How long to learn that?"

Yusuf considered. "Selim will teach you. Two weeks, perhaps three. He likes you now."

Cornelis thought about the *Noord-Holland* and October and Pauw's expectant face. He did some arithmetic, quietly, walking through the *Koza Han* courtyard with the silk merchants moving around him and the light coming down clean through the central dome.

"I have time," he said.

---

They stayed in Bursa seventeen days.

Cornelis spent his mornings with Selim in the back room of the silk stall, learning to read cloth the way he had once learned to read wool — with his hands first, then his eyes, building a vocabulary of texture and weight and the particular way light moved across different qualities of weave. Selim was a patient teacher who communicated partly in Italian, partly in Turkish that Cornelis was accumulating faster than he'd expected, and partly in the universal language of the merchant's table: the sample placed before you, the question in the eyes, the wait for your assessment.

His afternoons he spent with Yusuf's accounts, which were more complex than they had appeared on the road. There were relationships here that went back years — credit extended to smaller merchants, goods taken on consignment, a system of informal agreements that functioned like contracts without the paper. Cornelis organized it, not changing the substance but giving it a structure that made the liabilities visible. Yusuf looked at the resulting pages with the expression of a man seeing a familiar room newly tidied.

"You've made the worry smaller," he said.

"I've made the worry *legible*," Cornelis said. "It was always this size."

Yusuf almost laughed.

In the evenings he wrote in the back of his ledger. The pages were filling — Turkish words, observations about the silk market, the price differential between raw cocoon and finished cloth at different stages of processing, a sketch he had attempted of the *Koza Han* courtyard that was not good but was recognizable. He had begun, without deciding to, to write in a mixture of Dutch and Italian, sometimes mid-sentence, as if the two languages together were more accurate than either alone.

On the fifteenth day, Selim showed him a bolt of silk and waited.

Cornelis examined it. Ran his thumb along the warp. Held the selvedge to the light from the doorway. Brought it close enough to see the individual threads.

"Bursa," he said. "From the western workshops — the ones closer to the river. Woven tight, good weight. Dyed after weaving, not before — you can see it in how the color sits on the surface rather than through the thread." He set it down. "Good quality. Not exceptional."

Selim looked at him for a moment. Then he said something in Turkish, turned, and went to serve another customer.

Cornelis looked at Yusuf, who was leaning in the doorway.

"He said: *adequate*," Yusuf translated. "From Selim, this is high praise."

---

They left Bursa on a Tuesday, the road turning southeast now, the mountain behind them, the Anatolian plateau opening ahead. The caravan was larger than it had been — two additional merchants joining for the road to Konya, their goods adding a pleasant complexity to the manifest that Cornelis found himself genuinely enjoying the management of.

Mehmet had developed an opinion about Cornelis, which was that Mehmet was more reliable than Cornelis at anticipating what a horse might want, and transferred his primary loyalty accordingly. Cornelis accepted this without taking it personally.

On the first evening out of Bursa, Yusuf rode alongside him as the road climbed through oak forest toward the plateau.

"Selim asked if I would sell your contract," he said.

Cornelis looked at him. "What did you tell him?"

"That you were not mine to sell." Yusuf paused. "But also that we were going further east, and that the education continuing would be worth more to you than staying in Bursa."

Cornelis looked at the road ahead, the oak trees thinning as the elevation increased, the first suggestion of the plateau's vast openness somewhere beyond the ridge.

"Further east than Konya?" he asked.

"Perhaps," said Yusuf.

The road climbed. The trees fell away. The plateau opened before them like a page.

### Chapter 5: The Plateau

The oak forest ended like a sentence.

One moment there were trees — the road climbing through them, dappled, enclosed, the world legible in the way that forests make the world legible, one thing at a time — and then the ridge, and then nothing. Or not nothing: everything. The Anatolian plateau opened in every direction with a scale that Cornelis had no prior experience of and no ready language for. He stopped *Amsterdam* without meaning to, and for once the horse agreed with the decision.

He had grown up with Dutch horizons, which were generous by European standards — the flat polderland going out to meet a sky that came more than halfway down. He had thought he understood horizontal distance. He did not. This was different in kind, not degree. The plateau was not flat exactly — it rolled, slowly, in long swells like a sea that had forgotten how to move — and it went on until it went hazy, and then went on further into a blue-grey that was neither sky nor land but the place where they negotiated. Somewhere in that distance were mountains — a white suggestion of them to the south that Ibrahim had called the Taurus — but they were so far away they seemed painted rather than real.

The road ran southeast and disappeared into the middle distance as a pale thread.

Yusuf rode up beside him and waited.

"How long?" Cornelis said, meaning the plateau, meaning all of it.

"To Konya? Eight days, perhaps nine." Yusuf looked at the horizon without the expression of a man looking at something remarkable, which told Cornelis something about how long it took for enormity to become ordinary. "The road is good this season. We had rain in March."

They rode on.

---

The plateau had its own logic, and Cornelis learned it the way he was learning everything here — through accumulation, through the slow build of specific instances into general understanding.

The wind was the first lesson. On the road through Bursa's forests there had been weather — rain, sun, the occasional morning mist — but wind had been a local matter, a thing that moved through trees and was redirected by hills. On the plateau the wind had no such obligations. It came from wherever it came from and went where it intended, and between those two points there was nothing to negotiate with it. The first afternoon it came from the northeast, cold still despite the season, and Cornelis learned that his coat, which had been adequate for Istanbul, was optimistic here. He watched how the other men rode — angled slightly, shoulders adjusted, a particular economy of posture that reduced the surface the wind could work on — and tried to copy it.

Mehmet noticed and said something in Turkish. Cornelis caught the word for *cold* — *soğuk*, one of his early acquisitions — and a word he didn't know.

"He says you ride like a door," Yusuf translated, without particular sympathy.

"How should I ride?"

Yusuf said something to Mehmet, who demonstrated — a subtle shift, the body carried differently, narrower. Cornelis adjusted. The wind, if not defeated, became more manageable.

He wrote *ride like a door* in the back of his ledger that evening and underneath it the adjustment, described as precisely as he could manage.

---

The villages on the plateau were different from those on the Bursa road.

They were lower, for one thing — built into the landscape rather than on it, as if modest about their own existence. Stone the color of the earth around them, so that from a distance a village was a thickening of the ground rather than a collection of structures. The people were more reserved with the caravan — not unfriendly, but measuring, the hospitality offered at a slight distance until Yusuf's name had been established, at which point it opened fully, because Yusuf's name on the plateau meant something specific: a man who paid what he said he would pay and did not misrepresent his goods and had been on this road, or his father had been on this road, longer than most people could remember.

This was, Cornelis was coming to understand, a form of currency more durable than silver.

On the third evening they stayed in a village whose headman had done business with Yusuf's father, and the meal was generous enough that even the guards relaxed visibly, and afterward there was tea — the sweet apple tea that Cornelis had been drinking since Istanbul, which he had decided he preferred to coffee — and the headman asked, through Yusuf, about the Dutchman.

Cornelis had answered this question several times now, in various forms, and had developed a version of the answer that was true and also translated well: that he had come east by water and had found the road more interesting than the return. This produced, reliably, a response that was something between recognition and approval, as if missing one's ship were a respectable life decision rather than an accident.

The headman asked — again through Yusuf, though Cornelis was now catching enough Turkish to follow the shape of it before the translation — what trade he had been learning.

"Silk," Cornelis said, in Turkish. "And accounts. And the road."

The headman looked at him for a moment, then said something that Yusuf translated with what might have been a shade of amusement: "He says the road is the longest education."

"How long did it take him?" Cornelis asked.

The headman heard enough of this to answer before Yusuf translated. He held up both hands, fingers spread. Then one hand again.

Fifteen years.

Cornelis nodded seriously. The headman nodded seriously back. Some understanding had been reached, though Cornelis could not have said precisely what it was.

---

His Turkish was growing in the way that language grows on the road: not systematically but hungrily, driven by necessity and embarrassment in roughly equal measure. He had learned the wool and silk vocabulary first, by proximity and repetition. Then the numbers — these came easily, the merchant's reflex. Then food, because hunger was a reliable teacher. Then weather, because the plateau demanded it.

What he found harder was the register — the layers of formality and informality that determined how a thing was said as much as what was said. Mehmet spoke to him in the easy Turkish of a sixteen-year-old who found adult formality unnecessary. Yusuf spoke in the careful middle register of a man of business. The headmen in the villages spoke in something older and more considered that Cornelis grasped only in outline.

He made mistakes constantly. He had learned to make them without retreating from them, which was itself a lesson — in Amsterdam, an error in speech was a thing to be corrected and not repeated. Here, a mistake made with good humor and corrected openly seemed to produce the opposite of embarrassment. Mehmet in particular found his errors instructive in both directions, and had begun, unprompted, to correct Cornelis's pronunciation with a seriousness that was its own kind of generosity.

One afternoon, riding through a long shallow valley where the road ran beside a stream still carrying the last of the mountain snowmelt, Cornelis managed an entire exchange with one of the guards — a slow exchange, the guard patient with it — about the quality of the road surface and the likelihood of rain. When it was over the guard said something to Mehmet, who relayed it to Cornelis.

"He says your Turkish is like your riding," Mehmet said, in careful Italian. "Getting better. Still like a door."

Cornelis wrote this down too.

---

On the sixth day they came to a *kervansaray* — a proper imperial caravanserai, built under Suleiman the Magnificent a century before, maintained by the state as part of the old system of protected roads. It was large enough to shelter several caravans simultaneously, and when they arrived in the late afternoon two others were already there: a spice caravan coming west from Konya, heavily laden, and a smaller group of pilgrims heading south toward the Taurus passes and beyond.

The caravanserai was built to a plan that Cornelis, who had a mind for structure, appreciated immediately: a great central hall for the animals, smaller chambers around the perimeter for the merchants, a hamam, a small mosque, a kitchen that was already producing smells of extraordinary authority. The walls were thick enough to make the interior temperature irrelevant to whatever the plateau was doing outside. The architect, working a hundred years before, had understood the road.

That evening the caravanserai was briefly its own city.

Cornelis ate with Yusuf and the westbound spice merchant, a compact man from Konya named Davud who wore his age somewhere between fifty and seventy in a way that made the question irrelevant. They traded information — the road conditions east and west, the market prices at both ends, the political atmosphere in Konya, which Davud mentioned with a careful neutrality that suggested there was something to be neutral about.

Cornelis followed perhaps sixty percent of the Turkish and filled the rest from context. At one point Davud asked him a direct question, and he understood it — *what do you carry?* — and answered it himself, in Turkish, before Yusuf could intervene: the manifest, summarized, goods and weights and destination.

Davud looked at Yusuf. Said something.

Yusuf answered. Davud looked back at Cornelis with an expression that Cornelis was learning to read as the recalibration of an initial assessment.

"What did he say?" Cornelis asked Yusuf, in Italian.

"He said: 'Your Dutchman speaks like a merchant.'" Yusuf picked up his tea. "I told him that was because you are one."

Cornelis sat with this for a moment. Outside the thick walls the plateau wind was doing its work, and the oil lamps in the great hall moved in a draft from somewhere, and the pilgrims on the far side of the room were speaking in low voices in Arabic that he did not yet know, and the smells of the kitchen had given way to the smells of the night — stone and old wool and the particular dry cold of the interior.

He had been a junior factor. He had been a clerk with a ledger and a passage home booked on a ship that left without him. He was not sure, sitting in a hundred-year-old caravanserai six days into the Anatolian plateau, what he was becoming. But the word Yusuf had used — *merchant* — had landed differently than it would have two months ago. Less like a category. More like a direction.

He opened his ledger. Added to the accounts, which were current and correct. Then turned to the back pages, and wrote — in Dutch, which he was saving for things he wanted to keep — a sentence he had been thinking about since the ridge where the forest ended:

*The plateau does not care what you were before you arrived on it.*

He closed the ledger, and slept.

### Chapter 6: Konya

They smelled the city before they saw it.

Not unpleasantly — it was the smell of a place that had been inhabited for a very long time, the accumulated sediment of centuries of bread and tanning and woodsmoke and animal and humanity, carried on the plateau wind with a warmth that the open road did not have. Then the road crested a long slow rise and Konya was below them in the early morning light, spread across the plain at the foot of a low hill, the green dome of the Mevlâna shrine visible above everything else like a thumb pressed into the skyline.

Mehmet said something reverent. Even the guards adjusted their posture slightly.

"Konya," Yusuf said, unnecessarily.

---

It was one of the oldest cities in the world, though it wore this fact quietly. Çatalhöyük — the great Neolithic settlement that predated writing and the wheel and most of what Europeans meant when they said *civilization* — was a day's walk to the southeast, though only the farmers who occasionally turned up strange shaped stones in their fields knew it was there. The Greeks had called the city Iconium. Paul of Tarsus had preached here, been driven out, returned. The Seljuk Turks had made it their capital three centuries before the Ottomans existed, and the Seljuk fingerprints were still visible in the stonework, in the tilework, in the particular quality of the city's sense of itself — older than Istanbul, less imperial, more certain of its own significance in ways that did not require demonstration.

And Rumi was here. Had been here for three and a half centuries, in the green-domed tomb at the center of the city, and the city had organized itself around his presence the way a landscape organizes itself around a mountain — not worshipping exactly, but oriented, everything pointing slightly toward that green dome without meaning to.

Cornelis knew the name. He had encountered it in Leiden, in a Latin translation of Persian poetry that a professor had recommended with the careful enthusiasm of a man sharing something he suspected his students were not ready for. He had read some of it, found it beautiful and bewildering in equal measure, and filed it under *things to return to* in the way that young men file things they do not yet have the experience to understand.

He had not expected to arrive at the source.

---

Yusuf's business in Konya took three days, and it was more complex than Bursa had been.

The city was a junction — roads coming in from the west, from the south through the Taurus passes, from the east along the plateau, and from the north. What this meant commercially was that Konya's markets carried a layering that Bursa's, for all its silk wealth, did not have. Bursa knew what it was. Konya knew everything that passed through it, which was a different kind of knowledge.

Yusuf moved through it with a different manner than he had in Bursa — more careful, more deliberate, the greetings warmer in some places and more measured in others. He was reading something that Cornelis could not yet read. On the second morning Cornelis asked him directly, in the Italian they used for things that required precision.

"Davud said something about the political atmosphere," Cornelis said. "You were both careful about it."

Yusuf was quiet for a moment — not evasive, just considering how much context was necessary. They were walking through the copper market, the sound of hammering a constant texture beneath the conversation.

"The Janissaries," Yusuf said. "There have been difficulties. Between the Sultan's authority and the corps. It is not new — there are always difficulties — but at this moment the difficulties are closer to the surface than usual." He glanced at Cornelis. "For a merchant, this means: be careful about whose side of a dispute you appear to take. Be careful about who you are seen with. Be generous with officials without being so generous it looks like a bribe that requires concealment."

"Is it dangerous?"

Yusuf made the sound that was not quite a laugh. "Everything is dangerous. The road is dangerous. The markets are dangerous. Konya at this particular moment requires care. It is not the same as danger." He paused. "Stay close to me in the bazaar. Don't speak Turkish to officials — let me speak. Your foreignness is useful here. A Dutch factor is nobody's enemy."

Cornelis filed this and said nothing further. But he noticed, after that, the texture of what Yusuf was doing — the way he positioned himself in conversations, who he faced and who he kept at a slight angle, which invitations he accepted and which he declined with particular warmth, as if the warmth itself were the important thing and the declination incidental.

It was, Cornelis thought, not unlike accounting. The same information, managed differently, produced entirely different outcomes.

---

On the second afternoon Yusuf sent him to the Mevlâna shrine alone.

Not for business — there was no business there. "You should see it," Yusuf said simply. "It will be useful later to have seen it."

Cornelis did not ask useful how.

The shrine complex was approached through a courtyard of ablution fountains, the sound of water a deliberate transition from the city outside. He removed his shoes at the threshold as he had seen others do, and went in.

The interior was dim and cool and layered with the particular silence of a place where many people are being quiet simultaneously — not empty silence but full silence, weighted. The sarcophagus of Jalāl ad-Dīn Rūmī stood at the center, draped in cloth embroidered in gold, beneath the green dome that was taller inside than it appeared from the street. Around it, in the dim light, people moved slowly or stood still or bent in private attitudes of address that Cornelis could not read and did not try to.

He stood for a long time.

He was not a religious man in the active sense — he had been raised correctly Reformed, attended services with the dutiful regularity of a family that understood the social contract of faith without being consumed by it. He had no framework for what this place was or what it asked of him. But he understood, standing in it, that something had happened here — that a man had thought and written and loved and suffered in this city three centuries ago, and that the thinking had been powerful enough that people were still coming from very far away to stand near where he was buried, and that this was not nothing.

He thought about the Latin translation in Leiden. The lines he remembered imperfectly: something about the reed flute cut from the reed bed, crying for its origin. He had not understood it at twenty, reading it in a cold library in the Netherlands. He was not sure he understood it now. But he thought he understood more of what there was to understand.

He bought a small copy of the *Masnavi* from a bookseller outside the shrine — not in a language he could read, Arabic script that was closed to him — and put it in his coat pocket, weight against his ribs, next to the ledger.

When he came out Yusuf was waiting in the courtyard, which surprised him.

"I thought you had business," Cornelis said.

"I did." Yusuf looked at him with the assessing patience Cornelis had come to recognize as his version of a question.

"I bought a book I can't read," Cornelis said.

Yusuf nodded as if this were a reasonable response to the shrine. "I will teach you the letters," he said. "On the road east. It gives you something to do in the saddle."

"East of Konya," Cornelis said.

"Yes."

"How far east of Konya?"

Yusuf began walking toward the courtyard gate. "There is a man in Kayseri I have been meaning to see for two seasons. He trades in things that require careful accounting." He said this without looking back. "The road is twelve days."

Cornelis stood in the shrine courtyard for a moment. He was, by his calculation, already past the point at which a direct return to Istanbul would guarantee the *Noord-Holland.* He had known this for several days. He had not mentioned it to Yusuf, and Yusuf had not mentioned it to him, and this mutual silence had the quality of an agreement reached without negotiation.

He followed Yusuf through the gate.

---

That evening he wrote a letter to Hendrick Pauw at the Galata trading house. He wrote it in Dutch, which felt appropriate for the purpose, and kept it factual: his location, his employment, his expected delay, a request that Pauw hold any correspondence for him until his return, date uncertain. He did not write about the shrine, or the book in his pocket, or what the plateau had said to him on the morning they crested the ridge and saw all that distance.

He sealed it and gave it to a westbound courier that Yusuf knew — a reliable man, Yusuf said, who would reach Istanbul within the fortnight.

Then he turned back to the accounts.

Then, later, the back pages.

He wrote: *Konya. The green dome. A book I cannot read. Twelve days further east, toward wherever Yusuf is from.*

And below that, after a pause:

*I did not tell Pauw I am not coming in October. I told him the date was uncertain. This is true. It is also, I think, no longer the most important question.*
