  Topic: How the Society got its two engines — Oldenburg's correspondence/Philosophical Transactions and Hooke's weekly experiments
  Time Period: 1662–c. 1670
  Follows from: User chose B (Oldenburg) into A (the first meetings and Hooke's demonstrations)
  This chapter covers: The two people who, between them, made the Royal Society actually *do* anything in its first decade — Henry Oldenburg running a continent-wide correspondence network from Gresham College that became *Philosophical Transactions* in March 1665, and Robert Hooke producing three or four experimental demonstrations a week on a modest salary.
  Key elements:
    - Oldenburg's background: Bremen-born diplomat, multilingual, connected to Boyle through tutoring Boyle's nephew
    - The correspondence machine: 3,000+ surviving letters, weekly news from Danzig to Florence
    - March 1665: the launch of Philosophical Transactions — a private venture, initially, to spare Oldenburg the copying labor
    - The birth of peer review — sending submissions to qualified readers
    - Hooke appointed Curator of Experiments 1662, contractually obligated to produce demonstrations weekly
    - What the meetings actually looked like: the air-pump, microscopes, pneumatic trials, mechanical contrivances
    - Micrographia (1665): the engraved flea, the coined word "cell", Pepys reading till 2 a.m.
    - The partnership (and tension) between the gentleman-patron model (Boyle) and the paid-craftsman model (Hooke)
  Figures featured: Oldenburg, Hooke, Boyle (background), Pepys (cameo), Wilkins
  Research note: Oldenburg imprisoned briefly in the Tower in 1667 on suspicion during Second Anglo-Dutch War — worth noting; verify Hooke's salary figure if mentioned
  Approach: Parallel portrait of two workhorses, both slightly outside the gentleman class, whose labor defined what the Society actually was in practice
  Research bible implications: Adds Oldenburg and the mechanics of the weekly meetings; sets up future chapters on Micrographia's reception, the plague/fire disruptions, and the Newton-Hooke conflict

CHAPTER HANDOFF
  Written: 2026-04-10

  A) Plague and fire — 1665–66 dispersed the Society to Gresham's temporary quarters and then nearly destroyed its home; how the young institution survived two civic catastrophes in eighteen months.
  B) The Society and its critics — the 1660s saw sharp attacks (Henry Stubbe, Meric Casaubon, and later Jonathan Swift), plus the oddly defensive tone of Thomas Sprat's 1667 official *History*. Who was making fun of them, and why?
  C) Margaret Cavendish's 1667 visit — the Duchess of Newcastle, prolific natural philosopher and celebrity, talked her way into a meeting as the Society's only woman visitor for two and a half centuries. What happened, and what it says about who the Society imagined itself to be.
  D) Oldenburg in the Tower — the Secretary was arrested in 1667 on suspicion of treasonous correspondence during the Second Anglo-Dutch War. A short, strange episode about what it meant to run an international letter network in wartime.
