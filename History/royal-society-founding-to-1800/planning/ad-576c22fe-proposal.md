  Topic: What it meant, in 1665, to suddenly be able to see the small world — Micrographia and the cultural impact of the microscope
  This addendum covers: The physical experience of the book itself (the foldouts, the scale), the specific moments of revelation it produced, the skeptics who thought Hooke was making things up, and the deeper shift — that ordinary familiar things (a flea, a piece of cork, the edge of a razor) turned out to have whole architectures inside them that nobody had suspected.
  Key elements:
    - The foldout flea and louse — physical scale of the plates, bigger than the book
    - The cork cells and the monastery metaphor
    - The razor's edge and the needle's point — familiar objects exposed as rough
    - Pepys and Evelyn's reactions as contemporary documents
    - The skeptics who said Hooke must be "improving on nature"
    - The larger point: the microscope as an epistemic shift, not just a new instrument
  Connection to main thread: Completes the picture of 1665 as the Society's breakthrough year — the demonstration table and the correspondence desk were quiet workhorses, but Micrographia was the thing that made the public realize what the new philosophy could see.
