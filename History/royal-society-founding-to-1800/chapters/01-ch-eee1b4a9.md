On the night of 28 November 1660, twelve men sat through a lecture on astronomy at Gresham College in Bishopsgate, and then stayed behind to do something unremarkable. They resolved to keep meeting. They agreed to collect a weekly subscription of a shilling. They nominated a registrar, drew up a list of people to invite, and went home. It is the sort of decision that small clubs make every week in every city, and ordinarily nobody writes it down.

This one was written down. The minute still exists, in a thin book that the Royal Society has preserved ever since. It records, in the cautious phrasing the times demanded, a plan for "a Colledge for the Promoting of Physico-Mathematicall Experimentall Learning." The twelve signed themselves to it. Charles II had been back on his throne for less than six months. None of them yet called themselves the Royal Society, and none of them would have said, that night, that they were founding anything. They were just formalising a habit.

The habit had two roots, both of them grown in the ruins of the Civil War.

The first was in London. Sometime around 1645 — the year the king's cause collapsed at Naseby — a handful of men with a shared interest in what they called the "new philosophy" began meeting weekly, sometimes at Gresham College and sometimes at the Wood Street lodgings of the physician Jonathan Goddard. They were a mixed company. The mathematician John Wallis was there. So was Theodore Haak, a German émigré connected to the Continental reformers around Samuel Hartlib. They talked, as Wallis later remembered, of "the circulation of the blood, the valves in the veins, the Copernican Hypothesis, the nature of comets and new stars, the satellites of Jupiter" — the full catalogue of what had unsettled the old natural philosophy. They agreed, tactfully, to leave theology and politics at the door. It was the only way a group of that kind could meet in London in the late 1640s and not get somebody arrested.

Around this same period, from his rooms in Stalbridge and then in London, the young Robert Boyle was writing excited letters to friends about what he called "our invisible college," or sometimes "our philosophical college." Historians used to assume he meant the Gresham group. They no longer do. Boyle's "invisible college" looks, on closer reading, like a different and more utopian network — something closer to the Hartlib circle's dream of universal reform through knowledge, with Boyle's sister Lady Ranelagh at its centre. Whichever it was, it planted in Boyle the conviction that natural philosophy had to be a collective enterprise, pursued by letter and by instrument, and not the solitary labour of a scholar in his study.

The second root was in Oxford. In 1648 the Parliamentary visitors reorganised the university, and a Yorkshireman named John Wilkins — royalist by inclination, mathematician by training, tactically conformable in all weathers — was installed as Warden of Wadham College. Wilkins was that rare administrative temperament that attracts people simply by being unflappable. Within a year his lodgings at Wadham had become the weekly meeting place of what contemporaries called, variously, the Philosophical Club or the Oxford Experimental Philosophy Club. Boyle came up to Oxford in the mid-1650s partly to be near it. Christopher Wren, still primarily a mathematician and astronomer, came as an undergraduate and then stayed. The astronomer Seth Ward was there. So was Wallis, now Savilian Professor. So were the anatomist Thomas Willis, the physician Richard Lower, and the political arithmetician William Petty. A gawky, sharp-eyed young man named Robert Hooke appeared as Boyle's paid assistant; he is supposed to have tested a flying machine in the Warden's garden, though nobody seems to have seen it fly. For a few years the most interesting scientific room in England was Wilkins's parlour.

Wilkins's gift was that he believed experimental inquiry could survive any regime. It was a creed he needed. He married Oliver Cromwell's sister and, when the Protectorate began to crumble, steered the Oxford group through the transition with most of its membership intact. By 1659 several of them had drifted back to London. Wren took the Gresham astronomy chair. Boyle set up his air-pump in Pall Mall. The London and Oxford roots were, in practice, braiding together.

Then Charles II came home, and on 28 November 1660 Wren gave his lecture, and the twelve stayed behind.

What turned their resolution into an institution was not the meeting itself but the presence in the room of Sir Robert Moray. Moray was a Scottish soldier, a freemason, a dabbler in alchemy, and — crucially — an old confidant of the king's. At the second meeting of the new club he reported that Charles II had been told about them and was pleased. A king's pleasure, in Restoration England, was a thing that could be turned into paper. It took eighteen months of drafting, courtier-work, and polite revision, but on 15 July 1662 a royal charter was signed, formally incorporating "The Royal Society of London." A second, more generous charter followed the next year. Lord Brouncker, a mathematician and courtier from the founding twelve, became the first President.

None of this made the Society rich, or gave it teeth. What the charters gave it was a name and a seal, and the right to exist openly. For a group whose habit had begun in the paranoid back rooms of the 1640s, that was the thing worth writing down.
