RESEARCH BIBLE
===============

SUBJECT & SCOPE
  Topic: China's transformation from imperial rule to the People's Republic and beyond
  Time Period: Late Qing dynasty (~1860s) through modern era
  Geographic Focus: China (broad national scope; cities and regions as relevant)
  Core question: How did one of history's oldest continuous civilizations collapse, reconstitute itself repeatedly, and end up governed by a Communist party — and what did that feel like from the inside?

KEY FIGURES
  Empress Dowager Cixi — De facto ruler of Qing China from 1861 until her death in 1908. Conservative anchor of the late court; suppressed the 1898 Hundred Days' Reform and had the Guangxu Emperor placed under house arrest. Revisionists challenge the "Dragon Lady" caricature. Central figure.
    Status: central, not yet featured in a chapter
  Guangxu Emperor — Nominal emperor 1875–1908, Cixi's nephew. Backed the 1898 reformers; placed under house arrest by Cixi. Likely murdered by arsenic poisoning the day before Cixi died.
    Status: peripheral, mentioned

ESTABLISHED CONTEXT
  THE QING DYNASTY (1644-1912)
  - Founded by the Manchu people, a seminomadic group northeast of China proper. Conquered a crumbling Ming dynasty in 1644; ruled 268 years.
  - Manchu minority ruling Han majority: a permanent ethnic tension. The Qing performed Chinese civilization thoroughly — mastering Classical Chinese, performing Confucian rites — while privately maintaining Manchu-Han separation (separate city districts, bannerman military caste, the queue enforced as submission).
  - Claimed the Mandate of Heaven (Tianming): heaven grants rightful rule to moral emperors and withdraws it from failing ones. Signs of withdrawal: floods, famines, rebellions, military defeat.

  SOCIAL STRUCTURE
  - ~85% peasants. Land fragmented generationally. Rents consumed half or more of yields. Tax manipulation by magistrates endemic.
  - Gentry (lower exam degree holders): mediators between state and village. Managed local governance, irrigation, militias, charity.
  - Scholar-officials (higher degrees): the imperial bureaucracy proper.
  - Examination system: 12-century-old meritocratic filter. Created a ruling class unified by Confucian texts. Also created millions of failed candidates — educated, underemployed, politically volatile.

  DEMOGRAPHIC CRISIS
  - Population tripled ~1650-1850 (150M to 400M+) without equivalent agricultural expansion.
  - Land fragmentation, rising tenancy, chronic rural poverty. Famine cycles frequent.

  THE CENTURY OF HUMILIATION
  - First Opium War (1839-42): Treaty of Nanjing — first unequal treaty. Hong Kong ceded, treaty ports opened.
  - Second Opium War (1856-60): British and French troops burned the Old Summer Palace, Beijing.
  - Extraterritoriality: foreigners exempt from Chinese law in treaty ports. A daily humiliation for the educated class.
  - Taiping Rebellion (1850-64): 20-30 million dead. Qing survived only via regional armies (Xiang Army under Zeng Guofan) — power began shifting toward regional strongmen.

  SELF-STRENGTHENING MOVEMENT (1860s-1880s)
  - Qing attempt to modernize military without changing political system. Built arsenals, shipyards, the Beiyang Fleet.
  - Philosophy: "Chinese learning for fundamentals, Western learning for practical application."
  - The fleet was the showcase of modernization — and was destroyed in 1894.

  THE 1895 CIVILIZATIONAL SHOCK
  - First Sino-Japanese War (1894-95): Beiyang Fleet destroyed at Battle of Yalu River (Sept 1894). Japan had modernized in 30 years and deployed it decisively.
  - Treaty of Shimonoseki (April 1895): Taiwan, Penghu Islands, Liaodong Peninsula ceded to Japan. 200 million taels indemnity. Korean independence recognized.
  - The shock: Japan had been a cultural tributary — a recipient of Chinese civilization. Its victory was not just military humiliation but civilizational disorientation. The conclusion many drew: the problem wasn't the West — it was China's own system.

THEMATIC THREADS
  - The Mandate of Heaven: legitimacy conditional on performance. Every chapter will find rulers claiming it and being judged against it.
  - Ethnic identity and power: Manchu vs. Han, and later other fault lines (class, region, ideology) threading through political change.
  - Reform suppressed from above, then arriving too late: a pattern visible from 1895 onward.
  - Regional vs. central power: a recurring Chinese political problem from the Taiping armies to the warlords to the Communist base areas.
  - Foreign humiliation as political fuel: driving both nationalism and radicalism across the 20th century.

THREADS TO PULL
  - The Boxer Rebellion (1900): peasant anti-foreign violence, Qing court's split reaction, Eight-Nation Alliance occupation of Beijing — the dynasty's nadir
  - The Hundred Days' Reform (1898) and its failure: Guangxu Emperor, reformers Kang Youwei and Liang Qichao, Cixi's coup
  - The 1911 Revolution and founding of the Republic of China — Sun Yat-sen, Yuan Shikai
  - The warlord era (1916-1928): what happens when central authority fully collapses
  - The May Fourth Movement (1919): intellectuals, nationalism, the birth of Chinese Communism
  - The Long March and Mao's rise within the CCP
  - The Second Sino-Japanese War (1937-1945): the crucible that exhausted the KMT and empowered the CCP
  - The Civil War and Communist victory (1949) — what motivated ordinary people on both sides
  - What "liberation" actually felt like on the ground
