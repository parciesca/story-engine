STORY BIBLE
============

PREMISE & TONE
  Genre: Occupational literary fiction
  Tone: Grounded, human-scale, quiet tension. The story lives in specific detail — the feel of felt under a finger, the smell of old wood and dried leather. No explosions. No chosen ones. The drama is the kind that happens when people meet objects that have been waiting.
  Core premise: Vera Brandt, a traveling restorer of antique mechanical musical instruments, takes a commission to evaluate an orchestrion in a dead man's farmhouse in rural Iowa. What she finds in the instrument — and in the heir — pulls her somewhere she didn't expect.

DRAMATIS PERSONAE
  Vera Brandt — Protagonist. Traveling restorer of antique mechanical music instruments (player pianos, orchestrions, music boxes, barrel organs). 51 years old. Lives out of her van. Divorced, no children. Recently lost her apprentice (Marcus), who left and took two clients with her. Precise, dry, uncomfortable with sentiment — but capable of great patience with objects. Voice anchor: she says what she means, which is rarely what she feels. Does not complain. Does not explain herself unless asked.
    Status: active

  Clem Horska — Estate heir. Mid-to-late 30s, inheriting his grandfather's farmhouse in Haugen, Iowa. Some sense of being a bit lost, inheriting a house full of things he doesn't understand and doesn't know how to value. Called the Mechanical Music Preservation Society to find someone. Voice anchor: careful with his words; there's something he's not saying yet.
    Status: active

WORLD RULES
  — Vera's trade is real and small: mechanical music instrument restoration. Orchestrions, player pianos, music boxes, barrel organs. It's a dying trade. She was trained by a mentor (unnamed so far). Her apprentice Marcus recently departed.
  — She lives peripatetically, driving between clients across the Midwest.
  — She drives a 1999 dark green Ford Econoline van, fully equipped as a mobile workshop.
  — The Mechanical Music Preservation Society is the industry body / referral network.
  — An orchestrion is a large self-playing instrument — cabinet, percussion, wind chest, pipes — that operates on pneumatics and rolls. Most were scrapped by 1940. Finding one intact is unusual.
  — Vera names a flat assessment fee regardless of whether the client proceeds with restoration.

THEMATIC THREADS
  — What we inherit that we didn't ask for (Clem and the farmhouse / orchestrion)
  — What we preserve and what we let go (Vera's whole vocation; her apprentice leaving)
  — Objects as containers of time
  — The cost of precision: people who are good with things and harder with people

UNRESOLVED TENSIONS
  — Why did Marcus (the apprentice) really leave? He took two clients — within his rights — but something cracked.
  — What is the orchestrion exactly? Manufacturer unknown. Forty years untouched minimum.
  — What was Vera's grandfather (or mentor?) like? She had one — who trained her?
  — Clem said his grandfather "wound it sometimes" — why was the grandfather so attached to a broken instrument?
  — There's something Clem isn't saying yet.

TIMELINE & STATE
  Current location: Vera is on the road in Illinois, heading north after a player piano job. She has agreed to arrive in Haugen, Iowa in two weeks.
  Time position: Early April, present day (approximately)
  Key events so far:
    Ch 1: Vera completes a player piano evaluation (Mason & Hamlin, a deceased mother's instrument). She receives a call from Clem Horska about an orchestrion in his late grandfather's Iowa farmhouse. She accepts the commission; arrives in two weeks.

READER PROMISES
  — We will see the orchestrion. The buildup of its description (complete, large, forty years untouched, a grandfather who wound it in the dark) promises something worth arriving at.
  — Vera's loss of Marcus will surface — it was set up, it has weight, it will matter.
  — Clem is hiding something (or doesn't know he is). That will emerge.
  — The story has committed to the specific texture of Vera's craft — it will continue to deliver that.
