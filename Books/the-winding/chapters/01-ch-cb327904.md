The hammers inside the Mason & Hamlin were felted in three different densities, which told Vera something. She pulled the key slip free and set it on the carpet, then leaned in with her headlamp and looked at the stack rails the way a doctor looks at someone's throat — not searching for anything specific yet, just taking inventory.

"My mother played it," the woman said from the doorway. She was holding a coffee mug in both hands and had been holding it for twenty minutes without drinking from it.

"Mm," Vera said.

The action was a mess. Someone had regulat it in the eighties, judging by the replacement parts, and not very well. Three hammers were completely unvoiced — the felt packed down to a surface like hardwood — and the damper timing on the bass end was off by enough that anything slow would sound waterlogged. But the cabinet was original, the soundboard had no cracks she could find, and the player mechanism, when she'd pumped the pedals, ran smoother than it had any right to after forty years of sitting.

She took a hammer out and held it under the light. The original felt, where it hadn't been replaced, was still a reasonable depth. The bones were good.

"When did she stop playing?" Vera asked.

"When she couldn't reach the pedals anymore." The woman finally drank from the mug. "That was, God, twelve years ago? She kept it tuned, though. She was very particular about that."

"You can hear it," Vera said. Which was true. The piano hadn't gone to war against itself the way untuned instruments did. It had just gone quiet.

She put the hammer back and straightened up. Her lower back registered its opinion. She'd been crouched over the action for forty minutes and she was fifty-one years old and these were facts that existed in the world whether she attended to them or not.

She gave the woman a number. The woman accepted it without negotiating, which meant it was probably low, and they shook hands at the door.

Her van was a 1999 Ford Econoline she'd bought from a retired plumber and repainted twice, currently a dark green that in certain lights looked almost black. In the back she kept a fold-down work surface, two rolling toolboxes, a parts cabinet sorted by instrument type, and a two-burner propane stove that she cooked on when she didn't feel like stopping. The back seat held her bag, her laptop, and four months of Mechanical Music Digest she hadn't finished reading.

She drove north on the state route with the windows cracked. It was early April and the fields were still winter-grey, but something in the air quality had changed — a loosening. She'd been out since February. Before that, a week at her sister's place in Peoria, which had lasted about five days longer than it should have.

Her phone rang through the van's ancient Bluetooth, which connected about sixty percent of the time, and this was one of the sixty percent.

"Vera Brandt?"

"Speaking."

"My name is Clem Horska. I got your name from the Mechanical Music Preservation Society. I have an instrument that needs evaluation. Possibly restoration." A pause. "It's an orchestrion."

She put her blinker on out of habit, though there was nothing behind her for a quarter mile. "What kind?"

"I don't actually know. It's in my grandfather's house. Was my grandfather's. He died in January."

"Where's the house?"

"Outside Haugen, Iowa."

She'd been to Haugen once, in 2011, for a Wurlitzer barrel organ in a grain elevator that turned out to be a Bruder. The town was a straight road and a grain elevator and a bar. She tried to remember if the bar had been good and couldn't.

"How large is the instrument?"

"It takes up most of a room."

That was something. A full orchestrion — a proper one, cabinet, percussion, wind chest — was not something you saw every year. Most of them had been scrapped for parts by 1940, the pneumatic tubing perished, the mechanisms cannibalized. Finding one intact was either very lucky or the result of someone very stubborn.

"Do you have photographs?"

He emailed them while she was still on the phone. She pulled over on the gravel shoulder to look. The photos were taken with a phone in a dim room, which told her the instrument's dimensions but not much else. What she could see: a cabinet that ran eight feet wide and at least that tall, dark walnut or close to it, with carved panels above the main doors and what looked like a glass front on the upper register showing stopped pipes. The manufacturer's plate wasn't visible. The condition was impossible to assess from the photos, but the cabinet itself was intact — no obvious collapse, no missing sections.

"When did it last run?" she asked.

"I don't know if it ever ran in my lifetime. My grandfather wound it sometimes. I remember the sound when I was very small." He stopped. "It's been forty years since anyone's touched it, at minimum."

Forty years in a farmhouse in Iowa. Pneumatic pouches rotted, probably. Valve leather cracked. The bellows might be salvageable or might be powder. A job like this, if the instrument was what it looked like — if it was complete, if the mechanism was worth restoring — was six weeks of work minimum. Possibly longer.

She named a rate. There was a silence on his end that told her she'd either shocked him or he was doing arithmetic.

"That's fair," he said finally. "I thought it would be more."

"Depends what I find," she said. "I'll give you a full assessment first. After that you decide if you want to proceed. The assessment is a flat fee either way."

"When can you come?"

She looked at the fields. Three jobs in her queue, all in Illinois, none urgent. Her apprentice — former apprentice — had taken two clients with him when he left, which had been his right, technically, and she'd told him so, and then she'd sat in a parking lot in Kankakee for forty-five minutes before she could drive again.

"Two weeks," she said. "I'll call when I'm a day out."

After she hung up she sat on the shoulder for a moment longer, the engine idling, the fields doing nothing in particular. An orchestrion in a farmhouse. Someone's grandfather, winding it in the dark.

She put the van in gear and drove.
