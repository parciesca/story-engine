The main path is, it must be said, an excellent path. Wide enough for two people walking abreast, smooth underfoot from centuries of confident feet, the glowmoss thick and obliging on the walls — it has the comfortable quality of a road that has been used so often it has worn a groove not just in the stone but in the general idea of stone. You set your shoulders, roll your neck in a way that feels purposeful, and walk.

The first chamber is exactly what everyone says it is. Sacks of flour along the left wall, each one stamped with Hendrik the baker's mark — the little wheel you'd recognise anywhere. Barrels of root vegetables along the right: turnips, parsnips, a determined row of something purple that is probably an experimental beet and is demonstrably not magical. The whole chamber smells like bread that hasn't happened yet, which is to say it smells like potential, which is really rather apt.

You pass through it at a dignified pace. This is going well. This is, in fact, exactly the right amount of adventure.

The second chamber is similarly shaped and is, if anything, even more organised. Someone has installed shelving — proper shelving, sanded smooth, bearing jars of pickled things in cheerful rows: onions, gherkins, something red that might be peppers and might have a more interesting provenance. There is a small stool in the corner with a lantern above it, extinguished now, for when the apprentices come to fetch things. There is, on a hook by the exit, a ledger.

You glance at it. This is the kind of thing you do.

The entries are in a careful hand, the dates regular. *37 lbs turnips, charged to Festival Account.* Standard enough. Then, a few pages in: *Note — the green smell is getting stronger. Asked Marta. She said not to worry.* The following entry reads simply: *Still there. Not worrying.* The one after that: *Fine.*

You return the ledger to its hook, because you are also fine, and because some questions answer themselves eventually and some questions answer themselves only if you keep walking, and you won't know which this is until you do.

You keep walking.

Beyond the second chamber, the shelving stops and the dungeon begins in earnest. The torchbrackets on the walls — wrought iron, original to the construction, still carrying the same geometric motif as the entrance arch — stand empty and have clearly stood empty for some time. The glowmoss thickens here, as if it prefers the uninterrupted dark, clustering in shapes that might be patterns or might be the kind of thing that looks like patterns when you've been underground for ten minutes and your eyes are looking for something familiar to hold onto.

The air shifts. Less cellar-cold, more stone-cold, with something underneath it that you'd call ancient if you were inclined toward drama and simply *old* if you were being practical. You are being practical. It's old. It smells pleasantly of minerals and time.

This is fine. You are doing very well.

The passage opens into a small antechamber — hexagonal, which is interesting, the ceiling vaulted above the six equal walls. The glowmoss here has arranged itself into something that is almost certainly a spiral, or possibly a sunburst, or conceivably a complicated opinion about spirals. There are exits ahead, left, and right, each an archway of the same fitted stone, each leading into shadow that the glowmoss hasn't quite gotten around to illuminating yet.

There is also a fourth opening. It is not, precisely, a door. It is more of a gap in the wall at approximately knee height — rounded at the edges, as though it were made with some care, and behind it something rustles briefly, then holds its breath.

And then there is the notice board.

It is mounted on the wall to your right: sturdy oak frame, corkboard in good condition, the kind of notice board that someone has maintained with genuine dedication. Three things are pinned to it, all of them in excellent condition.

The first is a hand-drawn map of the first two chambers. Extremely accurate — the shelving is marked, the barrel positions noted, and a small star labels one spot with the annotation: *good stool.*

The second is a notice, written in a small and exceedingly tidy hand: PLEASE DO NOT FEED THE ECHOES.

The third is a response in entirely different handwriting, considerably larger: WHAT ARE ECHOES.

Below that, in the original small hand, someone has added: *If you have to ask, you haven't heard them yet. Please don't worry. Also please don't hum.*

You stand before the notice board for rather longer than strictly necessary, reading it a second time and then a third, in the way you do when something is perfectly clear but you'd like more of it. The dungeon makes no further comment. The gap in the wall does not rustle again. The glowmoss maintains its ambiguous opinion about spirals.

Everything considered, you are having a very good morning.

Three passages wait. You still have your rope, your knife, and the comfortable confidence of someone who has, so far, navigated this entirely without incident.
