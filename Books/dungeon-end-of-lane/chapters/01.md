The dungeon at the end of Cobble Lane is, as its name rather honestly suggests, at the end of Cobble Lane. You've walked past it a hundred times on your way to the market. Everyone in Millhaven has. Old Brackenmoor Delve is practically a landmark — the way other towns have a clock tower or a particularly impressive oak, Millhaven has a dungeon.

You've never gone in. Not properly. When you were nine, you and Perris Cobblewick crept past the first door on a dare, touched the rusty iron ring on the second door, and ran screaming back to sunlight. That doesn't count.

Today counts.

You're not entirely sure what changed. You woke up this morning, stared at the ceiling, thought about your life choices, and decided that "person who has never actually explored the famous dungeon at the end of their street" was not the legacy you intended to leave. You packed your satchel — sensibly, with the things that actually matter: dried meat, a full waterskin, the belt knife your aunt gave you when you turned seventeen and she decided you were finally old enough not to stab yourself accidentally. You sharpened the knife. You put on your second-best boots, because your best boots have a good sole and Perris always said good soles were important in dungeons. You added the rope last, because ropes seem like the sort of thing you bring to a dungeon and you happen to own one.

You are, by any reasonable measure, prepared.

The entrance arch is made of dark grey stone, slightly at odds with the honey-coloured limestone of the rest of Millhaven, as though someone carted the material in from somewhere else specifically to be ominous. The carved lintel reads, if you squint: BRACKENMOOR DELVE, and below that, in smaller letters that someone added later: EST. APPROX. 300 YEARS AGO (GIVE OR TAKE). Below that, in a completely different hand: ADVENTURERS WELCOME. SURVIVORS ESPECIALLY.

You've always found that last bit either charming or deeply unsettling, depending on your mood.

Old Marta is at her usual spot just inside the arch, seated on a three-legged stool beside a low cart draped in a tartan cloth. She's been selling adventuring supplies at this entrance for as long as anyone can remember, which means she was here when your parents were young and almost certainly before that. The supplies she sells are of debatable quality. Her assessments of people, unfortunately, are not.

"Oh," she says when you stop before her. "You, is it."

"Good morning, Marta."

"Didn't take you for the type." She looks you up and down with the frank assessment of someone who has evaluated a great many adventurers and found most of them wanting. "Your mother know you're here?"

"I'm twenty-six."

"That's not what I asked."

Her cart today holds: three bread rolls, a coil of thinner rope (not as good as yours), a candle burned down to two-thirds of its original size, and a small object labeled LUCKY STONE in shaky letters that is, as far as you can tell, an ordinary stone.

"I've got my own rope," you say. "I'm all right."

"Then in you go." She waves you toward the corridor with her knitting needles. "Left's faster, for what it's worth."

"Faster to what?"

She doesn't answer. She's counting stitches.

You face the corridor. It opens from the arch into fitted stone, the walls furred with bioluminescent lichen — blue-green, steady, useful — that provides enough light to navigate by. The air is cool and a little damp, the kind of underground cool that doesn't threaten but promises. You can hear water somewhere deeper in. Maybe something else. Probably nothing alarming.

Ten paces in, the corridor splits.

To your left: a low archway with noticeably finer stonework, the frame carved in interlocking geometric patterns that speak of someone who cared very much about this particular passage. This is where the Fletcher twins went, seven years ago. They came back three days later with considerably more gold than they'd had before, and both of them have steadfastly refused to discuss it. To this day, Orvyn Fletcher goes a little pale if you mention the word *obligation*.

To your right: a rougher tunnel, wider, the stones less even — a later addition, cut after the original construction. This is where old Bastwick claims he found his lucky mushroom. The one that allegedly let him speak to fish for a week. Bastwick is the sort of man who either tells the truth about strange things or lies about ordinary ones; it's genuinely difficult to say which.

Straight ahead: the main path continues, broad and well-worn, the floor polished smooth by three centuries of feet. This is where everyone goes. The first two chambers are the baker's root cellar, perfectly ordinary, occasionally populated by barrels and sacks of turnips. Beyond that, the dungeon properly begins. At least you'd know what you were getting into, for the first part.

You stand there with your rope and your second-best boots and the full weight of your first actual decision.

From behind you, unhelpfully, Marta starts humming.
