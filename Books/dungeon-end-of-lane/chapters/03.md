The forward passage descends. Not precipitously — no treacherous slopes, no suspicious staircases, nothing that would make your second-best boots regret their life choices. Just a gentle grade, the kind that tells you you're going somewhere without making a fuss about it. The glowmoss thins along the first stretch and then comes back in enthusiastic, uneven patches, as though it can't quite commit to a position on lighting.

You are not humming.

This is something you become aware of about forty paces in: the active, conscious, effortful quality of not humming. You are not, by nature, a person who hums constantly. But as soon as the notice board instructed you not to, something in the back of your brain evidently decided that humming was the single most appealing activity imaginable. There is a tune — something your aunt used to sing while gardening, a cheerful bouncing thing in three-four time — that has been circling the perimeter of your thoughts since the antechamber. You are managing it. You are breathing through your nose in a deliberate and comprehensively tuneless fashion, and this is fine, and you are fine, and everything is proceeding exactly as planned.

The passage levels out into a long gallery. The ceiling rises here, the glowmoss spreading itself generously across the vault above, and the floor is smooth pale stone. Your footsteps — second-best boots doing their job admirably — produce a small, tidy echo.

And then the echo echoes.

You stop. You listen.

The echo of your footstep echoes again. And once more. Each repetition is slightly softer, slightly slower, and — you tilt your head — very slightly more to the left than any reasonable wall would account for.

You take one careful step.

The gallery produces: one footstep, followed by four echoes, each originating from a different point in the middle distance, none of them from the direction of a wall.

The Echoes, it turns out, are not a phenomenon. They are residents.

You can see them, if "see" is the right word for something you perceive mostly as a shimmer — a displacement in the air, a shape like a soap bubble that has been taking architecture classes. There are perhaps a dozen of them, hovering at various heights across the gallery, each one roughly the size of a large cat and roughly the shape of nothing in particular. They are regarding you with what you interpret as interest. You are interpreting interest generously.

One of them drifts a little closer.

You say nothing. You are being supremely sensible. The nothing you're saying has weight and discipline and a very firm policy on sound. You hold it for a full ten seconds, which is longer than it sounds.

"Right then," you say, because apparently that was always going to happen.

The gallery fills with it. *Right then. Right then. Right then.* Twelve soft repetitions, overlapping like ripples in a pond, each one a half-beat behind the last, each one — and this is the part you find hardest to account for — sounding fractionally more satisfied than the one before. The nearest Echo brightens, faintly and warmly, in the manner of something that has received exactly what it wanted. Two more drift over from the far end of the gallery with the unhurried purpose of creatures who knew this was coming.

You close your eyes for a moment.

When you open them, the Echoes have arranged themselves in a loose, companionable semicircle in front of you, and one of them is playing back the sound of your very first footstep in the gallery, doing so with an air of quiet pride. It sounds, for all the world, like a musician running through a favourite passage.

This is fine. You are being followed by several luminous beings made entirely of recycled sound, and it is fine. The notice said not to feed them, and you have fed them "right then," which is not a great deal of content. They seem content anyway. Content is good. Nobody on the notice board specified that content Echoes were the dangerous kind.

You resume walking. So do they, in their drifting way, producing a soft constellation of footsteps and *right thens* that trails behind you like the world's most ambient travelling companions.

The gallery stretches on toward a door — proper, solid, iron-banded oak, the kind of door that says *something on the other side of me was worth protecting*. Beside it, scratched into the stone at eye level, is what appears to be a brief note in the same small, tidy handwriting as the notice board. You'll need to get closer to read it.

To your left, midway down the gallery, a narrow alcove is set into the wall. You didn't notice it on the way in — the glowmoss is thinner there, and something on the low shelf inside it isn't quite catching the light the way stone would. The Echoes haven't paid it any attention, which might mean something or might mean they have different priorities.

One of them is now softly, reverently repeating the sound of your deliberate nose-breathing from earlier. You cannot decide if this is endearing or catastrophic. Probably it is both.

You are, all things considered, still having quite a good morning.
