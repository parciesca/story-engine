The registry clerk was a man named Purvis who had been in Helsinki since before Elias and who treated all requests for historical files with the same mild suspicion he treated everything, which was to say: he wrote the request number in a ledger, disappeared into the back room for a variable length of time, and returned without explanation or apology. He did not ask what you wanted files for. This was the professional courtesy of the registry: incuriosity as a service.

Elias waited on the wooden chair by the door and read a trade bulletin until Purvis came back with a folder that was thinner than he'd hoped.

"Oslo, sixty-one, sixty-two," Purvis said, as if announcing it to a court. "Cable traffic's been moved to the central archive. This is what we held here."

"Thank you."

Purvis returned to his desk and resumed whatever he'd been doing, which gave every appearance of not being reading.

Elias opened the folder.

There were four documents. Two were signal intercepts — fragmentary, partial transcriptions of what appeared to be commercial communications. One read like a shopping list. The other referenced a meeting in Drammen that had never been followed up. The third document was a surveillance photograph: a man in a wool coat on what appeared to be a wet Oslo street, his face turned three-quarters away from the camera. Elias looked at it for some time. He could not have identified anyone from this photograph. He doubted anyone could.

The fourth document was two pages.

The first page was a report from the Oslo station chief — a man named Carrington, whom Elias remembered vaguely from a conference in the mid-sixties, steady and careful — dated November 14, 1962. The report described the Shevelev case in the flat language of operational assessment: suspected asset of the GRU, commercial cover as a trade liaison, circumstantial evidence of contact with a foreign ministry source. Carrington recommended continued surveillance for a period of no less than six months, with a secondary request for London to cross-reference Shevelev against known GRU personnel files.

The second page was stapled to the first.

It was a case closure, dated December 3, 1962 — less than three weeks after Carrington's recommendation. The language was administrative and final: *insufficient operational basis for continued resource allocation, case closed pending review.* It had not been issued from Oslo. It bore a London routing code.

At the bottom, a signature: *J. Hartley, D/Operations.*

Elias looked at the name.

He read it twice. Then he closed the folder and sat for a moment with his hands flat on the cover, thinking about nothing in particular, which was what he did when he was thinking about something very specific.

D/Operations in 1962. He tried to place Hartley. The name had the quality of a face glimpsed in a group photograph — present, unremarkable, never the focus. He could not locate a career, a face, a voice. But the routing code was real, and the closure was real, and Oslo station had been three weeks from receiving authorisation to continue when someone in London had decided they shouldn't.

He thanked Purvis and left.

His flat was on Vironkatu, ten minutes from the embassy on foot. He had furnished it in the way of a man who did not expect to stay long and had been there four years: books, a radio, a decent chair. A kitchen that he used more than most people assumed. He made himself a meal he did not particularly taste and sat afterward at the table with the envelope in front of him.

He had been carrying it for three days.

He opened it.

One sheet of paper. Gromov's handwriting — careful, slightly cramped, the handwriting of a man more accustomed to typing. A name, a title, a brief summary that took up seven lines.

*Paavo Lindqvist. Senior Adviser, CSCE Preparatory Secretariat. Ministry of Foreign Affairs, Helsinki. Recruited Oslo, 1961. Handler: K. Shevelev. Active.*

Elias set the paper down.

Lindqvist. He did not know the man — not personally, not by reputation — but he knew the posting. A senior Nordic liaison rotation, Oslo to Helsinki to Stockholm, the kind of track that produced career diplomats who spoke four languages and sat on the right committees. Lindqvist would have been in his early thirties in 1961. Junior enough to be approached. Senior enough to be useful.

Shevelev, under commercial cover, would have had legitimate access to exactly that world. Trade delegations, cultural attaché events, the particular ecosystem of Nordic multilateral meetings where everyone knew everyone and a Soviet trade official with a friendly manner and good German was simply part of the furniture.

Oslo, 1961. The asset the case never identified.

Elias sat with the paper in his hands and felt the particular cold clarity of a problem that had stopped being abstract. The mole was real — if Gromov was real. The handler was real — Seija's signals confirmed it. And the case that might have uncovered the connection had been closed from London, by a man named Hartley, three weeks after the Oslo station recommended pressing forward.

He thought about what D/Operations in 1962 would look like in 1972. Retired, possibly. Dead, possibly. Or promoted, laterally or otherwise, into one of the positions that persisted regardless of administration because institutions needed people who remembered things.

He thought about Pamela Secker, who was his controller in London, and who had been in the service since the late fifties.

He did not finish the thought.

He folded the paper and placed it in the inside pocket of his jacket, against his chest, and sat for a long time in the quiet of the flat while the heating ticked and the city outside continued its patient, fogbound October.

He had not told London. He had not told Seija. He was sitting alone in a flat in Helsinki with a name that connected a walk-in to a GRU handler to a closed case to a signature he couldn't quite place, and no version of the next step felt clean.

Outside, it began to rain.
