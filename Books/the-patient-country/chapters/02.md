Seija Mäkinen's office was on the fourth floor of a building that officially housed a division of the Interior Ministry, and she had arranged it the way she arranged everything: spare, functional, nothing on the desk that didn't need to be there. The single window looked out over a courtyard where pigeons convened with bureaucratic persistence on a concrete ledge.

She was reading when Elias arrived, and she did not look up immediately. This was not discourtesy. It was the Finnish way of saying: you can wait, I'm nearly done, your time matters but so does finishing a thought. Elias had learned, years ago, not to interpret it as a slight.

"Elias." She set the document face-down on the desk. "Sit."

He sat. She poured coffee from a thermos without asking and slid a cup across to him. The coffee was strong enough to reconsider.

"You look like a man who has slept on a question," she said.

"I have a professional query," he said. "Background. Nothing operational."

Her expression did not change. In the three years they had been each other's liaison, Elias had never seen her expression change in a way she hadn't decided on in advance. "Of course," she said.

"Viktor Gromov. Soviet Trade Representation, six years here. What do you know about him?"

The pause was brief — two seconds, perhaps — and it was the kind of pause that contained information. Seija reached for her own cup. She drank. She set it down with a slight precision that struck him as deliberate.

"Why Gromov?"

"His name has come up in a context I'm trying to assess." Elias kept his voice flat. "I'm not asking you to share anything sensitive. I'm asking whether there's a texture to him. Whether SUPO has a view."

She looked at him the way she sometimes looked at the courtyard pigeons — not unkindly, but with the calm appraisal of someone who had stopped being surprised by the behaviour of unremarkable creatures.

"Gromov is not on our primary list," she said. "He's not KGB, officially. He works the trade side genuinely — he's useful to both governments, which makes him useful to himself. He has a reputation for being careful."

"Careful how?"

"He doesn't drink at official events. He doesn't pursue Finnish contacts socially. He attends what he must attend and he leaves." She turned her cup in her hands. "There was an incident eighteen months ago. A Finnish journalist approached him at a reception — probably fishing, probably nothing. Gromov reported the approach to the Trade Ministry himself, which was unusual. People who report their own incidental contacts are either very clean or very careful."

"Or very instructed," Elias said.

"Or that." She looked at him steadily. "Elias. You said background. You said nothing operational. I'm going to say something now, and I want you to understand I'm saying it as a colleague, not as a representative of a service."

He waited.

"The CSCE talks begin in three months. For us — for Finland — this is the most consequential diplomatic moment in twenty years. We are the host. We are the neutral party. That is not a formality. That is a position we have maintained at significant cost, and we intend to maintain it through these talks and whatever comes after." She met his eyes. "If there are things happening in Helsinki right now — things that involve the intelligence services of third parties — we would want to know about them. Not to interfere. To manage our exposure."

Elias heard this clearly. He also heard what she hadn't said: *tell me what Gromov said to you.*

"I understand the position," he said.

"I'm glad." She turned over the document she'd been reading and glanced at it briefly, as if to locate herself. "There's one other thing. It may be nothing."

"Tell me."

"A name has been appearing in our signals — background noise, mostly. A Soviet officer. Not Gromov. Someone further back, less visible. We picked it up first in August, and then again last week in a different context. We haven't been able to place him in the Helsinki residency officially. We're not sure what he's doing here."

"The name?"

"Shevelev. Konstantin Shevelev. Do you know it?"

Elias kept his hands still on the table. He took a breath the same way he always took a breath.

"I don't think so," he said.

He was lying, but only partly. He knew the name the way you knew a word you'd last seen in an old letter — from the edges, from association. It attached itself to a time and a place and the particular quality of anxiety that came with both.

Oslo. Eleven years ago, or twelve. The name had appeared in a file he'd been asked to assess. A GRU officer operating under commercial cover, reported to be running an asset in the Norwegian foreign ministry. The asset had never been identified. The case had been closed, inconclusive, with that flat bureaucratic finality that sometimes meant shelved and sometimes meant buried.

He had not thought of Shevelev since.

"I'll check our records," he said. "If anything surfaces on our end, I'll flag it to you."

"Thank you." She held his gaze a moment longer than necessary. It was not a question. It was the Finnish way of saying: I know you know more than you're telling me, and I am choosing to extend credit anyway.

He stood. She did not stand.

"Elias." He had his hand on the door. "The weather in October. You know what it does here."

"I do."

"The fog comes in off the water and it stays. People who don't know Helsinki think it will lift." She picked up her document again. "It doesn't lift. You stop waiting for it to lift, and then you can see."

He let himself out into the corridor and stood for a moment before walking to the stairs.

Shevelev. Here. Now.

The fog, as it happened, was already quite thick.
