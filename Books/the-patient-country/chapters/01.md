The café had sixteen tables. Elias Vane counted them from the doorway, as he counted everything, without thinking about it particularly.

He chose a seat against the far wall with sight lines to the entrance, the kitchen pass, and a partial view of the alley through a window that needed cleaning. The waitress brought coffee without his asking — he'd been coming here long enough that she knew — and he settled into the particular stillness that was his professional gift and his social liability.

Outside, the South Harbour was the colour of pewter under an October sky. Container ships moved with the deliberate patience of institutions. Helsinki was doing what it did in autumn: contracting, darkening, pulling its collar up. There was something Finnish in that, he thought. The capacity to simply endure.

The man arrived twelve minutes late.

He was neither conspicuously nervous nor conspicuously calm, which was itself a data point. Gromov — if the dead-letter contact had been Gromov, as the protocol suggested — was around fifty, with the compact build of someone who had been athletic once and had let it settle into something denser. He wore a grey suit that was Soviet in cut but not offensively so. Trade Representation, per their file on him. Six years in Helsinki, two prior postings, wife and daughter back in Leningrad.

He sat across from Elias without preamble.

"You are the one who speaks Finnish," Gromov said. His English carried the accent of someone who had learned it carefully, from a recording.

"One of them." Elias gestured toward the menu. "There's an excellent pastry."

Gromov looked at the menu without seeing it. Then he reached into his jacket and produced an envelope, which he laid on the table between them as if setting down a chess piece.

Elias did not touch it.

"Tell me," Elias said.

What followed was fifteen minutes of careful language. Gromov spoke in the abstract at first — there was a thing he had come to understand, a situation that had developed over many years, an arrangement that troubled him. He did not call himself a patriot. He did not reach for any of the usual frameworks. This too was a data point.

The substance, when it arrived, was this: there was a Soviet asset inside the Finnish foreign ministry. Not a recent recruitment — Gromov stressed this, the word fifteen surfacing three times with the weight of a confession. Fifteen years. The asset had survived multiple internal Finnish security reviews. Their output had shaped Finnish positions at two prior multilateral negotiations. With the CSCE preliminary talks approaching, with Helsinki about to become the crucible of every intelligence service in Europe, the asset was active and ready.

Elias listened. He asked questions by not asking them — a technique that involved very slight adjustments in posture, a fractional tilt of the head, the kind of silence that people rushed to fill. Gromov filled it.

The document in the envelope, Gromov said, was a summary. Not proof — he used this word carefully, proof, as if he'd rehearsed distinguishing it from what he was offering. A summary of what he knew, from the position he occupied. He was not in the Line KR; he was not counterintelligence. But he had seen things adjacent. Heard. Understood implications.

"Why now," Elias said. Not a question, exactly.

Gromov looked at his hands. "My daughter is seventeen."

Elias absorbed this. He did not doubt that the daughter was real — the file confirmed her — but he doubted the emotion, not because Gromov was incapable of love, but because the statement had the quality of a rehearsed truth. True things, when rehearsed enough, took on a different texture.

"There is a procedural matter," Elias said. "I'm going to ask you something, and I'd like you to answer without considering whether you should."

Gromov looked up.

"The way you made contact with us," Elias said. "The protocol. Where did you learn it?"

The pause that followed was three seconds long. Elias counted.

"An old friend," Gromov said. "From Leningrad. He had worked, in another time, in a related capacity."

"And your old friend is still in a related capacity?"

"He is dead."

Elias nodded slowly. He reached forward and placed the envelope in his jacket pocket without opening it. "Thank you for coming," he said. "We'll be in touch through the same channel. Don't initiate contact by any other means."

They did not shake hands.

Elias paid for both coffees. He left first, took a route along the harbour front toward Esplanadi, then cut north through the market. The birch trees had gone yellow in the park. A woman on a bench was reading a newspaper. As Elias passed, she turned a page — cover to front, against the natural direction. The motion was practiced. Invisible, if you didn't know what you were looking at.

He walked on without altering his pace.

The protocol Gromov had used was internal. Station-level, distributed to Helsinki, Stockholm, and Oslo on a limited distribution in June. Which meant Gromov's dead friend — if there was a dead friend — had access to SIS traffic.

Or the dead friend didn't exist, and Gromov had that access himself.

Or Gromov was carrying a document designed by someone else, delivered to Elias by a man who didn't know what he was carrying.

Elias reached his car on Unioninkatu and sat for a moment without starting it, watching the rearview mirror.

The envelope in his pocket contained a name. He knew this because Gromov had said, near the end, almost as an afterthought: *there is a name in there, a name you will recognize*.

That was the detail that worried him most.

In Elias's experience, intelligence that came packaged with personal familiarity — with the promise that it would resonate — was very often not intelligence at all.

He started the car and drove north toward the embassy.
