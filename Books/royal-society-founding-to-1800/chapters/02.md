A chartered club is still only a club. It has a seal, a name, and a room — and it has a problem. The twelve men of 28 November 1660 were not, by and large, people with time on their hands. Boyle had his own laboratory. Wren was being pulled toward the buildings that would consume the rest of his life. Wilkins was soon to be made a bishop. Brouncker was a courtier with court business. What the Royal Society actually needed, in 1662, were the people nobody else was going to remember: a workhorse to run the meetings and a workhorse to run the mail.

It found both of them within a year.

The mail was the easier problem, because Henry Oldenburg had been doing the job already.

Oldenburg was from Bremen. He had come to London originally as a diplomat for his city, staying on as tutor to one of Robert Boyle's nephews, which meant that by the late 1650s he was a fixture of the Boyle household and knew everyone Boyle knew. He spoke five or six languages depending on how one counted, kept up a voluminous private correspondence with scholars in Paris, Leiden, Danzig, Florence, Utrecht, and was the kind of man who filed things. When the Society was chartered, he was appointed Secretary alongside Wilkins, with particular charge of foreign correspondence. The charge was not really an addition to his life; it was a formalisation of it.

What came out of his rooms over the next fifteen years is difficult to summarise because the scale is absurd. Over three thousand of his letters survive, and those are only the ones that did not burn. He wrote in Latin, French, Italian, German, and English — sometimes all five in an afternoon — and he maintained, in effect, a single running conversation among the natural philosophers of Europe with himself as the switchboard. A Dutchman with a new lens-grinding technique wrote to Oldenburg. Oldenburg copied the interesting parts to Hooke in London, to Hevelius in Danzig, to Auzout in Paris. The responses came back to Oldenburg. The interesting parts went out again. It was the kind of labor that tends to break the person doing it, and by 1664 it was visibly breaking him.

His solution, in March 1665, was to print the news instead of re-copying it. The first number of *Philosophical Transactions: Giving Some Accompt of the Present Undertakings, Studies, and Labours of the Ingenious in Many Considerable Parts of the World* appeared on 6 March of that year. It was a thin pamphlet, sixteen pages, and Oldenburg meant it as a private venture — the Society was not the publisher, and the profits (such as they were) were his. The confession he offered, years later, was that he had simply got tired of writing the same paragraph six times in six languages. The Society did not formally take ownership of the journal until after his death in 1677.

Almost in passing, while solving his own workload problem, Oldenburg invented two things that are still around. The first is the scientific journal. The second is peer review: when a submission arrived that he could not judge himself, he sent it to someone who could, and asked whether it was worth printing. He did not call it peer review. He called it getting a second opinion. It became the protocol by which the new natural philosophy would decide what was true.

The meetings themselves were a different problem, and they had a different solver.

Robert Hooke, by 1662, was twenty-seven years old and had been orbiting Boyle's laboratory since his undergraduate days at Christ Church. He had built Boyle's air-pump — possibly the single most consequential instrument of the century — and understood it better than Boyle did. He was brilliant, chronically underslept, and chronically underpaid, and those facts about him never changed. When the Society was chartered, he was hired as Curator of Experiments. The contract was specific and brutal: Hooke was to produce three or four significant experimental demonstrations at every weekly meeting.

Three or four, every week, for years.

What that meant in practice is that Hooke effectively carried the intellectual content of the Society on his back through its first decade. A later Librarian of the Royal Society put it plainly: without Hooke's weekly experiments, the Society could scarcely have survived. The minute books record the range — pneumatic trials with Boyle's engine, the weights of air at different altitudes, the behaviour of capillary tubes, the dissection of a dog's lungs, the burning of diamonds, magnets, burning-glasses, the drawing of a fly's eye at a hundred diameters. Fellows would arrive, take their seats, and Hooke would show them something they had never seen.

In 1665, in the same season that Oldenburg's first *Philosophical Transactions* came off the press, Hooke published *Micrographia*. It was a folio book of engraved plates drawn from his microscope: a flea rendered at a scale that covered two foolscap pages and looked like an armored knight; the compound eye of a drone-fly; the point of a needle; the edge of a razor, shown to be a ragged mountain range; the structure of cork, whose tiny empty chambers he named *cells*, because they reminded him of the cells in a monastery. It was the first bestseller of the new natural philosophy. Samuel Pepys, who was on the Society's rolls more as an enthusiast than a contributor, bought a copy and recorded in his diary that he sat up reading it until two in the morning — "the most ingenious book that ever I read in my life."

Between the two of them, Oldenburg at his desk and Hooke at the demonstration table, the Society of 1665 had become something its founders had gestured at but not quite built: a place where facts came in from everywhere, got tested in front of witnesses, and went out again to everyone. It was not yet a powerful institution. It was something more useful. It was a working one.
