CHAPTER PROPOSAL
  Chapter: 1
  Topic: The late Qing dynasty as a living system — who governed, who obeyed, who starved — and the early fractures that the 1895 Japan defeat would crack wide open
  Time Period: Qing dynasty broadly (1644-1912), with focus on the 1870s-1895 period
  Follows from: New exploration brief — user wants China's 20th century approached through the population's lens, starting from "the last great dynasty or whatever"
  This chapter covers: Introduces the Qing dynasty as the final chapter of imperial China — a Manchu minority ruling a Han majority through a system of Confucian bureaucracy, examination culture, and claimed divine mandate. Grounds the reader in what ordinary life looked like across the social spectrum, then shows the dynasty's world beginning to crack under population pressure, internal rebellion, and Western humiliation — building toward the Japan shock of 1895 as the chapter's closing revelation.
  Key elements:
    1. Who the Qing were — Manchu outsiders who conquered China and ruled 268 years; the underlying ethnic tension
    2. The Mandate of Heaven — China's political theory that both granted and could withdraw legitimacy from emperors
    3. The examination system as social architecture — how it organized ambition, class, and governance
    4. Life across the social spectrum — peasant reality (85% of population), gentry intermediaries, the court
    5. The population crisis — land fragmentation, famine cycles, the Taiping Rebellion's death toll
    6. The first Western humiliations — Opium Wars, unequal treaties — and what they meant to Chinese self-understanding
    7. The 1895 defeat by Japan as chapter closer — the civilizational shock that changes everything
  Figures featured: Empress Dowager Cixi (mentioned), Guangxu Emperor (brief mention). Mostly systemic — the chapter is about structures more than individuals.
  Research note: Population figures and land data verified (Columbia Asia for Educators). Treaty of Shimonoseki terms confirmed. Taiping death toll is estimated 20-30 million — note the range.
  Approach: Sweeping overview with grounding in specific human detail — peasant life, the exam candidate, the court. Opens on a vivid scene (the examination grounds), pulls back to show the full system, narrows to the cracks.

CHAPTER HANDOFF
  Written: 2026-04-09

  A) The Boxer Rebellion — Three years after the Japan defeat, peasants in Shandong, driven by drought, flood, and economic ruin, formed secret martial arts societies and began killing missionaries and Chinese converts. The Qing court, split and desperate, sided with them. Eight foreign nations marched on Beijing.

  B) The Hundred Days' Reform — The Japan shock galvanized a reformist faction at court. In 1898, the young Guangxu Emperor backed sweeping proposals to remake China's government, education, and military along Western lines. Empress Dowager Cixi crushed it in 103 days.

  C) Life inside the examination system — The scholar-gentry world was the nervous system of imperial China. What did it actually feel like to spend decades chasing a degree? And what happened to all those men who didn't pass?

  D) The Century of Humiliation in daily life — How did ordinary Chinese actually experience the treaty ports, the foreign concessions, the extraterritoriality? What did it mean to live in a city where foreigners answered to their own courts?
