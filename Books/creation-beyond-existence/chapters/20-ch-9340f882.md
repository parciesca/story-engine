The network grew.

Not quickly. Nothing the *enne* did happened quickly, and the Kesh-an, who had spent four million years developing in isolation, had their own deeply ingrained patience. But over the centuries following the establishment of the bilateral link, other voices joined.

Region 2067-Leth. A civilization that had received an *enne* intervention roughly two million years prior, survived, and spent the intervening time working through the curriculum with a focus on field topology that neither the *enne* nor the Kesh-an had pursued. They came online with a communication capability similar to the Kesh-an's resonance channel — independently derived, architecturally distinct, functionally equivalent. They called themselves something the linguists spent decades translating and ultimately rendered as *the Leth-enne*, "the ones who almost didn't."

Region 884-Taur. Younger — their intervention had been only eight hundred thousand years ago — but prodigious. They had advanced through the curriculum at a pace that startled the Commission, developing substrate theory with a reckless creativity that the *enne* found both exhilarating and faintly alarming. Their self-referent translated roughly as "the grateful."

Region 5511-Ohn. The oldest survivor besides the *enne* themselves. Three and a half million years since intervention. Quiet, methodical, deeply philosophical. They had spent most of their post-intervention history not studying the curriculum's physics but its *pedagogy* — analyzing why it was structured the way it was, what assumptions it made about the nature of intelligence, what it revealed about the mind that had designed it. Their contributions to the network were less technical than conceptual, but the other survivors found their perspective indispensable. They called themselves, simply, "the students."

Five civilizations. Five universes. Five species that had looked at the threshold and stepped back, connected through a web of directed channels threaded through Fragment 7's extraction field. Each one different. Each one shaped by its own history, its own path through the curriculum, its own way of being intelligent. And each one contributing something the others lacked.

The Kesh-an brought resonance engineering. The Leth-enne brought topology. The Taur brought raw theoretical ambition. The Ohn brought philosophical depth. And the *enne* brought what they had always brought: the detection grid, the intervention infrastructure, the institutional patience of a species that had been doing this longer than anyone except Fragment 7 itself.

Together, they formed something that had no precedent in the history of the multiverse. A collaborative. A partnership of survivors, working across the boundaries of separate realities, unified by a shared curriculum and a shared purpose and the shared knowledge that they existed because someone — something — had reached across the void and taught them how not to die.

And together, inevitably, they turned their attention to the teacher.

---

The proposal to contact Fragment 7 directly — not the broadcast perturbation that had triggered the curriculum dump millions of years ago, but targeted, directed, conversational contact using the network's refined communication capabilities — emerged from the Kesh-an, who had been thinking about it since before they joined the network.

Rü, the Kesh-an liaison embedded with the *enne* Commission, presented the case with the careful diplomacy that characterized all inter-civilizational exchanges.

"We have the resonance channel technology. The *enne* have the detection grid that can locate Fragment 7 with precision. The Leth-enne have mapped the field topology between our region and Fragment 7's position. We can, for the first time, direct a structured communication to Fragment 7 specifically — not a perturbation broadcast, but a targeted transmission. A conversation."

Aven, the Commission's current First Chair, raised the question that everyone was thinking. "Fragment 7 responded to our original perturbation in eleven minutes. It broadcast the full curriculum across its entire network. It sent a single word on a separate channel: *Finally.* And then it went quiet. In the millions of years since, it has continued its standard operations — the extraction field, the genesis-event curriculum broadcasts into newborn universes — but it has not initiated further contact with us. What makes us think a directed communication will produce a different result?"

"We don't know that it will," Rü said. "But the original perturbation was a knock on the pipe. We announced our existence. Fragment 7 responded by giving us everything it had — the full curriculum, pushed out indiscriminately. That response was consistent with an automated protocol. A trigger condition being met. 'If someone knocks, open the floodgates.' What we're proposing now is qualitatively different. A directed, structured communication using the curriculum's advanced vocabulary, carrying specific content — our history, our network, our capabilities, our questions. Not a knock. A letter."

The Commission debated. The Consul was consulted. The Matrix was reviewed. The network's other members weighed in through their respective channels, each bringing their own perspective to the question.

The Ohn — the philosophical civilization, the ones who had spent millions of years studying the curriculum's design rather than its content — offered the observation that tipped the balance.

"We have analyzed the curriculum's pedagogical architecture extensively," their representative communicated through the translation framework. "The design reveals a teaching philosophy that is comprehensive, patient, and deeply considered. But it also reveals something about the designer. The curriculum is *complete*. It does not leave room for revision. It does not include mechanisms for feedback or adaptation based on the student's response. It is a fixed body of knowledge, structured for delivery, not dialogue. This is not the hallmark of an active teacher. It is the hallmark of a teaching left behind."

The network absorbed this.

"You're suggesting Fragment 7 isn't inhabited," Aven said.

"We're suggesting the curriculum was designed to outlast its creator. The extraction field. The broadcast mechanism. The dormant hotline that activated when the *enne* first knocked. These are infrastructure. Engineered to persist. Engineered to function without oversight, without maintenance, without a living intelligence guiding them. The civilization that built Fragment 7's systems may have mastered the threshold. But mastery of substrate physics does not imply immortality. They may have lived for a very long time. They may have lived for billions of years. But the systems they left behind have been running for longer than that, and the absence of adaptive behavior — the absence of any response more sophisticated than a pre-loaded curriculum dump — suggests that the systems are running alone."

Silence across the network. Five civilizations, connected through the void, contemplating the possibility that the teacher they had all been shaped by, the ancient intelligence they had revered and studied and built their entire collaborative around, was not an intelligence at all. Not anymore. Just the machinery of one, still humming, still running, still faithfully executing its mission long after the minds that designed it had gone wherever minds go when they're done.

---

The directed transmission was sent anyway. The network agreed unanimously: even if the Ohn were right, the attempt had to be made. You didn't assume an empty house without knocking on the door.

They knocked. A comprehensive transmission, carrying the combined knowledge and questions of five civilizations, directed along a precision channel to Fragment 7's exact position in the void. Not a perturbation. Not a broadcast. A letter, written in the most advanced vocabulary the network had collectively developed, asking: *Who are you? Are you there? We are your students, and we would like to meet our teacher.*

The response came in nine minutes. Faster than the original perturbation response, because the directed channel was more efficient than the crude pipe-knock the *enne* had used millions of years ago.

It was the curriculum. Again. The full curriculum, pushed out along the directed channel and simultaneously broadcast across the entire extraction field, exactly as it had been the first time. The same data. The same structure. The same layers, from the mathematical handshake to the deepest substrate theory.

And on the separate channel, in the simple vocabulary of the outer layers: *Finally.*

The same word. The same response. Triggered by the same condition: someone knocking.

Aven read the data in the Commission's workspace. Rü stood beside them, the Kesh-an liaison's translated emotional indicators registering something the *enne* linguists interpreted as a complex blend of sorrow and recognition.

"It's a recording," Rü said. "The most sophisticated, most consequential recording in the history of the multiverse. But a recording. The voice that said *finally* said it billions of years ago, and it says it every time someone picks up the receiver, because that's what it was designed to say."

"The Ohn were right," Aven said.

"The Ohn were right."

Fragment 7 was a monument. The greatest monument any civilization had ever built — a functioning, self-sustaining infrastructure of extraction and transmission and teaching that had outlasted its creators by an epoch, faithfully carrying out a mission designed by minds that had long since ceased to exist. It was a lighthouse, still burning, built by hands that had crumbled to dust so long ago that the dust itself had dispersed into the void.

The network mourned. Each civilization in its own way, according to its own traditions. The *enne* added a notation to the Matrix. The Kesh-an composed something that their culture considered a remembrance. The Ohn, who had suspected this longest, offered a reflection that the translators rendered as: *The greatest teachers are the ones whose lessons outlive them. Fragment 7 achieved what no other civilization in the multiverse achieved — not just survival, but legacy. They are gone. Their teaching remains. And we are the proof that it worked.*

---

It was Parre who found the final layers.

The deep curriculum researcher had been part of the joint *enne*-Kesh-an team working through the curriculum's most advanced material for three centuries. The work was slow — not because the team lacked capability, but because the curriculum demanded it. Each layer built on the last. Each concept required the foundation beneath it. Fragment 7 had designed the teaching to be learned at the pace understanding required, and no faster. You could not skip ahead because skipping ahead was how civilizations died.

The network's combined efforts had pushed deeper into the curriculum than any individual civilization could have managed alone. The Kesh-an's resonance theory unlocked layers that the *enne*'s detection-focused approach had stalled on. The Taur's theoretical ambition cracked open mathematical structures that the more cautious Ohn had circled for centuries without entering. Each civilization's path through the material illuminated corners that the others had missed.

And at the bottom — not the absolute bottom, Parre suspected there were layers deeper still, layers that might require capabilities the network hadn't yet developed — they found the model.

Not a model of the threshold. Not a model of substrate physics. A model of the void itself.

Fragment 7's creators had mapped the inter-universal medium — the space between realities, the fabric in which universes existed like bubbles in an ocean — with a comprehensiveness that made the Observatory's million-year cartographic effort look like a child's sketch. They had understood its structure, its dynamics, its load-bearing properties. They had understood how universes formed within it, how they grew, how they interacted with it, and how, when a genesis event occurred, the birth of a new universe from the death of an old one affected the medium around it.

And they had understood that the medium was not infinite. Not inexhaustible. Not an eternal, unchanging ocean in which bubbles could form and pop forever without consequence.

The void had structure. The void had limits. And the void was degrading.

Each genesis event — each civilization that crossed the threshold and tore its universe apart, birthing a new one from the wreckage — damaged the medium. Not catastrophically. Not immediately. The way a single crack in a foundation doesn't bring down a building. But the cracks accumulated. Over billions of years, across thousands of genesis events, the inter-universal medium had been slowly, incrementally weakened. Fragmented. The word the Observatory had always used for the remnants of dead universes — *fragments* — was more literal than anyone had known. Each dead universe left behind not just gravitational fossils but structural damage, a thinning of the medium, a reduction in its capacity to sustain the realities that remained.

The model projected forward. Not on a timescale that demanded panic — the void was vast, and the degradation was slow, and the timeline to critical instability was measured in billions of years, perhaps two, perhaps three, perhaps more depending on variables the model acknowledged it couldn't fully constrain. But the trajectory was clear. The medium was weakening. The rate of genesis events, while reduced by the network's interventions, had not stopped entirely. Civilizations still failed. Universes still died. New ones still bloomed from the wreckage. And each event pushed the medium a little closer to a threshold of its own — a point beyond which the fabric between realities could no longer maintain coherent structure, and the boundaries separating universes from each other and from the void would begin to dissolve.

Not an explosion. Not a dramatic collapse. A slow unraveling. Realities bleeding into each other. The void losing its capacity to be void — to be the medium in which existence was suspended. Like ice melting. Like a shelf eroding. Like a foundation, cracked and cracked and cracked again across eons, finally settling past the point of structural integrity.

And then Parre found the part of the model that concerned Fragment 7 itself.

The extraction field. The ancient, vast, beautiful infrastructure that Fragment 7's creators had built to power their civilization and, eventually, to carry their curriculum across the multiverse. It drew vacuum energy from the substrate of every reality it touched. It had been doing so for billions of years. The *enne* had known this since Kaida. They had measured the extraction density in their own universe and found it negligible — a rounding error, functionally zero.

But the model showed what "negligible" meant on a timescale of billions of years.

The extraction field was contributing to the degradation. Not as the primary cause — genesis events were the main driver, each one a structural wound in the medium. But the extraction field was a constant, low-level drain on the substrate of every universe it touched, and over billions of years, that drain added up. It was like a slow leak in a dam that was also being hit by earthquakes. The earthquakes did the visible damage. The leak made each earthquake a little worse than it would have been otherwise.

Fragment 7's creators had known this. The model included it. The curriculum's deepest layers didn't hide the problem — they presented it with the same patient, honest thoroughness that characterized every other layer. *Here is what our extraction does to the medium. Here is the rate of degradation. Here is the projected timeline. Here is what happens if nothing changes.*

And here — in the final layers Parre could access, the deepest material the network's combined capabilities could currently unlock — was the beginning of a framework for what to do about it.

Not a solution. The curriculum didn't contain a solution. It contained the *tools for developing one* — theoretical frameworks, mathematical structures, conceptual foundations that, properly understood and built upon, might allow a sufficiently advanced civilization to modulate the extraction field. Reduce its drain on the medium. Perhaps restructure it entirely, converting it from a constant low-level extraction into something more targeted, more efficient, less damaging to the fabric of the void.

Or perhaps — and this was the possibility that made Parre sit in the dark for a long time after reading the model — shut it down entirely.

Fragment 7's creators had built the extraction field to run forever. They had designed it to outlast them, to carry their curriculum into the deep future, to function without oversight or maintenance or the guiding hand of a living intelligence. And it had done exactly that, faithfully, for longer than any of them could have imagined. But they had also known — the model proved they had known — that the field's perpetual operation came at a cost. A slow, accumulating cost to the medium that sustained every reality in the multiverse.

They had built the lighthouse knowing it was, grain by grain, eroding the cliff it stood on.

And they had included, in the deepest layers of the curriculum, the beginnings of a framework for their students — if any ever survived, if any ever learned enough to reach these layers — to decide what to do about it.

---

Parre presented the findings to the network. All five civilizations. The full model. The degradation. The extraction field's role. The timeline. The framework.

The silence that followed was the longest the network had ever experienced. Five species, spanning five universes, connected through the very infrastructure that was slowly, almost imperceptibly, weakening the ground they all stood on.

Aven spoke first, because the First Chair always spoke first.

"How long?"

"The model's projections carry significant uncertainty at these timescales," Parre said. "But the central estimate is between one and a half and three billion years before the medium reaches critical instability. The rate depends on two variables: the frequency of future genesis events and the continuation of the extraction field at current levels."

"And if we reduce both? If we continue the network's intervention work — preventing genesis events where we can — and modulate or reduce the extraction field?"

"The timeline extends. Significantly. The model suggests that with a ninety percent reduction in genesis events and a restructuring of the extraction field to minimize substrate drain, the medium could remain stable for... the model's language here is imprecise, but the implication is essentially indefinitely. Not forever. But long enough that the distinction becomes philosophical rather than practical."

"And if we shut the field down entirely?" Rü asked.

Parre let the question sit.

"If we shut the field down, we lose the network. The extraction field is the infrastructure through which all of our communication flows. Every directed channel, every resonance link, every connection between every civilization in this collaborative exists within Fragment 7's field. Shutting it down would sever every link simultaneously. We would be isolated. Each of us alone in our own universe, unable to communicate, unable to coordinate, unable to intervene."

"And the curriculum broadcasts into newborn universes?"

"Would stop. No field, no transmission. Every future civilization that approaches the threshold would do so without the teaching. We would be saving the medium by abandoning the mission."

The network was quiet again.

The Ohn spoke. Their representative's words came through the translation framework slowly, weighted with the particular gravity of a civilization that had spent millions of years studying the philosophy of the curriculum rather than its physics.

"Fragment 7's creators knew. They built the field knowing it would contribute to the degradation. They built the curriculum knowing it contained the information their students would need to confront this choice. They did not make the choice for us. They gave us the tools and the knowledge and the framework, and they trusted that whoever survived long enough to reach these layers would be wise enough to decide."

A pause.

"They trusted us with the hardest question: how to preserve the mission without destroying the medium the mission exists to protect. The extraction field is both the instrument of salvation and the source of slow ruin. The curriculum that saves civilizations is carried by a system that weakens the void. The teacher's legacy is also the teacher's burden, and they left it to us because they could not solve it themselves."

Another pause.

"Or because they knew the solution required more than one mind. More than one civilization. More than one path through the material. Perhaps the answer is not in the curriculum at all. Perhaps the answer is in us — in what we build together, using what they taught us, that they could not have built alone."
