The resolution that emerged from the Security Council in the autumn of 2039 was called the Geneva Compact, though it was signed in New York, amended in Beijing, and ratified in a ceremony in Nairobi that nobody remembered correctly afterward. History has a way of sanding down its own turning points, making them seem inevitable in retrospect, and within a generation the Compact had acquired the hazy inevitability of scripture — something that had always been true, rather than something that fifteen frightened delegations had hammered together in a windowless room because a Chilean astrophysicist showed them a map of the dead.

The Compact did three things.

First, it established the Threshold Authority — an international body with jurisdiction over any research program whose energy manipulation capabilities exceeded a defined boundary, colloquially known as the Sarova Line. The line was conservative. Deliberately, almost absurdly conservative, set so far below the estimated danger point that the physicists who drew it compared it to banning campfires because of the theoretical possibility of nuclear weapons. But that was the point. The margin for error, when the error was the annihilation of everything, needed to be embarrassingly wide.

Second, it created the Fragment Observatory — a permanent, globally funded successor to the Coalition, tasked with mapping and monitoring the multiverse. The Observatory's instruments grew over decades, then centuries, from Lena Sarova's original Deep Array into a network of gravitational sensors scattered across the solar system and eventually beyond it. The map of dead realities expanded. The count of confirmed fragments climbed past a thousand, then five thousand, then became a number that most people stopped tracking the way most people stop tracking the number of grains of sand on a beach. The point had been made.

Third — and this was Melo's contribution, the one she fought for until her death in 2081 and which outlasted every other provision of the Compact — it mandated education. Not classification. Not secrecy. Not the hoarding of terrible knowledge behind clearances and need-to-know protocols. Every child born on Earth, and eventually every child born off it, would learn what the fragments were. Would understand, in age-appropriate terms that grew more sophisticated as they did, that the universe they inhabited was one of many, that most of the others had ended, and that the thing which ended them was the same curiosity and capability that made their own civilization possible.

Melo called it the Paradox Curriculum. Critics called it institutionalized fear. But it worked — not because it frightened people into compliance, but because it did something more subtle and more durable. It made the threshold *legible.* It gave humanity a shared framework for understanding a specific category of risk, the way earlier generations had learned to understand the risks of radiation or pandemic or climate disruption. You couldn't prevent someone from reinventing the wheel. But you could make sure they recognized the cliff at the end of the road.

---

The wars didn't stop immediately. Nothing that dramatic. But over the course of the twenty-first and twenty-second centuries, something shifted in the calculus of conflict that was difficult to name and impossible to reverse.

It wasn't that weapons became obsolete. It was that they became *small.* The Sarova Line constrained the upper bound of destructive capability, and the Threshold Authority enforced that constraint with an intrusiveness that would have been unthinkable in any prior era. Nations still quarreled. Factions still fought. But the scale of permissible violence shrank, and kept shrinking, because the shared awareness of what unrestrained capability looked like — the fragments, always the fragments, that silent gallery of annihilated realities — made escalation feel not just dangerous but absurd. Like settling an argument by burning down the building you both lived in. People still punched each other. Nobody reached for the gas can.

By the twenty-third century, the nation-state system had not so much collapsed as evaporated. Not into utopia — humanity remained stubbornly, magnificently incapable of utopia — but into a looser, more networked arrangement of regional authorities, professional guilds, and issue-specific governance bodies, of which the Threshold Authority was the oldest and most respected. Wars, in the traditional sense, stopped. Conflicts didn't. Humanity argued its way across the solar system and eventually into the near interstellar neighborhood, bickering about resources and territory and cultural grievances with the same vigor it always had, but within a framework that kept the bickering non-terminal.

The Threshold Authority endured. It changed shape — grew, contracted, reformed, split, merged, adapted to political realities that Tanabe and Raines couldn't have imagined — but its core mandate remained fixed. Watch the line. Study the fragments. Understand the risk. The Authority became the longest-lived human institution in history, outlasting every government, every religion, every corporation, every cultural movement that arose and faded around it. It persisted because its purpose persisted. The fragments didn't go away. The threshold didn't move. The lesson didn't expire.

---

And through all of it, the Observatory watched.

Generations of researchers — inheritors of Lena and Miguel's work, though the names had long since passed from memory into history into legend into the kind of ambient cultural knowledge that nobody attributes to a specific person — refined the instruments, expanded the network, deepened the map. The multiverse took shape in ever-greater resolution. The fragments told their stories to anyone patient enough to listen: here a reality that had lasted fourteen billion years before its inhabitants crossed the line; there one that had barely reached its first billion; here a cluster of small, young fragments suggesting a pocket of the void where civilizations had arisen and destroyed themselves in rapid succession, like a patch of forest struck by lightning again and again.

The ignition signatures were cataloged, classified, cross-referenced. Each one unique in its specifics but identical in its grammar — the same structured energy cascade, the same self-sustaining reaction, the same terrible flowering of a new universe from the corpse of the old. The details varied. The story never did.

But the Observatory wasn't only looking backward at the dead. It was looking outward, into the living void, waiting.

Because the detection that had started everything — those eleven seconds at Paranal, the birth scream of a new universe — had been a miracle of timing. The mathematics were humbling. The chance of a genesis event occurring within range of human instruments during the brief window of human technological capability was, by any honest calculation, vanishingly small. Like catching a single raindrop in a thimble during a storm that lasted eons. They had witnessed it because they happened to be looking at the right moment, with the right tools, in the right direction. Luck. Stupid, world-altering luck.

Would it happen again?

The Observatory built sensors for the question. Each generation more sensitive than the last, each generation extending the network further, casting a wider and finer net into the void. Listening for the specific gravitational signature of a universe being born — which was also the signature of a civilization dying. Waiting for lightning to strike twice, knowing it might take a thousand years or a million or never.

Centuries passed. The sensors listened. The void was quiet.

Humanity expanded. Settled. Matured in the way that a species matures when it knows, bone-deep, that the thing which makes it powerful is the same thing that could end it. The Paradox Curriculum evolved but never disappeared. Children still learned about the fragments. Researchers still staffed the Observatory. The Threshold Authority still watched the line.

And the sensors listened.

And listened.

And listened.
