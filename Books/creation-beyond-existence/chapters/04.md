The room didn't erupt. Lena had expected it to — fourteen scientists who'd just been told the military was sitting on relevant data should have exploded. Instead they went still, the way animals go still when they sense a predator, and the silence had a quality of focused, dangerous attention that was far worse than shouting.

Raines seemed to understand this. He didn't rush to fill the quiet. He sat with it, his notebook closed on his knee, his pen tucked into his breast pocket, and he waited until the room was ready to listen rather than react.

It was Tanabe who spoke first. She hadn't moved from her chair near the window, but something in her posture had changed — a stiffening, a recalibration. She was no longer managing a research group. She was managing a crisis.

"Arthur," she said. His first name. Deliberate. "How relevant?"

"Directly."

"And you've known this since you arrived."

"I've suspected it since I read Sarova's paper. I've been certain since tonight." He looked at Melo, and something passed between them that Lena couldn't read — an acknowledgment, maybe, that Melo's question had forced his hand in exactly the way she'd intended. "I was sent here to observe and assess. I was not authorized to disclose. I'm choosing to disclose now because Dr. Melo just described the stakes accurately enough that keeping quiet would make me complicit in exactly the kind of blindness she's warning about."

"You could lose your career for this," Voronov said. Not sympathetically. As an observation.

"Yes."

"Then why?"

Raines uncapped his pen, opened his notebook to a blank page, and began to sketch. Simple lines. A diagram. The room leaned forward collectively.

"Eighteen months ago, the Advanced Research Projects Agency initiated a program at Sandia called Project Kindling. Officially, it's an energy research initiative — next-generation power systems, fusion alternatives, the usual language that secures funding without inviting scrutiny." He drew a circle. Then, inside it, a smaller circle. Then a series of radiating lines from the inner circle outward. "Unofficially, it's an attempt to achieve what the researchers call a 'substrate excitation event.' The direct manipulation of vacuum energy at a foundational level."

He paused to let that land. Lena felt the blood leave her hands.

"They're not trying to build a bomb," Raines continued. "I want to be clear about that. The people working on Kindling are serious scientists pursuing what they believe is a clean, limitless energy source. They want to tap the energy that's already there — in the fabric of space itself. The vacuum isn't empty. It's saturated with potential energy. Everyone in this room knows that. What Kindling is trying to do is find a way to release a controlled portion of it."

"Controlled," Lena said. The word came out flat.

"That's the operative assumption, yes." Raines met her eyes. "Eighteen months of work. Three successful micro-scale tests. Energy yields that exceeded predictions by a factor of forty. The project leads are ecstatic. They think they're five to ten years from a prototype reactor that could power a city from a device the size of a filing cabinet."

"And none of them have seen our data," Tanabe said.

"No. Kindling is compartmented. The researchers know what they're building. They don't know what we've found here. They have no context for understanding what a substrate excitation event might look like if it went wrong at scale, because they don't know that it's gone wrong before. Thousands of times. Across thousands of realities."

The diagram sat on the page between them — the innocent little sketch of circles and radiating lines — and Lena stared at it and thought about the eleven seconds of data that had started everything. The bloom. The structured cascade. The birth scream of a new universe, which was also the death scream of whoever had accidentally lit the match.

"How close are they?" she asked.

"To what?"

"To the threshold. To whatever point the process becomes self-sustaining and irreversible."

Raines set down his pen. "That's what I don't know. That's what nobody knows, because nobody working on Kindling is asking that question. They're asking how to make it bigger, more efficient, more stable. They're climbing a mountain in the dark and they don't know there's a cliff on the other side."

Dr. Obi, who had been silent since Raines began, spoke from the far end of the table. Her voice was very quiet.

"The factor of forty."

Raines looked at her.

"The energy yields exceeded predictions by a factor of forty," she repeated. "That's not a rounding error. That's not instrument noise. That means their theoretical model is fundamentally incomplete. They don't understand the mechanism they're triggering. They think they're opening a tap, and they might be pulling a pin."

"Yes," Raines said. "That's why I'm talking to you."

The session broke at one in the morning. Nobody left the building. They scattered into smaller clusters — Voronov and the Beijing theorists commandeering a whiteboard, Obi and Chandrasekaran huddled over a laptop, Miguel already at a terminal pulling up every published paper on vacuum energy manipulation he could find. The cafeteria filled with people who should have been sleeping and couldn't.

Lena found Raines alone in the hallway outside the meeting room, standing by a window that looked out onto the dark Swiss countryside. He was holding a cup of the terrible espresso and appeared to be drinking it without complaint, which Lena took as evidence of either military discipline or total exhaustion.

"Why really?" she asked, leaning against the opposite wall.

He didn't pretend to misunderstand. "Because Melo was right. Intelligence doesn't fail because it's stupid. It fails because it's good at exactly the things that kill it. Curiosity. Drive. The refusal to leave a problem unsolved." He sipped the espresso. Didn't wince. Definitely military discipline. "I've spent thirty years in strategic command. I've seen classified programs in a dozen countries. And the pattern is always the same — brilliant people solving hard problems without anyone in the room asking whether the problem should be solved. Not because they're arrogant, but because the question doesn't occur to them. The mountain is there. They climb."

"And you think Kindling is the mountain."

"I think Kindling is *a* mountain. Right now. In one country. With safeguards and oversight and a budget that Congress reviews annually." He turned from the window. "But Kindling isn't the only program. The Chinese have something similar. The Europeans have theoretical work that's heading in the same direction. It's convergent, Dr. Sarova. Multiple civilizations on one planet, all walking the same path toward the same capability, and not one of them knows what's at the end of it."

Lena thought about the fragments. The hundreds of dead universes, scattered like broken glass across a void that shouldn't exist. Each one home to beings who had presumably been just as smart, just as careful, just as convinced that they understood what they were building.

"You said the factor of forty means their model is incomplete. What if you're wrong? What if the yields are high because the process is just more efficient than they expected?"

Raines almost smiled. "Then I've committed career suicide for nothing and we can all go home. But you don't believe that, and neither do I."

No. She didn't.

"What do you need from us?" she asked.

"I need your fragment data. Specifically, the terminal energy profiles — the ignition signatures from the dead universes. I need your physicists to build a comparison model. If Kindling's substrate excitation events share resonance characteristics with the signatures you've found in the fragments, then we have a direct link. Proof that the technology being developed at Sandia is the same category of technology that's ended civilizations before."

"And then what?"

"Then I take that proof to people who can shut Kindling down. And every other program like it."

"You think they'll listen?"

Raines finished his espresso, set the cup on the windowsill, and looked at her with an expression she hadn't seen from him before — not the careful observer, not the measured military liaison, but something rawer. Something that looked, despite everything, like hope.

"I think they'll have to," he said. "Because the alternative is being the next fragment on someone else's map."
