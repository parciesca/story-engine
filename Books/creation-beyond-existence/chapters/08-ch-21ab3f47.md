The first anomaly appeared three weeks after the genesis detection, and Kaida almost dismissed it.

She was building the detection grid — the system that would, in theory, allow the Observatory to scan for precursor signatures across any reachable region of the void. The work was painstaking. The precursor signal she'd identified in the dead universe had been subtle, a faint rhythmic perturbation buried in six centuries of background data, and calibrating the grid to detect similar signatures at greater distances required her to first characterize every source of noise in the Observatory's sensor network. Instrumental artifacts. Gravitational interference from local stellar bodies. The low murmur of the inter-universal medium itself, the quantum foam that filled the void between realities like static between radio stations.

It was the static that stopped her.

She'd been building a noise profile — a baseline map of what the void "sounded like" in the absence of any signal — and the profile wasn't flat. It should have been. The inter-universal medium, according to every model the Observatory had developed over two millennia, was homogeneous. Featureless. The same everywhere, the way deep ocean water is the same everywhere once you're far enough from shore. Variations existed, but they were random, Gaussian, the kind of smooth probabilistic scatter that nature produced when nothing interesting was happening.

Kaida's noise profile wasn't Gaussian. It had structure.

She ran the analysis again. Same result. A third time, with different filtering parameters. Same result. She called Tomás Herrera, her research partner, who was asleep in his quarters on the far side of the station and answered the comm with the bleary hostility of someone who had been promised that the genesis event was a once-in-a-career occasion and was beginning to suspect he'd been lied to.

"The background isn't background," Kaida told him.

"Kaida, it's the middle of my night cycle."

"The void noise. The baseline we've been subtracting from every observation the Observatory has ever made. It's not random. It has a signature."

Herrera was quiet for long enough that she thought he'd fallen back asleep. Then she heard him sit up.

"What kind of signature?"

"That's why I'm calling you. Because it looks like the precursor profile. The same class of perturbation I found in the dead universe's pre-ignition data. Except it's not coming from one location. It's everywhere. It's the background itself."

They worked for nine days straight before Kaida let herself believe it.

The structure in the background noise was real. It permeated the entire sensor network — every node, every array, every instrument the Observatory had ever built, stretching from the inner solar system to the deep-space platforms at the edge of interstellar space. The same faint, rhythmic perturbation, present in every direction, at every frequency, woven into the fabric of the void like a pulse in living tissue.

It was not random. It was not instrumental. It was not an artifact of their analysis methods, because Herrera spent three of those nine days trying to prove it was, using every statistical tool in the Observatory's considerable arsenal, and failed.

It was a signal. Constant, omnidirectional, and unimaginably old.

On the fourth day, Kaida did something that seemed obvious in retrospect but required a conceptual leap that kept her staring at her display for an hour before she committed to it. She reversed the direction of the analysis. Instead of treating the background perturbation as noise to be characterized and removed, she treated it as a signal to be traced. She asked the grid: where is this coming from?

The answer came back in six hours of computation, and it came back pointing at a single location in the void.

Fragment 7. One of the first sixty-three fragments Lena Sarova's Coalition had mapped twenty-two centuries earlier. Classified under Miguel Estrada's original system as Class One — the oldest category, the most degraded, the remnant of a universe so ancient that its gravitational signature was barely distinguishable from the quantum foam.

Except it wasn't a remnant. It wasn't dead. It wasn't a fossil.

Fragment 7 was the source.

Kaida stared at the directional data and felt the floor shift beneath a certainty she hadn't even known she was standing on. The Observatory had spent two thousand years studying the fragments as archaeological sites — the ruins of civilizations that had destroyed themselves. Fragment 7 had been classified, cataloged, filed alongside thousands of others as the cold remains of a reality that had ended eons before humanity existed.

But the signal was coming from Fragment 7 *now*. Not a residual echo. Not a gravitational fossil. An active, ongoing, structured extraction of energy from the vacuum substrate, radiating outward from Fragment 7 in every direction, reaching across the void, penetrating the boundaries of neighboring realities — including their own.

Including their own.

"Tomás," she said. Her voice was very calm. She noted this distantly, the way you notice the weather during an emergency. "I need you to run the precursor model against the Fragment 7 signal."

"The precursor model is calibrated for pre-ignition signatures. Fragment 7 is Class One. It's—"

"Run it."

He ran it. The model matched. Not at the tentative, early-stage level she'd found in the dead universe's fourteen-year ramp. Not at the crude, experimental level of Kindling or its twenty-first-century equivalents. The model matched at a scale so far beyond the catastrophic threshold — the point where extraction became self-sustaining and tore a universe apart — that the threshold itself disappeared into the lower margin of the data like a foothill viewed from orbit.

Whatever was operating from Fragment 7 was extracting vacuum energy at a rate that exceeded the genesis-event threshold by orders of magnitude. Continuously. Stably. Without cascading into the self-sustaining reaction that had destroyed every other civilization the Observatory had ever studied.

Someone had figured it out.

Not just figured it out — *mastered* it. Taken the same force that had annihilated thousands of realities and learned to ride it the way a surfer rides a wave that could crush them. The extraction wasn't uncontrolled. It wasn't the panicked, ignorant grasping of a civilization that didn't understand what it was touching. It was precise, sustained, and enormous, a feat of engineering so far beyond human capability that comparing it to Kindling was like comparing a supernova to a match.

And it had been running since before humanity existed. The background perturbation that the Observatory had been subtracting from its data as noise for twenty-two centuries — that faint, omnipresent hum in the void that every researcher had assumed was just the natural texture of the inter-universal medium — was the operational signature of Fragment 7's extraction process. It had been there all along. In every dataset. In every observation. In the original eleven seconds of data that Lena Sarova had recorded at Paranal in 2039.

They hadn't detected it because they hadn't known to look for it. And they hadn't known to look for it because it was so constant, so pervasive, so woven into the baseline of reality as they experienced it, that it had been invisible the way gravity is invisible — not hidden, but simply *there*, part of the furniture of existence.

Kaida sat back in her chair. Ganymede hung in the viewport behind her, its scarred surface gray-white and indifferent.

"It's pulling from us," she said.

Herrera looked up from his terminal.

"The extraction field. It's not contained to Fragment 7. It radiates outward. It reaches into neighboring regions of the void, including the space our universe occupies. The background signal we've been measuring — that's their extraction process operating through our substrate. They're pulling vacuum energy from our reality. From us. From everywhere around them."

"How much?"

"At current sensitivity? Negligible. The extraction density in our region is infinitesimal compared to what's happening at the source. Like the gravitational pull of a distant star — technically real, functionally zero." She paused. "At current sensitivity."

"Meaning?"

"Meaning we don't know if it's increasing. We don't know if it's been increasing for billions of years at a rate too slow to measure. We don't know what happens to a universe whose vacuum energy is being slowly siphoned by an external process over cosmological timescales. We don't have a model for that because until ten minutes ago, we didn't know it was possible."

Herrera was quiet. He had a habit, when processing something too large for immediate comprehension, of cleaning his glasses with excessive thoroughness. He cleaned them now, holding them up to the light, finding smudges that almost certainly didn't exist.

"They survived," he said finally.

"Yes."

"The one thing we were told couldn't happen. The core premise of the Threshold Authority, the Paradox Curriculum, the entire framework humanity has built for two thousand years — that crossing the threshold destroys you. That the substrate reaction is inherently uncontrollable. That every civilization that touched it died." He put his glasses back on. "And Fragment 7 is sitting out there running it like a power plant."

"Yes."

"So either our entire understanding of the threshold is wrong, or Fragment 7 figured out something that thousands of other civilizations didn't. Something that we, specifically, chose not to try to figure out because we decided the risk was too great."

Kaida nodded. The weight of it was settling now, layer by layer, each implication heavier than the last.

Two thousand years of restraint. Two thousand years of the Sarova Line, the Threshold Authority, the Paradox Curriculum, the collective human decision to step back from the edge and build a civilization on caution. It had worked. Humanity had survived. But the survival had been predicated on a belief — not just a policy, a *belief*, bone-deep, civilizational — that the threshold was absolute. That you either stayed below it or you died. That there was no middle path, no mastery, no way through.

Fragment 7 was proof that the belief was incomplete.

And the extraction field reaching into their own universe was proof that Fragment 7's mastery, whatever form it took, had consequences for everyone nearby. Consequences that might be negligible now and might not remain so. Consequences that no one had ever thought to measure because no one had imagined there was anything to measure.

"I need to talk to Director Asante," Kaida said. "And Asante needs to talk to the Threshold Authority."

"What are you going to tell them?"

Kaida thought about it. About the careful, principled, hard-won architecture of human survival that she was about to shake to its foundation. About the question that Fragment 7 posed — not a threat, exactly, or not only a threat, but something more destabilizing. A possibility. The possibility that the thing humanity had been right to fear was also the thing that, properly understood, could be the greatest capability any civilization had ever achieved. And that somewhere in the void, someone had understood it while humanity had only flinched from it.

"I'm going to tell them that we're not alone," she said. "That we never were. That something out there has been running the engine we were afraid to start for longer than our species has existed. And that we need to decide — very carefully, very quickly — whether they're a neighbor or a threat. Because their extraction field is in our walls, and we don't know if the dial is turning."
