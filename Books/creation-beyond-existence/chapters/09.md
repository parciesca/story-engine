The meeting took place in Director Asante's private office, a small room with a viewport that faced away from Jupiter and toward the stars. Four people. No recording. No minutes. Asante had disabled the room's ambient logging before Kaida and Herrera arrived, a gesture that carried its own weight — the Observatory logged everything, always, as a matter of institutional religion. Turning it off was the equivalent of closing a church door and locking it.

Kaida presented her findings in twelve minutes. She had distilled nine days of work into a clean sequence: the structured background, the directional trace to Fragment 7, the extraction model, the field that reached into their own universe. She laid it out the way she laid out everything — precisely, completely, without editorializing. The data would speak for itself.

When she finished, Asante didn't respond immediately. She stood at the viewport with her back to the room, hands clasped behind her, watching the stars with the particular focus of someone who was not seeing them at all.

Kofi Asante was sixty-three years old and had directed the Ganymede station for eleven of those years. She was an astrophysicist by training but a historian by temperament — the kind of leader who made decisions not by calculating forward from the present but by looking backward from the consequences. Her office shelves held more history than physics. She had read the original Coalition transcripts. She had read Melo's personal journals, preserved in the Authority's archive. She had read the accounts of the Compact negotiations, the early years of the Paradox Curriculum, the slow, wrenching cultural transformation that had turned a species of ambitious apes into something that could hold a loaded gun and choose, generation after generation, not to pull the trigger.

She understood what that transformation had cost. And she understood how fragile it still was, even after twenty-two centuries.

"Kaida," she said, still facing the viewport. "What would you recommend we do with this?"

"Disclose it. To the Authority first, then to the broader research community. This changes our understanding of—"

"I know what it changes." Asante turned around. Her expression was not unkind, but it had a gravity that made Kaida stop mid-sentence. "Let me tell you what else it changes."

She sat down. She didn't hurry.

"In 2039, a woman in Chile found evidence that the universe was surrounded by the wreckage of dead civilizations, and that humanity was walking the same path that had killed them all. The response to that discovery — the Compact, the Authority, the Curriculum — was not inevitable. It was a choice. A fragile, improbable, nearly impossible choice made by a species that had spent its entire history doing the opposite. Escalating. Competing. Reaching for every capability it could grasp and worrying about the consequences later."

She looked at Kaida steadily. "The choice held. For twenty-two hundred years, it held. Not because humans stopped being curious or ambitious. Because they understood, collectively, that the threshold was absolute. That crossing it meant annihilation. That understanding — that *certainty* — is the foundation of everything we've built. Every institution, every cultural norm, every instinct that keeps a researcher from pushing past the Sarova Line. It all rests on the belief that there is no safe way through."

"But there is," Kaida said. "Fragment 7 proves—"

"Fragment 7 proves that *someone else* found a way through. Someone with capabilities so far beyond ours that comparing their technology to Kindling is meaningless. We don't know how they did it. We don't know how long it took them to learn. We don't know how many of their own civilizations died in the process before one finally succeeded. All we know is that they're operating at a level we cannot replicate, cannot study, and cannot communicate with."

She let that settle.

"Now imagine we announce this. Not to this room. To humanity. To eight billion people spread across three star systems, educated since childhood in the Paradox Curriculum, raised on the foundational certainty that the threshold is death. And we tell them: actually, it's not. Someone figured it out. The thing you were taught to fear above all else — someone mastered it, and they've been running it successfully for longer than your species has existed."

Kaida opened her mouth. Closed it.

"Some will hear caution," Asante continued. "Some will hear what you hear — wonder, possibility, the expansion of knowledge. But others will hear permission. They'll hear that the line we've held for twenty-two centuries was drawn in ignorance. That we've been limiting ourselves unnecessarily. That the greatest power in the multiverse is achievable, and we've been too frightened to reach for it."

"That's not what the data says."

"The data says what you just told me. But data doesn't survive contact with human nature intact. It gets interpreted. Simplified. Weaponized. And the interpretation that will spread fastest — because it always does — is the one that tells people what they want to hear. That the rules don't apply. That the danger was overstated. That it's safe to reach."

The room was quiet. Herrera was cleaning his glasses again. Kaida stared at the floor, not because she disagreed but because she couldn't yet find the words to articulate why Asante was right and why it was unbearable that she was right.

Threshold Consul Vasić, who had been listening from a chair in the corner with the practiced stillness of someone who had spent a career letting other people talk before she decided anything, finally spoke.

"The extraction field," she said. "How long has it been present in our substrate?"

"Based on the signal characteristics, the field predates our universe's current structure," Kaida said. "It's been there since before Earth formed. Before our sun ignited. Possibly since our own Big Bang, or very shortly after."

"And in all that time, it has caused no detectable harm to our reality."

"None that we can currently measure. But—"

"None that we can currently measure." Vasić repeated it without emphasis. A statement of fact, stripped clean. "And we have no means of contacting Fragment 7. No theoretical framework for inter-universal communication. No indication that they're aware of us, or interested in us, or capable of receiving a signal from us."

"Correct."

Vasić nodded. She was a tall, angular woman with the stillness of deep water, appointed to the Consulship not for charisma or political skill but for the quality that the Authority valued above all others: the willingness to make decisions that would be judged across centuries rather than news cycles.

"Then here is what I propose," she said. "Dr. Kaida has identified a previously uncharacterized component of the background signal. This component is constant, omnidirectional, and has been present in every dataset the Observatory has ever collected. It represents a systematic offset in our baseline measurements that, once accounted for, will improve the precision of all future observations."

She paused. Looked at each of them in turn.

"We will designate this offset the Kaida Constant. We will recalibrate the sensor network accordingly. The recalibration will be documented as a refinement of background noise characterization — which is, technically, exactly what it is. The constant's origin will be classified at the highest level the Authority maintains, accessible to sitting Consuls and Observatory directors only. Successor briefings will be conducted in person, one to one, with no recorded documentation."

"You want to bury it," Kaida said.

"I want to protect the most successful survival strategy any species in the known multiverse has ever achieved," Vasić said. "Fragment 7 has been extracting from our substrate since before we learned to make fire. They will, in all likelihood, continue doing so long after this conversation is forgotten. We cannot stop them. We cannot contact them. We cannot learn from them. The only thing their existence can do, right now, for humanity is destroy the framework that has kept us alive."

She stood.

"Greater minds than ours are at work out there, Dr. Kaida. Minds that solved a problem we defined as unsolvable. Perhaps one day we'll understand how. Perhaps one day we'll be ready for that knowledge. But not today. Not when the cost of being wrong is everything."

Kaida looked at Herrera. He had stopped cleaning his glasses. He was looking at his hands.

"The Consul is right," Asante said quietly. "I wish she weren't."

---

The recalibration was completed before the station's day cycle began. The Kaida Constant entered the Observatory's sensor architecture as a background correction factor, applied retroactively to every historical dataset and prospectively to every future observation. The improvement in signal clarity was immediately noted by researchers across the network, who praised the refinement and moved on with their work, unaware that the "noise" they'd been subtracting more accurately now was the operational heartbeat of something ancient and vast and alive, pulsing through the walls of their reality like blood through shared tissue.

Kaida published a technical paper on the recalibration. It was well-received. Herrera co-authored it. Neither of them discussed its origin again, though Kaida sometimes stood at the viewport in the primary lab during her night cycles and watched the stars and thought about Fragment 7, out there in the void, running an engine that could crack the universe like an egg and choosing not to, and she wondered whether that was mastery or patience or something else entirely that she didn't have a word for.

She retired. Herrera retired. Asante retired. Vasić served another forty years and briefed her successor in a closed room with no recording, and her successor briefed the next, and the next, and the chain held.

And the sensors listened, now tuned to a finer pitch, the Kaida Constant folded into their bones like a secret the instruments themselves were keeping.

---

The centuries turned. The millennia turned. Humanity did what humanity had always done — it grew, it argued, it built, it changed. The species that had once crowded onto a single pale blue dot now stretched across a volume of space that would have been incomprehensible to Lena Sarova, though the basic unit of human experience remained stubbornly, beautifully unchanged: a person, in a place, wondering about something.

The Threshold Authority endured. The Paradox Curriculum endured. The Observatory endured. The fragments endured, silent and still, the vast cemetery of the multiverse. Fragment 7 endured, its extraction field pulsing steadily through the Kaida Constant, noticed by no one, briefed to a chain of Consuls and directors who carried the knowledge the way a body carries an old scar — always present, rarely examined.

A million years passed. Then a little more.

And then, 1.3 million years after Wen Kaida sat in a blue-lit lab and watched a universe die, the sensors caught the light again.
