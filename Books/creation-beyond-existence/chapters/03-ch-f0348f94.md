Beatriz Melo had a habit that irritated almost everyone in the Coalition and that Lena had come to depend on like oxygen. She asked the question no one wanted asked, and then she sat quietly while the silence did its work.

She did it now, in the days following Lena's presentation, not in a formal session but over coffee in the CERN cafeteria, where the fluorescent lighting made everyone look slightly ill and the espresso machine produced something that was technically coffee in the same way that a photograph of a meal was technically dinner.

"How many fragments have you mapped?" Melo asked. She was a small woman, dark-eyed, with close-cropped silver hair and the particular stillness of someone who had learned that most people would fill a silence if you gave them long enough.

"Confirmed? Sixty-three. Probable candidates, another hundred and forty." Lena wrapped her hands around her cup. "Miguel thinks we're seeing maybe five percent of what's actually out there. The rest are too old or too far to resolve with current instruments."

"So hundreds. Possibly thousands."

"Possibly."

Melo nodded slowly. She stirred her coffee. She didn't drink it.

"Every one of them a civilization," she said. "Every one of them intelligent enough to manipulate the fabric of reality. And every one of them dead."

"We don't know they were all civilizations. The ignition signatures suggest directed process, but—"

"Lena." Melo looked at her with the gentle, immovable patience that made her so effective and so maddening. "You know what the data says. I'm not asking you to prove it in a journal. I'm asking you to sit with what it means."

What it meant. Lena had been avoiding what it meant with the same disciplined focus she brought to everything — by burying herself in methodology, in calibration tables, in the clean certainty of numbers. But Melo had a way of stripping the insulation off a live wire and handing it to you.

"It means intelligence is a pattern," Lena said quietly. "It means that across however many billions of years, across realities that had nothing to do with each other, the same thing keeps happening. Life arises. It gets smart. It builds. And eventually it builds something that cracks the foundation of its own existence."

"Yes," Melo said. "That's what it means. And you need to let the Coalition wrestle with that before Raines and Tanabe turn this into a policy problem."

---

The session Melo convened — informally, over Tanabe's mild objection that they had "more pressing analytical work" — took place in one of the smaller meeting rooms on a Thursday evening. Fourteen of the twenty-two Coalition members attended. General Raines, to Lena's surprise, was among them, sitting in his usual back-row seat with his notebook open.

Melo stood at the front of the room without slides, without notes, without anything except the question she'd been carrying since Lena's presentation.

"I want to talk about the pattern," she said. "Not the physics of it. The meaning of it."

Dr. Konstantin Voronov, one of the quantum field theorists from Beijing — though he was originally Russian, a detail he corrected with weary frequency — shifted in his chair. He was a lanky, sharp-featured man who spoke rarely and precisely. "Meaning is not our department, Beatriz."

"It is now. Sixty-three confirmed dead universes. Each one killed by the same kind of event. Each event bearing the signature of technological origin. This is not a physics problem anymore, Konstantin. This is a question about what intelligence *does.*"

The room was quiet. Melo let it sit.

"The Fermi Paradox asked why the universe seemed empty of intelligent life," she continued. "We had dozens of proposed answers. Rare Earth. Great Filters. Zoo hypotheses. But none of them accounted for what we're seeing now, because none of them imagined that the answer might be written in the wreckage of other realities entirely. The silence isn't because intelligence is rare. It's because intelligence, given enough time and capability, destroys the very space it inhabits."

"That's an enormous generalization from sixty-three data points," said a voice from the middle of the room. Lena recognized it — Dr. Priya Chandrasekaran, one of the cosmologists from the Indian Space Research Organisation, careful and methodical to a fault.

"Is it?" Melo didn't challenge the objection. She opened it up. "Sixty-three is what we can see. Miguel estimates we're resolving perhaps five percent of the total field. Scale that up. Thousands of realities, Priya. Thousands of civilizations. Every single one we can analyze in enough detail shows the same terminal signature. At what point does a pattern become a law?"

Voronov leaned forward. "There is a difference between a statistical trend and a law of nature. You are proposing something closer to a... a behavioral inevitability. That any sufficiently advanced intelligence will, given enough time, develop the capacity to trigger a genesis-level energy event. That is not physics. That is prophecy."

"It's neither," Melo said. "It's observation. I'm not saying it's inevitable. I'm asking this room to consider the possibility that it might be, and to think about what follows from that."

What followed was three hours of the most difficult conversation Lena had ever witnessed.

It began with objections — reasonable, scientific, properly skeptical. The sample was biased toward catastrophic outcomes because those were the events energetic enough to detect. Civilizations that avoided the threshold wouldn't leave fragments, and therefore wouldn't appear in the data. Survivorship bias. Selection effects. The usual tools for dismantling uncomfortable conclusions.

Melo absorbed them all. She agreed with most of them. And then she asked the room a question that nobody had a tool for.

"Suppose some civilizations do avoid it. Suppose the fragments represent only the failures — half, a third, even a tenth of all the civilizations that ever reached that level of capability. That still leaves hundreds, possibly thousands, of intelligent species who crossed a threshold they didn't know existed and annihilated everything they'd ever built or known or loved. Not through malice. Not through war. Through *achievement.* Through the natural, predictable progression of curiosity and capability." She paused. "What does that tell us about the relationship between intelligence and survival?"

The room fractured. Not angrily — these were people trained to disagree productively — but deeply, along fault lines that Lena hadn't known were there.

One camp, led loosely by Voronov, argued that the pattern was a warning but not a sentence. Intelligence was adaptive. The fact that many civilizations had failed did not mean all would. Humanity's advantage was precisely this knowledge — they could see the wreckage, map the threshold, and build safeguards. The fragments were cautionary tales, not destiny.

The other camp, which coalesced around a young cosmologist named Adaeze Obi whom Lena hadn't paid much attention to before, took a darker view. The sheer number of failures suggested something structural. Not a lack of caution but a feature of how intelligence itself worked — the same curiosity and capability that allowed a species to reach the threshold was the same drive that pushed them across it. You couldn't have one without the other. The tool and the trap were the same thing.

"You're describing a closed loop," Voronov said, his voice tight. "Intelligence arises, intelligence builds, intelligence destroys itself. That is nihilism dressed as cosmology."

"It's not nihilism," Obi replied. She was calm, almost eerily so, her voice carrying the flat clarity of someone who had already followed the logic to its end and made peace with where it led. "It's a design constraint. Nihilism says nothing matters. I'm saying everything matters — that's the problem. Everything an intelligent species does matters so much, affects reality at such a fundamental level, that eventually the cumulative weight of their capability exceeds the structural tolerance of their own universe. It's not that they're reckless. It's that they're *effective.*"

Lena looked at Raines. The general was writing steadily, his expression unreadable.

Melo let the debate run. She let it tangle and knot and circle back on itself, because she understood something that Lena was only beginning to grasp: the Coalition needed to have this argument now. Before the policy meetings. Before the classified briefings. Before the question of what to *do* consumed the question of what they were actually facing.

It was nearly midnight when Melo brought them back.

"I'm not asking you to agree on whether this is fate or a tendency," she said. "I'm asking you to agree on what the stakes are. If there is even a possibility that intelligence carries within it the seeds of this kind of destruction — not as a flaw, but as a *feature* — then the project of this Coalition changes. We're not just mapping fragments. We're not just doing cosmology. We're trying to answer the most important question any species has ever faced."

She looked around the room. Fourteen exhausted faces looked back.

"Can intelligence survive itself?"

No one answered. The ventilation hummed. Somewhere in the building, a door opened and closed.

General Raines closed his notebook, capped his pen, and for the first time since joining the Coalition, spoke without being spoken to.

"That's the right question," he said. "And I think you need to know that my government has been running energy-loss experiments at the Sandia National Laboratories for the past eighteen months that are more relevant to this conversation than anyone in this room currently realizes."

The silence that followed was of a different kind entirely.
