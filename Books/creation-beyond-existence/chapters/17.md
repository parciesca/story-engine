The celebration, such as it was, lasted about four hours. Then Nath ruined it.

She hadn't meant to. She'd been running routine propagation analysis on Fragment 7's response — characterizing the transmission path, mapping how the curriculum data was flowing through the extraction field's network — when she noticed something that should have been obvious from the beginning but that the sheer overwhelm of the moment had buried.

"The response went everywhere," she told the Commission.

Ry looked up from the curriculum data they'd been drowning in. "Clarify."

"Fragment 7's transmission. The full curriculum. It wasn't directed at us. It was broadcast across the entire extraction field. Every universe the field touches. Every fragment, every region of the void within the network's reach. The same data, the same density, the same structure, propagating outward in every direction simultaneously."

The workspace was quiet as the implication settled.

"They don't know where we are," Kiol said.

"No. They know *that* we are. Our perturbation signal reached them and was recognized as intentional. But the extraction field isn't a precision instrument for locating the source of a perturbation. It's a gradient — energy flows from surrounding regions toward Fragment 7. We introduced a structured interruption into that flow, and the interruption propagated inward. But from Fragment 7's perspective, the signal arrived from the entire field simultaneously, the same way a vibration in a spiderweb reaches the center without telling the spider which strand was plucked."

"So they responded the only way they could," Nath continued. "By transmitting back along every strand at once. The full curriculum, pushed out through the entire network, saturating every universe and every region the extraction field touches. Because they couldn't send it specifically to us."

Esse, who had been working through curriculum translations with the focused intensity of someone drinking from a fire hose, leaned back from the data.

"A billion years of sending targeted messages into newborn universes," they said. "Precise, directed transmissions into the boundary zone of each genesis event. But when someone finally answers from an *existing* universe, they can't find them. The hotline works. The address book doesn't."

"The extraction field was designed for energy transfer, not communication," Pell observed. "What we used it for — and what Fragment 7 is now using it for — is an improvisation. An extraordinary one. But the underlying architecture wasn't built for bidirectional exchange. It was built to pull. We knocked on an intake pipe and heard us on the other end. They shouted back through the entire plumbing system because they couldn't shout through just our tap."

Ry sat with this for a long time. The curriculum data was still pouring in — a torrent of structured knowledge flowing through every node in the Observatory's sensor network, through every universe in Fragment 7's vast extraction web. Somewhere out there, the transmission was washing through the substrates of dead fragments and living realities alike. An ancient teacher, shouting its lesson into every room in the building because it couldn't tell which room the student was sitting in.

"This changes our mandate," Ry said.

"This changes everything," Nath replied.

---

The Consul received the Commission's supplementary report within days. The core finding was stark: Fragment 7's communication capability, while extraordinary, was omnidirectional. The ancient intelligence could broadcast but not target. It could listen for perturbations in its extraction field but not localize their origin. The *enne* had initiated contact, but the contact was, in a meaningful sense, one-sided — the *enne* could locate Fragment 7, could study its transmissions, could even send messages that Fragment 7 would receive. But Fragment 7 could not find *them* without help.

The supplementary report also noted something that the Commission considered both a strategic asset and a profound responsibility: the *enne* possessed a capability that Fragment 7 apparently did not. Kaida's precursor detection model — refined across a million years of Observatory work — could identify and localize substrate extraction signatures with precision. The *enne* could pinpoint active extraction sources. They could detect civilizations approaching the threshold. They could, in principle, find every living intelligence in every universe the extraction field touched.

They had eyes where Fragment 7 had only a voice.

The Consul read the report. Read the Matrix. And for the first time in the institution's history, issued a mandate that expanded the Commission's scope beyond contact.

*Contact Commission. Your mandate is hereby extended. In addition to ongoing communication with Fragment 7, the Commission is tasked with developing a directed communication capability — a means of converting the extraction field from an omnidirectional broadcast network into a system capable of targeted transmission. The objective is to complement Fragment 7's reach with the Observatory's precision. If we can locate civilizations approaching the threshold, and if we can direct Fragment 7's curriculum to them specifically, then we have the foundation for intervention.*

*You are no longer preparing to make contact. You are preparing to make contact matter.*

---

The work took generations. Of course it did. Everything the *enne* did that mattered took generations, and this mattered more than almost anything.

The problem was fundamentally one of engineering at a scale the *enne* had never attempted. The extraction field was Fragment 7's creation — an energy transfer system of incomprehensible sophistication, threaded through the substrate of every neighboring reality like mycelium through soil. The *enne* could interact with it locally. They could introduce perturbations, read modulations, send and receive signals along its gradient. But reshaping the field itself — converting it from a broadcast medium into a directed one — required understanding the field's architecture at a depth that the *enne* didn't yet possess.

The curriculum helped. As successive generations of Commission researchers worked through its layers, they developed an increasingly sophisticated understanding of how the extraction field functioned. Fragment 7 had, in its comprehensive way, included the theoretical foundations for its own engineering in the teaching materials. Not as a separate technical manual, but woven into the substrate physics curriculum — because the extraction field *was* substrate physics, applied at a level that made the *enne*'s entire technological civilization look like finger painting.

The early generations focused on theory. Understanding the field's topology. Mapping its structure within their own universe with instruments calibrated by the new knowledge the curriculum provided. Each layer of the curriculum that the Commission cracked open revealed new dimensions of the field's architecture — branching pathways, resonance channels, propagation modes that the Observatory's sensors had been filtering out as noise for a million years.

The middle generations began experimenting. Small-scale field modulations, carefully constrained, each one approved by the sitting Consul after review against the Matrix. The Sarova Line still held — the *enne* were not manipulating vacuum energy for power or capability. They were learning to shape a communication channel within an existing field. The distinction was meaningful. The curriculum's pedagogical sequence ensured that each step in capability was accompanied by the corresponding understanding, exactly as Fragment 7 had designed it. You could not misuse what you were learning because you could not learn it out of order.

The later generations achieved the breakthrough. Not a single dramatic moment — the *enne* had long since outgrown the narrative of sudden discovery — but a gradual convergence of understanding and capability that reached, over the course of several generations, a functional threshold of its own. They learned to create directional channels within the extraction field. Pathways that could carry a signal not to every universe simultaneously but to a specific region, a specific substrate, a specific point in the void.

They built, in essence, a telephone system inside Fragment 7's plumbing. A way to call a specific room instead of shouting into every room at once.

And they married it to the Observatory's detection grid. Kaida's precursor model, refined across a million years, could identify a civilization beginning to experiment with substrate extraction — the telltale rhythmic perturbations, the slow ramp of increasing capability, the signature of a species reaching toward the threshold without knowing it was there. The directed communication system could then route Fragment 7's curriculum — or a targeted portion of it, calibrated to the recipient's apparent level of development — to that specific civilization, in that specific universe, at the moment when the teaching might do the most good.

Detection. Direction. Delivery. The *enne* had become, without quite planning it, the postal service for Fragment 7's billion-year mission. The ancient teacher finally had a partner who could find the students and put the textbook in their hands.

The Commission's report to the Consul at the end of the development period was the most consequential document in *enne* history since the original Kaida Constant files. It was also, in keeping with Commission tradition, the most understated.

*The directed communication system is operational. The detection grid can identify precursor-state civilizations across the full range of the extraction field network. We are prepared to deliver targeted curriculum transmissions to civilizations approaching the threshold.*

*We recommend beginning with observation. Identify candidates. Study their development. Understand their trajectory. When we are confident in our assessment, deliver the introduction layers of the curriculum in a format calibrated to their stage of development.*

*Fragment 7 has been trying to do this alone for billions of years. It has been trying by broadcasting into newborn universes and hoping someone receives the message before it's too late. The success rate, as far as we can determine, is zero.*

*We can do better. Not because we are wiser than Fragment 7, but because we can see what it cannot: exactly where the students are, exactly how far along they've come, and exactly how much time they have left.*
