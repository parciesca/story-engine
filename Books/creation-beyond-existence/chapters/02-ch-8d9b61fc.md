The first attempt to classify the data came eleven days after the discovery, and it arrived not as a formal order but as a phone call from a man who didn't identify himself, asking Lena — politely, in the way that powerful people are polite when they haven't yet decided whether you're useful or dangerous — to please refrain from publishing until "the appropriate parties" had reviewed her findings.

She published that night.

The paper went up on the preprint server at 2:14 AM Chilean time, co-authored with Miguel and three colleagues at the South Pole Telescope who had independently verified the gravitational signatures. It was forty-six pages of dense technical analysis under a title Lena had fought with herself about for hours. She'd wanted something measured, something that would signal to the scientific community that this was rigorous work and not the ravings of a woman who'd stared too long at the noise floor. She'd settled on: *Evidence for Extra-Universal Gravitational Signatures and Structured Ignition Profiles in Deep Array Observations.*

It was downloaded eleven thousand times in the first hour.

By morning, Director Yuki Tanabe at CERN was on the phone, and unlike the anonymous man from the day before, she identified herself immediately and spoke with the brisk, slightly exasperated warmth of someone who had been managing geniuses and their catastrophes for thirty years.

"You've started a fire, Dr. Sarova."

"I've reported an observation."

"You've reported that the universe is surrounded by the wreckage of dead realities and that something is making new ones. In the current political climate, that's not an observation. That's a grenade." Tanabe paused. Lena could hear her tapping something — a pen, probably, the woman was famous for it. "How confident are you in the ignition structure analysis?"

"Completely."

"That's what worries me. I have three governments asking me to contain this, two asking me to weaponize it, and the Secretary-General's office asking me what it means. What does it mean, Dr. Sarova?"

Lena had rehearsed this. She'd been rehearsing it in her head since the night she'd called Miguel, turning the words over like stones, trying to find the arrangement that was both honest and not paralyzing.

"It means that universes don't just happen," she said. "They're caused. The ignition signatures are too structured for natural quantum events — the energy distributions follow patterns that suggest a directed process. And the fragments we've mapped, the dead universes, all show terminal energy profiles consistent with the same kind of event. Something begins it, and something ends it, and they may be the same something."

"You're suggesting a civilization could accidentally create a universe."

"I'm suggesting it's happened. Repeatedly. Over a timescale that makes the age of our universe look like a rounding error."

Tanabe was quiet for a long time. The pen-tapping stopped.

"Come to Geneva," she said. "Bring Estrada. Bring whoever you trust. I'm going to do something I've never done in twenty-eight years at this institution — I'm going to convene a working group before a committee tells me to."

---

The Coalition — no one called it that at first, but the name stuck almost immediately, as though the word had been waiting for them — assembled in a converted lecture hall in CERN's Building 40 over the course of three weeks. Tanabe had a gift for recruitment that bordered on the surgical. She didn't want the loudest voices or the biggest names. She wanted people who could sit with terrible knowledge and still think clearly.

Lena counted twenty-two researchers by the end of the first month. Astrophysicists. Cosmologists. Two quantum field theorists from Beijing who barely spoke to anyone but each other and produced work of breathtaking elegance. A philosopher of science from São Paulo whom everyone initially resented and quickly came to rely on, because she was the only person in the room willing to say "we don't know what that means" when everyone else was building castles on assumptions.

And then there was General Arthur Raines.

He arrived in the fourth week, unannounced, in civilian clothes, with credentials from U.S. Strategic Command and a manner so deliberately unthreatening that Lena distrusted him instantly. He was tall, gray-haired, soft-spoken, and had the particular quality of attention that she associated with people who were trained to assess threats.

"I'm not here to shut you down," he said, when Tanabe introduced him to the group with visible reluctance. "I'm here because my government wants to understand what you're finding, and I'd rather be in the room hearing it firsthand than reading a summary written by someone who doesn't understand the physics."

"And if you don't like what you hear?" Lena asked.

Raines smiled. It was a careful smile, practiced, but not entirely empty of warmth. "Then I'll say so. And you'll ignore me, and we'll both go about our business. I'm an observer, Dr. Sarova. I observe."

She didn't believe him. But Tanabe had made a calculation — better to have the military in the room where they could be managed than outside the room where they could not — and Lena respected Tanabe's instincts enough to tolerate the arrangement.

The work itself was consuming. The Deep Array data was only the beginning. With the Coalition's combined resources, they gained access to gravitational wave detectors, orbital telescopes, and particle accelerator data from six countries. Piece by piece, the map of the multiverse grew. New fragments emerged from the noise — some so faint they required weeks of signal processing to confirm, others startlingly clear once you knew what to look for.

Miguel, who had never wanted to be more than a technician and was now suddenly in the intellectual center of the most important discovery in human history, handled it by doing what he had always done: meticulous, grinding, unglamorous work. He built the database. He standardized the fragment classification system. He created the nomenclature that the entire Coalition adopted within days, because it was clean and intuitive and Miguel had a gift for making complicated things simple.

Fragments were classified by estimated age and size. The oldest, the most degraded, were Class One — barely detectable gravitational whispers, their original universes so long dead that only the faintest structural echoes remained. The youngest, including the newborn universe that had started everything, were Class Five — hot, bright, still expanding. Their own universe, by Miguel's system, was a healthy Class Four. Mature. Stable. Still very much alive.

The ignition signatures told the real story. By the third month, the pattern was undeniable. Every fragment above Class Two — every dead universe with enough remaining structure to analyze — showed the same terminal energy profile. Not the slow fade of heat death. Not the crunch of gravitational collapse. A spike. Sudden, massive, and bearing the unmistakable hallmarks of structured energy release.

Lena presented the findings to the full Coalition on a gray Tuesday afternoon in Geneva. She spoke for forty minutes without notes. The room was silent throughout.

"Every dead universe we can adequately analyze died the same way," she said. "A single catastrophic energy event, originating from within, with characteristics inconsistent with any known natural process. The energy isn't random. It's organized. It carries the fingerprint of directed manipulation — not intent, necessarily, but *process.* Something built. Something engineered. Something that worked exactly as designed, right up until the moment it did something its creators didn't expect."

She clicked to her final slide. The map. Their universe in the center, the fragments scattered around it like broken shells, the newborn universe glowing at the edge.

"We are surrounded by the remains of civilizations that learned to manipulate energy at a fundamental level and didn't understand what they'd built. Each of those fragments was, we believe, a universe like ours. Each contained — or could have contained — stars, planets, life, intelligence. And each one ended in a moment, because someone somewhere reached too far without looking down."

The room was very quiet.

General Raines, sitting in the back row, was writing something in a small notebook. He didn't look up.

The philosopher from São Paulo — Dr. Beatriz Melo, who had become the group's informal conscience — raised her hand.

"The newborn," she said. "The Class Five. The one that started all of this. You're saying someone just died. An entire reality, with everyone and everything in it. And what we detected — the event that brought us all here — was their death."

Lena nodded.

"Not their death," she said. "Their mistake."
