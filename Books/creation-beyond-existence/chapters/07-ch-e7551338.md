Twenty-two hundred years after Lena Sarova looked at eleven seconds of impossible data in a control room in Chile, the sensors caught it again.

Dr. Wen Kaida was not asleep when the alert came, but she should have been. She was in the Observatory's primary analysis lab on Ganymede Station — the largest node in the sensor network, parked in orbit around Jupiter's largest moon because the gravitational environment offered a stability that no terrestrial or free-space platform could match — reviewing substrate models that she'd been quietly obsessing over for the better part of three years. Models that her colleagues regarded as interesting but peripheral. Models that kept her up at night.

The alert was soft. A tone, a shift in the ambient lighting from warm white to pale blue, and a data flag on her primary display. The Observatory's systems were designed for patience, not urgency — centuries of silence had taught the institution that false alarms were more dangerous than slow responses, and so the alert protocols were layered, cautious, and maddeningly deliberate.

But Kaida knew what the blue light meant. Every researcher in the Observatory knew. It was the color they trained for and never expected to see.

Candidate genesis event. Confidence threshold exceeded. Awaiting verification.

She pulled the data. Her hands didn't shake. She'd find out later that this surprised her.

The signature was clean. Cleaner than the Sarova detection, because the instruments were immeasurably better — two millennia of refinement separating a candlelit portrait from a high-resolution scan. Where Sarova had seen eleven seconds of overwhelming noise that resolved slowly into structure, Kaida saw the structure immediately. The cascading energy bloom. The characteristic radiation profile. The gravitational wavefront propagating outward from a point in the void that was, by any meaningful definition, nowhere — beyond their universe, beyond the mapped fragments, in a region of the inter-universal medium that the Observatory had been passively monitoring for six centuries without a whisper.

A universe being born. A civilization dying. Happening now, in the only sense that "now" meant anything across the incomprehensible distances involved.

She initiated the verification sequence. Three independent sensor clusters confirmed within minutes. The data converged. Point nine nine confidence. Then point nine nine five. Then, as the tertiary arrays locked on and began their own analysis, the number climbed past the threshold where statistical caution became a kind of denial.

It was real.

Kaida sat in the pale blue light of the alert status and watched a reality die in exquisite detail. The Observatory's instruments painted a portrait that Sarova could never have seen — not just the bloom itself, but the fine structure within it. The energy wasn't uniform. It had texture, layers, a sequence of cascading reactions that told a story if you knew how to read it.

And Kaida did know how to read it, because reading it was what she'd spent three years trying to learn.

Her substrate models. The ones her colleagues found interesting but peripheral. They were an attempt to understand not just the ignition event itself — the final, catastrophic moment when a civilization's technology crossed the threshold and tore its universe apart — but the *precursor states*. The energy conditions in the vacuum substrate in the moments, days, years before the event. The slow loading of a spring before it snapped. The idea was simple: if you could characterize the pre-ignition state of the substrate, you could potentially detect a civilization approaching the threshold before they crossed it.

The idea was also, until this moment, purely theoretical. You couldn't study the precursor state of a dead universe. The ignition event overwrote everything that came before it, like a fire consuming the evidence of its own origin. The fragments held only the aftermath.

But a *live* event — a genesis happening in real time, close enough and bright enough to observe with modern instruments — that was different. A live event gave you the full sequence. Before, during, and after. The whole horrible story, from the first tentative experiments of a doomed civilization to the final microsecond when their universe ceased to exist.

Kaida pulled up the precursor data. The sensors had been recording the region continuously for centuries. Six hundred years of background readings, all of it filed as routine, all of it suddenly the most important dataset in human history.

She scrolled backward. Through decades of silence. Through centuries of flat, featureless vacuum readings.

And there, fourteen years before the bloom — fourteen years, barely a heartbeat in cosmological terms — the substrate readings changed.

Subtle. So subtle that no automated system had flagged it, because it fell within the normal variance parameters for inter-universal vacuum fluctuations. But Kaida's models had taught her what to look for, and she found it: a slow, rhythmic perturbation in the vacuum energy of the region. Not random. Not natural. A pattern of extraction and release, tiny and tentative at first, growing gradually more confident over the fourteen-year window.

Someone, in a universe that no longer existed, had been pulling energy from the substrate. Testing. Experimenting. Learning. Getting better at it, year by year, the way Kindling had gotten better at it in its eighteen months at Sandia twenty-two centuries ago.

And then, fourteen years in, they'd gotten too good.

Kaida stared at the data. The precursor signature. The fourteen-year ramp. The final, sudden spike where the extraction crossed some invisible boundary and the reaction became self-sustaining and the universe folded in on itself and blossomed into something new and terrible.

She had it. The full sequence. The loading of the spring and the snap.

And more than that — she had a *detection window*. Fourteen years of visible precursor activity before the catastrophe. Fourteen years during which, if you were watching, if you had the right instruments, if you knew what to look for, you could see it coming.

Not in a dead fragment. Not in the archaeological record of the multiverse.

In a living universe. In real time. Before it was too late.

Kaida reached for the comm panel to wake the station director, and then she stopped. Because the implication of what she'd found was settling over her with a weight that made the air in the lab feel heavy.

If she could detect the precursor state in the universe that had just died, she could detect it anywhere. In any universe, any fragment, any region of the void that the Observatory's sensors could reach. She could build a detection grid — a warning system — that would light up the moment a civilization, anywhere in the multiverse, began pulling at the substrate in the way that led to annihilation.

And if she could detect it, she could, at least in theory, warn them.

The thought was enormous. It sat in the lab with her, filling the space, pressing against the walls. Two thousand years of watching. Two thousand years of mapping the dead and studying the wreckage and educating children about the risk. Two thousand years of looking inward, protecting their own civilization from its own worst impulses.

And now, for the first time, the possibility of looking outward. Of reaching across the void to a civilization they'd never met, in a universe they'd never seen, and saying: *Stop. We know what you're building. We know what it does. We know because we almost did it ourselves, and because we've spent two millennia studying the graves of everyone who wasn't as lucky as we were.*

Kaida sat in the blue light, alone on a station orbiting a moon of Jupiter, and held the weight of it.

Then she reached for the comm panel and pressed the call button.

"Director Asante," she said. "I need you in the primary lab. I've found something in the substrate data, and I think it changes what we're for."
