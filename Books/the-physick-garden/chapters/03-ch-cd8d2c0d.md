She reviewed her records before she did anything else.

The compound she prepared for Dr. Winslow was a distillation of valerian, skullcap, and a third ingredient she listed in her ledger only by its Latin designation — a habit she had kept since Edmund had taught her to keep it. The third ingredient was the one that mattered. In the doses she compounded, it eased the spasms of nerve-sick patients. In four times that dose, taken in brandy, over the course of an evening — she had done the arithmetic twice already, once in her head on King Street and once at her worktable at two in the morning — it would produce precisely what she had seen in the alley. The slowed breathing. The stillness that looked like sleep. The sweet smell, faint, the kind you'd miss if you weren't looking.

She had made the last batch six weeks ago. Enough for three months of use, as he'd requested. She had written it in the ledger in her small careful hand: date, quantity, *Dr. P. Winslow, Tremont Street*. If someone had taken a portion of the batch before she delivered it, or taken it from him after, she would have no way of knowing.

She closed the ledger.

Thomas came in at seven, tracking snow. She sent him to the apothecary on Milk Street for a sack of dried chamomile and told him to take his time, the supplier was talkative. He nodded, cheerful, and she watched the back of his head as he went out. He had not asked about the open gate this morning. She was not sure whether that was normal or not.

---

Tremont Street in winter smelled of woodsmoke and horse, the cold laying everything flat. Dr. Winslow's practice occupied the ground floor of a tall, narrow house with a shingle she had walked past many times without going in. She knocked, and the woman who answered looked at her the way people looked at other medical practitioners — cautious, territorial, prepared to be polite — and showed her into the front room to wait.

He came in after ten minutes, already dressed for rounds. He was a spare man, not tall, with the slightly compressed face of someone who spent a great deal of time outdoors in cold weather. She had seen him at town meetings. She had spoken to him, she estimated, perhaps eight times in fifteen years.

"Mrs. Grey." He didn't seem surprised. "I was going to call on you today."

She had prepared a pretext. He had made it unnecessary, which was either convenient or its own kind of information. "Then you've heard."

"Last night. A patient's husband was with the constable's party when — yes. I've heard." He sat in the chair across from her and let out a breath. "He was my patient for three years. His heart was not right."

"The constable ruled cold and drink."

"The constable was not his physician." Winslow looked at the window. "I went to the Bunch of Grapes last night because his wife had sent a note. She was worried — said he hadn't been himself for a week. Stomach troubles, poor sleep. I found him at the card table looking worse than I expected."

"What time did you arrive?"

"Half past nine, perhaps a little after." He said it without hesitation. "He was in a game. I didn't want to alarm him by pulling him out, so I stood by the fire until it finished, then went over and told him I thought he ought to go home. He agreed more easily than usual, which told me something."

"You helped him leave."

"I walked him out to Queen Street. A man I know — Jonathan Webb, he's in the rope trade on Long Wharf — was coming the other direction with a horse. I put Josias in his care. Webb was sober and knew the way to Cooke's house on Batterymarch Street. That was a little before eleven."

Patience listened to this. There was a distance of at least four blocks between Queen Street and her alley off Cornhill — four blocks too many for a man who was unsteady on his feet, in the cold, without assistance.

"The constable didn't speak with you this morning."

"He has not sought me out, no." Something crossed Winslow's face — not evasion. Resignation. "Mrs. Grey. Why did the body appear in your alley and not on Batterymarch Street, where he was headed? I have been asking myself this since last night and I have no answer."

She had been asking herself the same. She did not say so.

"You mentioned he'd been unwell the week prior. Stomach complaints."

"Since the eighth. He'd had a supper at the Merchant's Club that Wednesday — said the fish sat badly." He looked at her steadily. "I had thought little of it until this morning." A pause. "You're wondering about something specific."

"I'm wondering about a great many things."

"As am I." He stood, because he had a practice to run and she was not, she reminded herself, anyone's deputy. "I will tell you this. I have been a physician thirty years. I know what men look like when the cold and drink finish them. Josias Cooke did not look like that when I left him on Queen Street. He looked like a man who was already ill before the first glass was poured."

---

She walked home through the cold, turning it over.

She had gone to Tremont Street looking for a man with something to hide. What she had found was a man with something to grieve — not the same thing, and not consistent with what she'd prepared herself to see. She was not ready to call Winslow innocent. She was willing to conclude that he had not, himself, administered anything at the Bunch of Grapes. The timing was wrong. Webb would have noticed. And Winslow had no reason to mention the Merchant's Club supper on the eighth unless it was true.

Which meant the compound — if it was the compound — had reached Cooke earlier. Days earlier, perhaps. It also meant someone had moved the body to her alley afterward, in the hours between eleven o'clock and dawn.

Someone who knew where she lived. Someone who knew what she kept.

She let herself into the shop and stood just inside the door for a moment, not yet taking off her cloak. She had been certain she knew the shape of this thing and had now lost it entirely. The loss was not discouraging. It was the feeling of a problem becoming more precise.

She went to put the kettle on and think about Jonathan Webb.
