The Bunch of Grapes on King Street was the sort of establishment that tolerated respectable women provided they arrived with a husband or a sufficient air of impatience. Patience arrived with only the latter, which she deployed at the door in the form of a look that suggested she would not be stopping long and was not here for reasons anyone needed to examine closely.

The landlord was a thickset man named Horne who recognized her on sight, which told her something about the size of this town and the reach of an apothecary's custom.

"Mrs. Grey." He set down the cup he was drying without finishing the job.

"I won't take long." She placed coins on the bar without being asked and didn't look at them. "Alderman Cooke was here last evening."

"I've spoken with the constable."

"I'm sure. I'm not the constable."

Horne looked at her with the patient discomfort of a man who had learned that women who came to taverns alone at night were either very determined or very foolish, and who was not yet certain which kind he was dealing with. He picked up the cup again. "He was here from seven or thereabouts. Card game in the back. He was with the usual men."

"And later? When he left?"

"I don't watch the door."

"Who does?"

He said nothing, but his eyes moved — briefly, unconsciously — to the far end of the bar, where a girl of about sixteen was stacking cups with the focused care of someone trying not to be noticed. Patience walked to her end of the bar.

The girl's name was Nell, as it turned out, and she had the useful quality of not being old enough to have learned which things you weren't supposed to say.

"Yes, I saw him leave," she said, when Patience asked. She kept stacking cups. "He wasn't well. Leaning."

"Who was with him?"

"The doctor. Dr. Winslow." She said it the same way she might have said the time of night — a fact, without weight, offered and released. "He had him by the arm. I thought it was kind, really. The doctor being here and helping him out."

Patience heard herself say, "Yes. Very kind," from somewhere slightly outside herself.

She asked one more question, evenly, as if it were an afterthought: "Do you know if Dr. Winslow had been here long? Or did he come in late?"

Nell thought about it. "Late, I think. After the card game started. He didn't sit — just stood by the fire a while, then went over to the alderman." She paused. "They talked quiet. The alderman seemed glad to see him."

Patience thanked her and left before Horne could decide to object.

---

Dr. Phineas Winslow. She turned the name over as she walked, feeling its edges.

He was sixty, or nearly. He had a practice on Tremont Street and a reputation built over thirty years of being available and not too expensive and willing to make house calls in bad weather. She had referred patients to him and he had sent patients to her, and they had exchanged perhaps four hundred words total in fifteen years of professional adjacency. He was not a man she knew. He was a man she had assumed she knew, which was a different thing.

The constable had not called him this morning. This was not necessarily significant — Pratt had made his determination before sending for anyone, and if the determination was cold and drink, there was nothing for a physician to certify. The case was closed. There was no need to trouble Dr. Winslow.

Unless Pratt had tried and couldn't find him.

Unless Pratt knew exactly where he had been last night and had decided not to try.

The wind off the harbor came sharp down King Street and she turned her collar up against it, walking. The sweet smell from the alley this morning had narrowed, in her books, to two or three possibilities: one of them required equipment she didn't think was available in Boston; one was so bitter that no one would take it voluntarily in a drink; and one was something she compounded herself, twice yearly, for a physician on Tremont Street who had three patients with a particular complaint of the nerves.

She stopped at the corner of Cornhill.

Then she started walking again, faster, because the thought that had arrived was not one she could stay still with.

---

The gate to her physick garden was standing open.

She always locked it — the habit was older than thought, the hand knowing before the mind did — and she had locked it at dusk, before she went out. The iron lock was there, hanging from the post. The gate was open.

She stood in the lane for five seconds, listening. The alley was empty, the snow unmarked except for her own footprints coming in and — she looked — two sets going out. One large, one smaller, the smaller one running.

She went inside and lit a lamp and stood in the garden with it, the cold coming through her cloak.

It took her ten minutes to find what was missing. The locked chest of dangerous simples stood against the back wall, under the eaves, with the padlock in place. She had the key on her person. The chest was undisturbed.

But the drying rack above the workbench — the one she kept for things already compounded and prepared, the things nearly ready to use — had been gone through. Carefully. Almost carefully. She could see where a bundle of dried material had been shifted and set back at a slightly wrong angle, and the small tin she kept at the left of the second shelf had been moved three inches and not returned to its circle in the dust.

The tin was full. Whatever they had come for, they had not needed to take more.

They had only needed to look.

She set the lamp on the workbench and stood in the cold garden for a long time, thinking about what it meant that someone already knew what she had.
