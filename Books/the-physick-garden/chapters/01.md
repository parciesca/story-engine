The body was Thomas's discovery, though he managed to make it everyone else's problem within the quarter-hour.

Patience heard him come in through the back — the particular way the door stuck and then gave, and the particular way Thomas stumbled when it did — and she was already measuring out dried valerian root when he appeared in the doorway between the garden and the shop, his face the color of new tallow.

"There's a man," he said.

"There are several," Patience said. "Close the door, Thomas. The cold."

"In the alley." He closed the door. "A dead one."

She set down her weights.

The alley ran between her shop on Cornhill and the tannery wall behind it, wide enough for a cart but generally used for nothing so industrious. In winter it collected snow, wind, and the particular smell of a tannery that had been there longer than anyone wanted to admit. It was not, by any measure, a place a man of quality would choose to spend the night.

Josias Cooke had apparently not been given a choice.

He lay on his back between the rear of her woodpile and the fence post, one arm folded beneath him at the angle of accident rather than arrangement, his hat several feet away in the snow. He was dressed well — a wool coat with silver buttons, good leather shoes — and he was very thoroughly dead. His face bore the particular settled stillness that has no medical name but that anyone who has attended a deathbed recognizes at once as final.

Patience crouched beside him.

The cold had been sharp enough overnight to frost the hay outside the stable two streets over, but the man's lips and nostrils showed none of the rime she would have expected. She noted this and said nothing. She noted the faint smell — sweet, almost, beneath the leather and the tannery's ambient corruption — and said nothing about that either. She pressed two fingers to the angle of his jaw, which told her nothing she didn't already know, and stood.

"Go fetch Constable Pratt," she said.

"Yes, Mrs. Grey." Thomas hesitated. "Should I say how he is?"

"Dead," she said. "Say he is dead. That should be sufficient."

Constable Pratt arrived in twenty minutes with two boys trailing behind him who had no official capacity but strong opinions. He was a broad man with a careful, unhurried walk, and he looked at Josias Cooke the way he looked at most things that had already happened: with the air of someone confirming a suspicion rather than receiving a surprise.

"Alderman Cooke," he said.

"You know him," Patience said.

"Half the town knows him. The other half owes him money." Pratt bent at the knee to peer at the face, then straightened. "He was at the Bunch of Grapes last night. Several people saw him leave, late, in a state."

"In a state," Patience repeated.

"Drunk," Pratt said, in the tone of a man who did not believe in dignifying obvious things with careful language. "Cold night, wrong alley. It happens."

"He has no frost on his mouth," Patience said.

Pratt looked at her the way men of his particular temperament always looked at her: not unkindly, but with a patience that bordered on the paternal. "Mrs. Grey. The man was senseless with drink and froze in an alley. This is not a difficulty."

It was, she thought, a very specific kind of confidence — the kind that stopped a man from looking at things he hadn't already decided about. She did not say so.

"Of course," she said.

Pratt had the body removed by midmorning. The two unofficial boys went with it, still with opinions. Thomas stood in the doorway to the garden watching them go, his arms folded against the cold, and Patience watched Thomas.

He had found the body on his way to fill the kindling box. The kindling box was to the left of the back door. The woodpile, where Cooke had been lying, was to the right. There was no particular reason to walk to the right side of the garden before going left to the kindling, unless something had drawn him there.

She did not ask him about it.

Instead she went back to her valerian root, and her weights, and the particular arithmetic of seeds to water that kept her hands busy while her mind moved in its own direction. The shop filled with its ordinary morning sounds — the street outside, the creak of the building settling in the cold, Thomas eventually finding his way to the sweeping.

Around noon, a woman came in for pennyroyal. Around one, a gentleman's servant came for a headache remedy his employer had been promised last week. Patience filled both orders, took the correct money, and said the correct things, and all the while she was thinking about the smell.

She knew most of the smells that came off the dead, and off the nearly dead, and off the living who were moving toward dead without knowing it yet. This had been her education and her practice for fifteen years, the last five of it alone. The sweet beneath the leather she had catalogued in her mind quickly and without ceremony: not spirits, though close; not illness; not decay, which announced itself differently.

It was the smell of something given rather than taken.

She locked the front door at dusk and sat in the back room with the lamp and her husband's old reference books — Culpeper, a battered copy of the Pharmacopoeia Londinensis — and she found the thing she was looking for in less than an hour, which told her that she had already known what she was looking for before she started.

She closed the book and sat for a while in the quiet.

Josias Cooke had been a wealthy man with enemies she didn't know and a death she wasn't supposed to notice. The constable had closed the matter. There was no reason, none at all, for Patience Grey, apothecary, widow, to do anything further.

She got up and put on her cloak, and went to find out who had been at the Bunch of Grapes last night.
