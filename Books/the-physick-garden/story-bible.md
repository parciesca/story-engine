STORY BIBLE
============

PREMISE & TONE
  Genre: Cozy mystery, period drama
  Tone: Dry wit beneath decorum. The ordered surface of Puritan Boston — meetinghouse, merchant class, careful social hierarchy — and what happens when something ugly pushes through it. Atmospheric (cold wharves, woodsmoke, lamplight). Low violence, high observation.
  Core premise: Patience Grey, a widow running a small apothecary on Cornhill Street in Boston, 1712, finds herself drawn into the city's darker currents when a body appears behind her shop — and she alone notices that the death doesn't look like what the constable says it is.
  Setting: Boston, Province of Massachusetts Bay, winter 1712. Population roughly 10,000. The town is tightly knit, densely gossiped, still bearing the scars of the witchcraft panic twenty years past. The merchant class is rising; old Puritan certainties are fraying at the edges.

DRAMATIS PERSONAE
  Patience Grey — Widow, apothecary, Cornhill Street. 38. Keeps her late husband Edmund's shop and physick garden. Observant to an almost unsettling degree. Speaks in short, precise sentences; rarely says more than she means. Known as reliable, discreet, and impossible to fluster. Her voice anchor: she notices the thing everyone else steps around. Status: active.
  Thomas Finch — Patience's apprentice, 19. Cheerful, earnest, prone to talking himself in circles. Son of a cooper on Ship Street. Loyal in the way of someone who hasn't yet learned to be otherwise. Status: active. [Note: walked to the wrong side of the garden before the body was found — undisclosed reason.]
  Constable Ezra Pratt — Town constable, 45. A solid, practical man who prefers tidy explanations. Not corrupt — simply incurious. Speaks with the confidence of someone who decided long ago not to be surprised. Status: active.
  Alderman Josias Cooke — Merchant, deceased. 52. Found dead in the alley behind Patience's shop, morning of 14 February 1712. Wealthy, well-connected, reportedly prone to drink. Status: deceased.
  Dr. Phineas Winslow — Town physician, Tremont Street. Nearly 60. Thirty-year practice built on availability and reasonable fees. Professional acquaintance of Patience's — she compounds a specific nerve remedy for him twice yearly. Was at the Bunch of Grapes on the night Cooke died; came in late, sought Cooke out, escorted him out the door. Was not called to examine the body. Status: active, suspect.
  Nell — Serving girl at the Bunch of Grapes, 16. Forthcoming, not yet trained in what not to say. Status: minor/active.
  Silas Horne — Landlord, the Bunch of Grapes, 50s. Careful, prefers not to know things. Status: minor/active.

WORLD RULES
  - Early 18th century Boston: no police force, no forensics. The constable handles public order; physicians handle the body if available; gossip handles everything else.
  - Patience's trade is legal and respected. She compounds remedies, sells simples, advises on dosage. She also knows what kills.
  - Women of her station (respectable widow, property-holding, in trade) occupy an ambiguous social position — more latitude than most women, less standing than any man of equivalent means.
  - The physick garden behind her shop is small but well-kept: drying herbs, a cold frame, a locked chest of dangerous simples, a drying rack for prepared compounds.
  - Patience compounds a specific nerve remedy for Dr. Winslow twice yearly. The primary active ingredient of this compound can, in sufficient concentration, cause a death that presents as natural — particularly in a man already known to drink.
  - The town's notable institutions: the Town House (government, market), Old South Meeting House, Long Wharf, various taverns (the Bunch of Grapes on King Street is reputable; others less so).

THEMATIC THREADS
  - Visibility and invisibility: who gets to notice things, who is noticed, who is overlooked.
  - The cost of competence in a world that prefers its women quiet.
  - The tension between public piety and private appetite.
  - Complicity: Patience may have unknowingly supplied the instrument of murder.

UNRESOLVED TENSIONS
  - Did Dr. Winslow kill Cooke using Patience's compound? Who supplied it to him and how?
  - Why did Winslow seek Cooke out at the tavern — was it planned, or opportunistic?
  - Why was Cooke's body left specifically in Patience's alley?
  - Why did Thomas walk to the wrong side of the garden — what drew him there?
  - Who searched Patience's drying rack, and why only look (not take)?
  - Did Pratt not call Winslow because he couldn't find him, or because he knew?

TIMELINE & STATE
  Current location: Cornhill Street apothecary shop; Bunch of Grapes (visited Ch. 2)
  Time position: Night of 14 February 1712 / into 15 February
  Key events so far:
    Ch. 1 — Body of Alderman Cooke found in Patience's alley. Constable rules cold and drink. Patience notes no frost on lips, sweet smell — identifies it from reference books as something administered. Decides to investigate.
    Ch. 2 — Patience goes to the Bunch of Grapes. Serving girl Nell reveals Cooke left with Dr. Winslow, who came in late and sought him out. Patience connects Winslow to the nerve compound she makes for him. Returns home to find garden gate open and drying rack searched — someone confirmed what she stocks. They did not take more.

READER PROMISES
  - The real cause of death will be revealed and it will be consistent with clues already visible.
  - Patience's restraint will eventually cost her something.
  - Thomas knows more than he's said.
  - The compound Patience makes for Winslow will be central to the resolution.
  - The body being left in Patience's alley specifically was not random.
