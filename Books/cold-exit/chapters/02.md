The Ferret goes in at 08:14 ship time. I watch the borehole open on my secondary display — the drill head engaging the surface at a spot 200 meters east of the Mouth, in a stretch of undifferentiated chondrite that the surface scan says is unremarkable in every direction except down. The haptic rig gives me a low grinding through my palms, the rock's resistance translated into sensation, and I think: good. This is work I know how to do.

The lag at this range is negligible. Eleven milliseconds. I can pilot the Ferret in something close to real time, which is a small mercy. On some jobs, out past the relay network, you work with 30-second delays and you might as well be guiding it by letter. This is almost like being there.

I remind myself: *almost* like. Not there.

At 200 meters I hit a layer of water ice and have to adjust the approach angle to spiral around it rather than through it — the Ferret isn't rated for ice at depth, the expansion risk is non-trivial, and I was in EOD long enough to develop a deep personal dislike of non-trivial risks. At 350 meters I hit a silicate inclusion the size of a truck. At 470 meters the rock opens up into a natural void, a pocket of nothing roughly two meters across that the drill passes through in four seconds, its forward camera showing me darkness in the darkness.

Normal. All of it normal. I know what an asteroid looks like from the inside. I have spent the better part of four years making asteroids show me their insides, and they always look like this: ice and silicate and nickel-iron inclusions and the occasional gas pocket from a volatile that outgassed slowly into nothing and got trapped. Ancient. Random. Indifferent.

At 1,200 meters the Ferret's density probe starts climbing.

I lean forward. Not much — I'm aware of myself leaning and I correct, because leaning at a readout does not make the readout change, but I lean anyway. The numbers are still within natural range at this depth — just high. The kind of high that would get you excited on a mineral survey. I stay calm.

At 1,800 meters something changes in the rock.

I notice it first through the drill's resistance: harder than it should be, given the composition readings I'm getting. The silicate percentage is dropping, but not because I've hit a nickel-iron vein — the composition readout isn't showing me metal, not exactly. It's showing me something the spectrometer can't resolve. Unknown. The instrument suggests contamination and flags it for my attention, as if I might have missed the part where an 800-meter object that shouldn't exist was buried under here.

I flag the flag as acknowledged and keep going.

The rock is warm.

That's the next thing — the temperature differential shows up at 2,100 meters, subtle enough that I check the probe's calibration twice before I accept it. The ambient temperature in this rock should be hovering around 4 Kelvin. The sensor is reading 7.3 Kelvin at the borehole wall. Not dangerous. Not dramatic. Just: warmer than a rock that has spent four billion years in the outer dark has any business being.

I am now 600 meters above the Object.

The last 400 meters take eleven minutes because I am going slowly and because at 2,400 meters the rock stops being rock entirely, or stops being only rock, and I don't have words yet for what it becomes. The drill bit is still eating through it, but the stuff it's eating has been — worked on, somehow. Changed. The natural crystal structure of the chondrite has been altered in a direction I can't categorize. Under the forward camera's light, the walls of the borehole have a faint regularity to them, not quite geometric but not random either, the way wood grain is not quite random.

And then, at 2,690 meters, the Ferret's forward camera shows me the Object.

I don't say anything. I'm alone, so there's no one to say anything to, but still. I don't even make a sound.

The surface of the Object fills the bottom of the borehole. In the camera's LED illumination it is the color of deep water, which is to say not quite any color I have a precise name for. It absorbs the light across a spectrum that the spectrometer struggles with — some of the wavelengths just seem to go in and not come back, and the ones that do return carry information the instrument's library can't match to anything. The surface is not smooth. There are structures on it — not machined exactly, but deliberate, in the way that a bone's shape is deliberate, grown toward a purpose. Ridges. Recesses. And running between them, barely visible at this scale, a seam: a join in the surface geometry, long and slightly curved, that the analytical part of my brain identifies as a probable boundary between two sections of a larger assembly.

A hatch, maybe.

I hold the Ferret at 11 meters from the surface and run full spectrum scans for ninety seconds. I am being methodical. I am not, I notice, scared — I am past the speed of fear, into the calmer territory on the other side where everything becomes information and information is manageable.

Then the Ferret's systems go offline.

All of them. Simultaneously. Camera, sensors, motor controls, comms — blank. I am looking at a dead display. My hand finds the abort override by muscle memory and sits there, two fingers on the lever, because if I pull it without active comms I'm firing the emergency tether blind and I might not reel in anything useful, but I can reel in something, I can get a physical sample, I can —

3.7 seconds.

Then the display comes back. All systems nominal. The Ferret is intact, hovering, all sensors reading clean.

Except: the heading marker has changed.

I stare at it. The Ferret is now pointed 14 degrees off my last commanded vector. In real terms this means it has rotated — something rotated it — to aim its forward camera directly at the seam in the Object's surface. At the possible hatch. At the join in the geometry that looked, from 11 meters above, like the kind of thing that might, under the right circumstances, open.

The Ferret floats there, patient, pointed at a door.

I sit back in my chair and I put my hands in my lap and I look at my hands for a while.

Then I look at the display.

The Object waited four billion years for someone to knock. I knocked. And whatever is on the other side of that surface just told me, in the clearest possible language it had available, exactly where to look.
