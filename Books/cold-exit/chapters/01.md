The mass spectrometer lies to me sometimes. Not out of malice — it's a nine-year-old instrument bolted to the hull of a ship that's seen worse — but out of fatigue, the same way old sensors start misreading background radiation as signal. I've learned to run the diagnostics first and ask questions second. It saves time and it saves the particular exhaustion of getting excited about nothing.

So when the scan of 4-Kep-7741's southern hemisphere returns a density figure that makes no physical sense, I run the diagnostics.

Clean. All green. Sensor suite nominal.

I run them again.

Still clean.

I pull up the raw numbers and stare at them in the way I've learned to stare at things that might kill me: without letting my pulse do anything stupid. The reading says that approximately 2.7 kilometers beneath the surface of this unremarkable carbonaceous lump — a forty-kilometer potato of nickel, silicate, and frozen volatiles — there is a region of material with an apparent density of roughly 38,000 tonnes per cubic meter.

For comparison, osmium — the densest naturally occurring element on the periodic table — runs about 22.

White dwarf matter runs around a million.

Nothing I have ever surveyed, nothing in the geological literature, nothing I can find in three minutes of searching the Yevgenia's reference database, comes close to 38,000 t/m³ in a cold rock drifting in the outer belt at 4 Kelvin ambient.

I close the reference database. I open the raw scan data and look at it again. The anomaly is approximately 800 meters along its principal axis. Egg-shaped, or close to it. Buried under 2.7 kilometers of ordinary carbonaceous chondrite, sitting there in the dark, dense as a secret.

"Okay," I say aloud. My voice sounds weird in the cabin. It always sounds weird when I talk to myself — I never sounded weird to Tomasz, somehow. There was always somewhere for the sound to go.

I get up and make coffee. This is not avoidance. This is procedure. When you find something that doesn't make sense, you give yourself ninety seconds to do something physical before you decide what to do about it, because the first thirty seconds of any anomaly response are mostly adrenaline and adrenaline makes you stupid. Tomasz used to roll his eyes at me for this. *You and your procedures, Kez.* He had a point. I have a lot of procedures.

The coffee is the freeze-dried kind and it tastes like hot brown water with ambitions, but I drink it standing at the porthole and I look at the asteroid outside.

4-Kep-7741 fills most of the view. At this range — 8 kilometers — it looks like a wall, a dark and cratered wall with a slow rotation that takes sixteen hours to complete. I've been staring at it for nineteen days. I know its major impact craters by informal name. The big one near the southern pole I've been calling the Mouth because it looks like one, open and waiting. The cluster of three overlapping craters near the equator are the Eyes. I do this on every survey because naming things is how you stop being afraid of them and start just working on them.

I have not yet named whatever is 2.7 kilometers inside it.

I finish the coffee. The adrenaline is mostly gone, replaced by something colder and more careful — the same state I learned in EOD training, where they taught us that a device you don't understand is still a device, and devices have rules, and your job is to figure out the rules before the rules figure you out.

My comm relay window is four days, two hours away. Nearest buoy at standard burn. I could push the drive a little and shave maybe six hours off that, but that's still essentially four days in either direction. No one knows I'm here. No one knows what I've found.

Discoverer's rights are clear: first filed claim takes the find. If I burn for the relay and something else — another surveyor, a corporate sweep drone, a lucky random scan — pings this rock before I get back, the claim goes to them. That's the law and that's the game.

On the other hand, the thing in the rock is not a mineral deposit.

I stand there for a while, thinking about that. The thing in the rock is not a mineral deposit, which means discoverer's rights law does not cleanly apply, which means I am in territory where the law has not yet caught up to the situation, which means whoever controls the narrative of what happened here controls everything that follows. I was a sailor once and I know how narratives get controlled and I know what it costs the person who doesn't control them.

I set down my mug.

I go to the Ferret's bay — a cupboard-sized compartment aft of the main cabin — and I run a diagnostic on the probe. The Ferret is a good instrument: a 40-centimeter autonomous drill unit with a full sensor package, designed to core up to 4 kilometers into soft-to-medium rock. I've used it on 31 surveys without a major failure. It can be piloted remotely or run on autonomous search pattern. It has enough battery for 18 hours of continuous operation.

The math is obvious. I can get a direct sensor reading from close range without committing to anything. No burns, no relay, no notification. Just the Ferret going into the rock while I stay up here and watch.

I'm running the pre-launch checklist when it happens.

A vibration moves through the Yevgenia's hull. Not mechanical — the drives are off, there's nothing running that would produce a resonance like this. It moves through the ship the way sound moves through water: in a wave, felt more than heard, a single pulse that comes up through the deck and into my boots and is gone.

I check the hull integrity readout. No breach. No change in external pressure.

I check the seismic sensors I have passively monitoring 4-Kep-7741's surface.

One event. Point source at approximately — I let the computer triangulate — 2.71 kilometers depth, directly below my position.

Single pulse. No precursor, no aftershock. Not random.

I stay very still for a long moment.

Then I go back to the Ferret's pre-launch checklist, because I was in EOD for seven years and I know that the worst thing you can do when something unexpected happens is stop moving. You work the problem. You do not let the problem work you.

The asteroid fills the porthole, dark and patient. Waiting.

I keep checking boxes.
