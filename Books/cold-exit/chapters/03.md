I override the heading.

It takes three keystrokes — cancel autonomous, set vector, confirm — and the Ferret rotates 14 degrees back to my commanded angle, camera pointing at the center of the Object's surface rather than at the seam. A small correction. Eleven meters away from something that has been here since before the first fish pulled itself onto a Terran shore, and I am arguing with it about where to look.

I note, as I do it, that I am doing it deliberately. Not because I think my stubbornness is going to matter to whatever is inside that structure. But because I need to know that my hands are still on the controls.

Then I get to work.

The exterior scan takes forty minutes. I run it in methodical sweeps, the way I was trained — outermost layer first, work inward, build a picture from the largest scale to the smallest. The Ferret's sensor suite wasn't designed for this, not exactly. It was designed for mineral analysis of core samples and rock faces. But it has a thermal imager, a spectral analyzer, an acoustic probe, and a fine-resolution surface camera, and I have used those instruments to learn everything there is to know about a great many rocks, and I am going to use them now.

The thermal map comes first. I already knew the Object was warmer than the surrounding rock, but the image resolves the gradient: it runs along the Object's length, higher at the rounded end opposite the seam, lower toward the stern. Not a large difference — 0.4 Kelvin between bow and stern, against an ambient of 7.3 Kelvin in the altered rock. In absolute terms, negligible. In terms of what it means for a sealed object that has been buried in one of the coldest places in the outer belt: something inside is concentrated toward the front. Something that produces heat. An engine, or an analog for one.

The acoustic profile takes longer to extract. The Ferret's probe has to average against the ambient noise of the borehole — rock stress, my own drill cuts, the faint mechanical sounds of the probe itself — and what I end up with, after filtering, is a signal so close to the noise floor that I run it three times before I'm confident it's real. A subsonic oscillation. Roughly 0.8 hertz, which is to say less than one cycle per second, which is to say the kind of frequency you feel in your chest before you hear it in your ears. Steady. Periodic. Not random. The Object is doing something on a 1.25-second cycle that produces a tiny, regular compression wave in the rock immediately surrounding it.

Breathing, I think, and then I catch myself thinking it and file it under *not useful*.

The surface analysis is where it gets strange.

At survey resolution — the level I use for mineral classification — the surface looks like what I described to myself on first contact: ridges, recesses, a geometry that's deliberate without being machined. Regular in the way a nautilus shell is regular, grown toward a function rather than stamped out of a die. But I have the Ferret's camera at maximum magnification, and I have forty minutes, and I spend the last fifteen of them on the surface itself.

There are structures at smaller scales. Much smaller — visible only at maximum zoom, distributed across the ridged surface in a pattern I can't resolve at full extent but can see in section. Repeating elements, not random, arranged in what might be rows if I could pull back far enough to see the whole. They don't look like script. They don't look like circuitry. They look the way a thing looks when it has a function that you can recognize the category of without understanding the specifics. Like seeing a lock and knowing it's a lock without having the key or even knowing what the key looks like.

I spend four minutes cataloguing the section I can see, flagging it in the data record, and making myself stop before I spend another hour on it and accomplish nothing.

Then the borehole walls creak.

It's a sound that comes up through the Ferret's contact microphones and through the Yevgenia's hull sensors almost simultaneously — a low, heavy sound, not a crack, more like the settling of a building in cold weather. Something in the 400 meters of altered rock between me and the surface relaxed, fractionally. Not a collapse. Nothing moved dangerously. But the material around the Object shifted in a way that rock at this temperature and pressure, after four billion years of stasis, has no geological reason to shift.

I note it. I continue.

At the forty-minute mark I pull up the Ferret's system status to plan my next move.

The battery indicator reads 7 hours, 14 minutes.

I look at this for a moment. Then I go back through the log to find when the reading changed. The answer is: 9 minutes ago, while I was cataloguing the surface markings. The change was instantaneous — one log entry showing 14 hours, 22 minutes; the next entry, 4 seconds later, showing 7 hours, 14 minutes. No heat spike in the thermal log. No EM discharge in the electromagnetic sensors. No mechanical work detected. The power didn't go anywhere I can find. It simply stopped being there.

The Ferret has 7 hours of battery left. It took 22 minutes to drill to this depth, and it will take roughly 22 minutes to drill back out. I have, in practical terms, about 6 hours and 15 minutes to do whatever I'm going to do 11 meters from this object.

I sit with my hands in my lap — I keep doing this, I notice; it's what I do when I'm preventing myself from touching something — and I think about what I know.

The Object pulsed when I was above it, before the Ferret went in. It redirected the Ferret when it got close. It settled the surrounding rock while I was scanning. And now it has cut my operating window in half with a mechanism I cannot detect or describe.

Each of those things is an action. Actions imply intent or at least process — something happening inside that thing, running through something like a decision tree, producing something like responses.

Seven hours.

The seam is still 11 meters away. The bow is still 0.4 Kelvin warmer than the stern. The oscillation is still cycling at 0.8 hertz, patient and steady, like something waiting to exhale.

I look at the seam for a long time.

Then I start making a list.
