Gram gave them porridge in a clay cup each for the road, without comment. Maret paid and tied the horses and said nothing about the night's conversation. Davan tied his corner of the canvas and said nothing either. The wagon moved north in the gray of early morning, before the light had settled into anything.

The road aged as they went. The farmland behind them now — they were in the border region, where the land hadn't decided what it was yet. The fields gave way to rough pasture and then to open hillside, and the marker stones here were older, some replaced with newer ones and some leaning in the slow lean of a thing that had been doing it for a century. The road was well-kept, which meant something used it, but there were fewer wagons than the day before, fewer riders. The sound of wheels on stone felt like it carried farther.

Davan watched the stones. Counted them without meaning to.

---

The luck-shrine was where the road forked — a painted post at the junction, the Compact's interlocked rings on one face and a local saint's mark on the other. A clay dish at the base held a scattering of copper coins, three knotted cords, a dried sprig of something yellow. Beside it on a low stool sat a woman who might have been sixty-five, unremarkable, with a basket of cords in her lap and the look of a person who had been sitting in that specific place for a long time.

Maret drew up without being asked. Whether she always stopped at shrines or just stopped at this one, Davan didn't know.

"Cord for the road?" the woman said. Same tone as *porridge at sundown*. A service.

"Two," Maret said, and paid.

The woman set her basket down and picked up the first cord — plain, undyed, knotted in the middle — and reached for Maret's wrist. Maret offered it without looking; she was watching the fork, calculating, the way she always watched roads.

The woman tied the cord and said something brief about good roads and fair weather, the kind of words that had been said at that post for as long as the post had stood. Then she took the second cord and turned to Davan.

She looked at his hands first, where they rested on the strap of his satchel. She took his wrist — her grip was dry and light — and began to tie.

She talked while she worked, the same road-blessings, the same fair weather. And then she paused, not in any dramatic way, just a pause in the way of a person who has noticed something and is deciding whether to say it.

"Scribe's hands," she said. Not to him exactly. Partly to herself. "Ink stays in the lines, doesn't it. Even after."

She finished the knot. She pressed it once with her thumb, the way you'd test a rope you'd tied, and let go of his wrist.

"Left fork for Aldenmere," she said. "Right fork comes back to itself." She picked up her basket and began sorting through the cords in it, and that was all.

---

Maret clicked her tongue at Stub. The wagon moved.

They took the left fork.

Davan looked at the cord on his wrist. Undyed, plain, knotted once in the middle. The woman had said it the way she might have said *the road runs long* or *take water where you find it* — practical, impersonal, not offered as mystery. Just a thing she had noticed. Scribe's hands. Ink in the lines.

He looked at his hands. She was right, of course. The ink was there — in the fine lines of his knuckles, at the base of his first finger on the right hand, at the edge of his thumbnail. Years of it. He had scrubbed his hands clean every night and it made no difference. The ink was in the lines.

He thought about the originals. The archive room in Mael & Sons, where everything went — every document he had ever copied, filed in wooden boxes by year, by client, by type. The boxes that were ash now. The originals that were ash.

His copies were not ash. His copies were in his satchel, under his hand, because he had the habit of keeping them and the habit of keeping them had been in place since he was twenty-two years old and had never seemed like a significant thing. It was just what he did. A scribe's habit.

The ink was in the lines. Even after.

He did not think the woman at the shrine knew what he was carrying. He did not think she had meant to say anything about it. He thought she had looked at his hands the way a person looks at anything long enough — and said what she saw, which was: a scribe, marked by what he'd written, carrying it with him whether he meant to or not.

That was not magic. That was just accurate.

"She does that," Maret said, from beside him.

"Does what?"

"Says the thing." She flicked the reins. Reliable twitched an ear. "She's been at that fork for thirty years. She says the thing you're carrying. Usually it's small. Weather worries, a sick sheep. Something people haven't said out loud yet."

Davan looked at the cord.

"Does it help?"

"Depends on what you do with it," Maret said. She said it as information.

The road went on ahead of them, left fork, the right one falling away behind. The marker stones here were newer — someone had replaced them recently, within the last few years. Compact work, standard issue. A sign of either good administration or recent concern.

Aldenmere was half a day north.

The cord sat on his wrist, knotted once. He stopped looking at it. The road went forward the way roads do, and whatever it was going to ask of him, it hadn't asked it yet.
