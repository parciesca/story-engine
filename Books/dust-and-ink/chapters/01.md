The wagon listed to the right, which Maret said was normal, and Davan had been trying to believe her since yesterday morning.

He sat beside her on the board, the road unreeling ahead of them in the pale early light, and tried to find a position that distributed his weight more charitably across whatever passed for a seat beneath the thin plank. There was no such position. He had concluded this in the first hour and had spent the hours since accepting it.

"You're sitting wrong," Maret said, without looking at him.

"I've been sitting my whole life."

"You've been sitting on stools and chairs. This is different." She adjusted her own posture slightly — some shift of the hips that looked involuntary but produced, he suspected, a significant mechanical advantage. "Stop fighting it."

He stopped fighting it. The wagon shifted under him in a way that felt like the beginning of a catastrophe and then resolved into something almost tolerable.

Stub and Reliable pulled them forward through the morning. Stub was the short-legged one, brown and solid, who set the pace and seemed indifferent to everything except the grass at the road's edge. Reliable, the grey, was of a more volatile temperament. He had already shied twice from birds this morning, which Maret absorbed with the resigned patience of long familiarity.

"Why is the reliable one called Reliable?" Davan had asked yesterday.

"My husband named him," Maret had said, and said nothing else, and the way she'd said it was its own complete answer.

The Aldenmere road ran straight through country that had been farmland long enough to forget it was anything else. Stone walls divided the fields, each built from rocks pulled out of the soil over the course of a hundred years of planting, and the walls listed at angles that suggested they'd been built in argument with the earth and had reached a grudging compromise. Hedgerows grew where the walls left off. Crows sat on everything.

Davan had his satchel between his feet. It was the one thing he'd saved from the fire — grabbed it reflexively on his way out of the building, the strap in his hand before he was properly awake to what was happening. He had three contracts in there, two grammar texts, a pot of ink stoppered with wax, four trimmed quills, and a copy of everything he'd transcribed in the last month. The copy was habit. He'd been copying documents for five years and he'd kept a duplicate of every one that passed through his hands, in a separate archive — which the fire had taken — and in his satchel, which it hadn't. He did not examine the habit closely. It was the kind of thing that had seemed useful someday, and someday had not yet arrived.

His house was ash. So was everything else he'd owned.

Maret had found him sitting outside what was left of Mael & Sons with his satchel on his knees, and she had said: *I need a scribe who can read elvish formal script at the Aldenmere toll gates. Eight days' work, meals and lodging, six copper a day.* He had said yes before she finished the sentence.

---

The league-marker at the bottom of a long hill had been defaced. Someone had taken a chisel or a sharp stone to the face of the post — the Compact sigil, the interlocked rings that meant *this road is maintained under common treaty*, was scratched through in several deep gouges. The rest of the stone was untouched.

Maret pulled up long enough to look at it, then clicked Stub and Reliable back to pace.

"How old is that?" Davan asked.

"Week, maybe two. The stone's still bright where it was scratched."

"Who does that?"

She was quiet for a moment. "People who want to make a point. Or people who want other people to know a point's been made." She didn't elaborate, and he understood that she was not being evasive — she genuinely wasn't sure which it was, and she'd said all she had to say.

---

The dragon appeared mid-morning, when the road crested a long ridge and the sky opened up to the south.

Davan saw it because he was looking up — something he'd been doing since they left Vardene, because the sky was bigger out here than it had any right to be. It was high, much higher than he'd imagined they flew, a shape against the blue that his mind first categorized as a very large bird before his mind caught up with itself. The wingspan was wrong. The silhouette was wrong. Nothing with a wingspan that wide flew with that economy of motion.

"Maret."

"I see it."

She had already stopped the wagon. Stub and Reliable were still, ears back, the unreliable one for once living up to his name.

The dragon moved west-to-east across the sky, unhurried, as though it had simply decided to be somewhere else and was attending to that decision. Davan couldn't tell color or detail at this distance. Just the shape and the immensity of it, and a feeling — not fear exactly, but the thing that lived next door to fear and occasionally borrowed cups of sugar.

"That's the wrong direction," Maret said.

"For what?"

"For where it's supposed to be." She watched it until it was gone behind the eastern hills. Then she picked up the reins. "The corridor's north of here by forty miles."

She said it lightly, as a statement of fact, which meant she wasn't going to tell him what it implied.

---

The Sweetwater waystation was a two-room stone building at a crossroads, with a well that justified the name and a long shed with room for six animals. Two other wagons were already tied up in the shed when they arrived for midday rest. A family with a heavy load of wool was eating on the bench outside. A woman sat alone at the one interior table, with a wooden bowl and a piece of bread and the particular stillness of someone who is not interested in being spoken to.

She was dressed in road clothes — thick wool, good boots, a cloak with the dust of several days on it. Her hair was dark and straight and pinned back practically. But there was something in the lines of her face that Davan couldn't quite resolve: a proportional quality, a slight elongation in the jaw and brow, that didn't fully read as human.

Maret got bread and hard cheese from the wayside keeper and they ate outside, standing. She didn't look at the woman through the door. She didn't make a point of not looking, either.

"Elf?" Davan said quietly.

"Outcast, maybe. Or traveler." Maret took a bite of bread. "Don't stare."

He looked at the road instead, at the place where the marker stone would have been if they hadn't passed it two hours back. He thought about the shape against the sky. He thought about the satchel at his feet, the familiar weight of it, all those copies he'd made of all those documents he'd thought were ordinary.

The road stretched on toward Aldenmere. The day was getting long.

He had a feeling — precise and useless, the kind he was good at — that he had said yes to something considerably larger than eight days of toll-gate work, and that it was already too late to say anything else.
