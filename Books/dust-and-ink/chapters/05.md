The silence went on long enough for a cart to pass on the southern road — axle-groan, the clop of hooves, going away.

Then Maret came around the wagon.

She had finished with Stub. She had an apple left in one hand and a look that said she had read what she'd walked into and was not making a thing of it. She stopped at Davan's elbow without quite touching him.

Perrin looked from Davan to Maret. Back to Davan. He did it the way a man checks a sky he has read a thousand times — not for surprise, just for accuracy.

"Another time, then," he said. "Good roads to you both."

He picked up the jug and filled a cup and set it in front of Maret without asking whether she wanted it. She took it, drank, set it back on the plank.

"Thank you, Perrin."

"Maret Ashvale."

She put the last apple on the plank beside the cups and walked to the wagon and got up.

---

They were past the crossroads before she said anything.

"That was a long silence," she said, in the voice of the day.

Not a question. Davan had his satchel across his lap instead of in the back with the rest of his things, which was where it had been yesterday and the day before. He had moved it this morning without deciding to. He was aware of it as a weight, the way a man becomes aware of a word he keeps not saying.

"There's something I should tell you," he said.

Maret drove. The road ran straight here between summer hedges. Stub went steadily. Reliable was in one of his good hours, which was a mercy.

"Go on."

Davan told her. Not tidily — he hadn't rehearsed this version — but accurately.

He kept copies of everything he'd ever transcribed. Professional habit, picked up when he was an apprentice: his master said if a scribe could be blamed for an error, a scribe should be able to prove the error wasn't his. Mael had permitted it because it meant mistakes could be traced. The copies were Davan's own; he kept them in a locked chest in his room above the house. The copies included a document with two sigils he had asked Maret about — indirectly, in the voice of a man asking out of idle curiosity about grammar texts.

He had the copy in his satchel. Now.

Maret drove. A plover crossed the road ahead; Stub watched it without breaking step.

"The nested arcs," she said.

"Yes."

"The grammar text you were looking at."

"That was— no. I was lying about that."

She drove for a moment.

"I know," she said.

---

She asked who had commissioned it.

He told her: the work had come in through Mael himself, which was unusual. Mael ran the house; he didn't usually hand-deliver assignments. He had set the papers on Davan's desk without a word and gone. The client name on the paperwork was Aldric Thorne, merchant of Vardene, Grain Lane. Davan had looked him up afterward, out of habit.

"He's dead," Maret said.

"Three years. I found the notice in the civic ledger. He had a counting house; it folded the autumn before last." Davan turned the satchel strap over in his hands. "Someone used his name. There's a seal — a doubled V inside a circle, not registered with any guild I know of. Someone had paperwork drawn up in a dead man's name and sent it to a copying house and asked for Mael to assign it to a specific scribe."

"To you."

"To me."

The hedge on the right gave way to a field of cut hay, the rows still showing. A farmhouse sat back from the road a quarter mile, smoke from the kitchen.

"Were there other pieces," Maret said, "with the same seal."

Not quite a question.

"Two, in the past year. One was a land survey — agricultural land near the southern end of the Vrethian Corridor. I copied it and thought nothing of it; surveys are common. The second I didn't take myself." He stopped.

Maret waited.

"I was ill. Another scribe took it. Benne Farr." He set the strap down and put his hands on his knees. "Benne was in the house when it burned."

The road went on. Stub's ears moved at something in the hedge. Reliable had begun to look faintly suspicious of the hay field, which was not an urgent concern.

---

"What do you know about the fire," Maret said.

"I left early. I usually stayed to second bell but that night I had a headache. I left at first." He watched the hedge. "I came back in the morning and the front of the building was open to the sky. The city watch said it started in the archive room."

"Where the originals were kept."

"Every original I'd ever transcribed for them. Five years of Mael's archive." He didn't look at the satchel. "They can't know I kept copies. Or—"

"Or they'd have come for you before now." Maret said it evenly. "Or they do know, and they're watching where you go."

He had understood this in his bones since the morning of the fire. He had not said it out loud before, or heard it said, and there was a difference.

He looked at the road. Marker stone ahead — the interlocked rings, chipped on the left edge, still legible. He had started noticing whether markers were intact sometime in the past four days, and he did not think he would stop.

"I hired you," Maret said, "because you were affordable and you could read."

"Yes."

"You could have told me this when you hired on."

"I know."

She drove. The marker stone passed. Another would come in a league.

"Well," Maret said.

She didn't say what the *well* was attached to. She flicked the reins at Stub. Reliable made a sound like a man who has been told the weather is going to turn.

The road went on the way roads do: without opinion, in both directions, and quite a long way.
