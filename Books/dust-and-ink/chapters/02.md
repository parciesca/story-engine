The waystation at Whetham was smaller than Sweetwater and better run. A low stone building with a slate roof, a yard kept swept, a keeper who did not ask questions and charged three copper for a place on the floor by the hearth. Maret knew him by name — Hollis — and they exchanged four sentences, all of them about the roads, before she paid and the matter was settled.

Davan hauled the satchel in. Hollis pointed at a corner already spread with two clean sacks of straw, a wool blanket folded on each. It was not luxury. After the wagon bench it was paradise.

They ate supper outside on the bench. Bread, cheese, a bowl of bean stew from a pot that had been hanging over the fire since noon and would hang there until the last traveler came in. A dog wandered past them looking for crumbs, received none, and moved on without complaint. The sky worked its way through several shades of purple.

"The dragon today," Maret said.

Davan waited.

"Might have been nothing." She tore a piece of bread. "Dragons lose their way. Weather turns them. Sometimes one takes a detour to look at something — sheep, smoke, a curious-looking tree. They're allowed to be curious."

"You don't think it was that."

She chewed. "I think nothing specific. I think things that look alike mostly turn out to be alike. So." She shrugged. "We saw a dragon. It was a strange day."

"And the marker."

"Someone was angry at a rock. The rock is still there."

It was not a dismissal. It was the way she packaged worry so that it fit in a pocket. He had already observed her doing this several times, and it had occurred to him that it was a skill — possibly *the* skill — that had kept her moving on this road for four years alone.

He wanted to ask about the woman at Sweetwater. He decided not to.

---

Maret was asleep within minutes. Her breathing went slow and even in the way of a person who had long ago made peace with beds that were not beds. Hollis had left a small taper on the sideboard for travelers who needed one. Davan rose quietly, took it, and brought it back to his corner.

He sat with his back against the wall. He opened the satchel.

He had done this every night on the road so far — just an inventory, the scribe's version of a prayer. It calmed him. Three contracts, present. Two grammar texts, present. Ink pot, stoppered. Quills, four, still sharp. The bundle of copies, tied with twine he had tied himself.

He untied the twine.

He did not know why he untied it tonight. He had not untied it any of the previous nights. He would think about this later.

The top of the stack was a two-year tenancy for a cooper on Pelter Street, copied last week. Below that a grain contract. Below that the inventory of a dead woman's estate, the most recent thing he had finished before the fire. He thumbed past them gently. He had copied each; he knew each the way a mason knows the walls he has built.

He found it seven down in the stack. He had written on the back, in his own small hand: *C.A. Addendum, per Mael's instruction. Twelfth of Vreth. — D.O.*

Three days before the fire.

He remembered: Mael himself had brought it out of the back office, which Mael never did, and had set it on Davan's desk and said: *Clean copy. Twice. One for me, one for the hand. Take your time and be neat.* And Mael had stood there until Davan said yes, and had then gone back into the office and closed the door.

It had been three pages. Compact Plain, with a few formal flourishes in the body — an attestation structure Davan had seen once before in a military contract out of Vardene. Not exotic. The kind of prose that was trying to sound important.

He had copied it in an afternoon. He had made his personal copy without thinking. Habit, not decision.

He held his copy to the taper now and read it as a reader, not a scribe. The body was as he remembered: formal, self-important, vague in the specific places where documents were vague when they had been written by committee. He had no context for what it meant. He skimmed.

He stopped at the header.

Four sigils. In the top margin of the first page, above the date line — four marks in a row, slightly smaller than the text below.

The first was the Compact's interlocked rings. Clean, standard, the same rings that had been scratched off the marker stone at the bottom of the hill. He had copied that sigil a thousand times.

The second was the Warden's Chancellor mark — a small stylized cross within a circle, used on documents reviewed by the Chancellor's office at Aldenmere. Standard.

The third sigil was not Compact.

The fourth sigil was not Compact either.

Davan held the taper closer.

He was a scribe trained in four scripts. Common, Elvish formal (read, not spoken), Old Compact — which the Chancellor's office occasionally still used for ceremony — and Merchant's Mark. He knew the shape families of all four. He knew the way a stroke sat on its line in each; he knew which scripts drew with the tip and which with the flat; he knew what kinds of flourish belonged where.

The third and fourth sigils were not in any of them. They weren't even in the neighborhood. They had the deliberate quality of professional notation — a draftsman's precision — but the underlying geometry was wrong. One of them had a nested arc pattern that resolved into a small glyph at the center. The other was a single mark made of three intersecting curves that met at a point he couldn't quite locate.

He looked at them for a long time.

He did not know what they were.

He did not know who would.

Somewhere outside, something moved in the straw of the stable — a horse shifting, most likely. Reliable, probably. A small sound, followed by nothing.

Davan closed the document. He tied the twine back around the bundle with hands that he noted, with mild surprise, were not quite steady. He put the copies back in the satchel. He snuffed the taper.

He lay down on the blanket. He looked at the ceiling, which was beams and straw and the moving shadow of the banked hearth, and he tried to think about the person who had written the third sigil, and the person who had written the fourth, and what they had been to each other when they signed this document together.

He was still thinking when he finally slept.
