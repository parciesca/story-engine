Davan had slept badly. He had slept badly in the way of a person who makes a decision and then watches himself carrying it into the next morning, second-guessing every step of the carriage.

The decision was this: he would ask her.

He would ask her as a scribe asked things, idly, the way a professional turns a tool in his hand to see it in the light. He had rehearsed it between the hours of moonset and dawn. He had four versions.

He watched Hollis bank the hearth at first light. He did not think Hollis looked at him longer than necessary. He also did not entirely trust his own reading at the moment.

They loaded the wagon in the pale damp. Maret checked Stub's harness and then Reliable's and did not comment on the fact that Reliable was standing more steadily than he usually did, as if ashamed of the day before. She handed Davan the canvas that went over the back. He tied his corner neatly. Yesterday his corner would have been sloppy.

"Breakfast," she said, and jerked her chin toward the bench.

Hollis had left them bowls of porridge and a small loaf and a pot of tea. Davan ate the porridge as if it were a performance — spoonful, spoonful, swallow. His hands were fine. It was his face he was not sure about.

He waited until she was halfway through the bread. He had decided — one of the four versions — that the middle of her bread was the neutral moment.

"Odd thing, last night," he said. "I was looking through some of my copies — old habit, it steadies me on the road — and it occurred to me to wonder something."

Maret chewed.

"There are four scripts I was trained in. Common. Elvish formal — I read it, I don't speak it. Old Compact, for ceremony. Merchant's Mark. Do you know if people use anything else? For trade, I mean. Administrative things. Papers that might cross a toll gate."

He said it looking at his porridge. He did not look up when he finished. He took another spoonful because he had planned to.

Maret did not answer for a moment. She finished her bread. She picked up her tea and drank.

"Two," she said.

He nodded slowly, as if this were an ordinary thing to be told.

"One is dragon script," she said. "You won't see it on a trade paper. It shows up on sealed dispatches — Warden office to Warden office. I've seen it through the glass of the gatehouse at Aldenmere twice in four years. Once on a packet the Dragon Warden's clerk was carrying. Once on a folded square a courier had to show, without unfolding, to get through the gate."

"Anyone at the gate can read it?"

"No. The Warden's own clerk, maybe. Nobody else." She set her tea down. "The second is something I've only heard of. Old-road talk. They say the Arcanate uses a script for certain dealings with certain dragon lineages — the old ones, the ones who were signatories. I don't know the name of it. I don't know anyone who's seen it. It might not exist."

She said this all the way she said everything. Flat, exact, the same voice she used to describe the weather or a crack in the wagon axle.

"Why are you asking?"

The question arrived gently. Not suspicious. Not especially curious. She asked it the way she would have asked whether he wanted more porridge.

He had rehearsed the answer four times. He said the shortest one.

"Just curiosity. I was looking at a piece of old script I couldn't identify, in one of the grammar texts I carry. It nagged at me."

Maret nodded.

It was a nod he watched more carefully than he had watched anything of hers in two days, and the watching itself was a small betrayal of the lie. Her nod was not belief. Her nod was not disbelief. It was a movement the shape of *understood*, and the *understood* was filing the question in a place where she could find it again.

She drank the last of her tea.

"We should get on," she said.

---

They were on the wagon and past the first bend before she spoke again, and when she did it was in the voice of the day.

"Arden Crossroads by midday. There's a cider stand — an old man runs it, cups the color of tea. Worth stopping for."

"Alright."

"Reliable likes the apples."

"Alright."

She flicked the reins. Stub and Reliable went forward.

Davan looked at the road because the road was safe. He looked at the fields because the fields had no opinions. He looked at Maret's hands on the reins because her hands had told him nothing yesterday that he had been able to read.

He did not look at the sky for a long time, and when he finally did, it was empty.

The absence of the dragon was almost louder than the dragon had been.

He thought about what Maret had said — dragon script, on sealed dispatches between Warden offices. Rare. Unreadable. He thought about the shape of the third sigil in the header of his copy, nested arcs resolving to a glyph at the center.

He thought about the fourth sigil — three intersecting curves — and the rumor of a script used between the Arcanate and certain old dragon lineages.

He thought about how the two marks had sat beside each other, in the same row, in the same header.

He did not have a word for what that meant. He was not sure there was one.

He watched Maret's hands on the reins for another quarter hour, and then he stopped watching, because whatever he was going to do next, he was going to have to do it without her help.

He did not yet know what that was.
