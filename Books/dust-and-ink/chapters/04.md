They were an hour from Arden Crossroads when the sheep came.

They came in the way sheep come everywhere, which is to say: slowly, inevitably, and not entirely in one direction. Forty or so of them, gray-brown and muddy in the legs, poured across the road from a gate in the left-hand hedge and paused in the middle of the road in the belief that they were now somewhere they had meant to be.

Maret pulled up and set the brake without ceremony. Stub took this as a chance to eat grass. Reliable took it as a chance to be offended by sheep.

"Easy," Maret said to Reliable, and then, to the sheep, "you're fine," as though the sheep had been the ones worried.

A boy about ten came through the gate at a jog, carrying a stick half his height and followed by a black-and-white dog who moved with the focused malice of a junior officer. The dog dropped and crawled, flanked the flock from the downhill side, and drove them rightward across the road toward a second gate on the other side. The boy called things to the dog that had no syllables Davan recognized. The dog listened with visible contempt and did what it was going to do anyway.

"Morning," the boy said, cheerful, walking past the wagon. "Sorry. She gives orders, Bill doesn't take them."

"Bill?" Davan said.

"Bill's the dog."

"And she's?"

"The boss sheep." The boy gestured at a heavy-faced ewe in the middle of the flock. "I'm just in charge of the gate."

The sheep cleared. The boy raised his stick in salute and was gone through the far gate before Stub was done with the grass.

Maret clicked her tongue at Stub. Stub finished the bite he was already working on and moved.

"Good dog," Maret said, to no one.

"Good sheep," Davan said.

"Good *gate.*" She flicked the reins. Reliable, relieved that nothing further was being asked of him, went back to doing the job he was named for.

---

The crossroads came into view not long after, from the top of a gentle rise: four roads meeting at a broad paved circle that had been built long enough ago that the paving stones had settled in patterns you could read like a map of bad winters. The Compact marker at the center of the circle was intact — Davan noticed this with a small internal flinch, the flinch of a man who now noticed whether markers were intact. It stood plain, the interlocked rings, clean.

Under a willow at the southeast corner sat a small shelter with a roof of woven reeds, and beneath it a plank table and a man who could have been any age between seventy and a hundred. He was small. His hands were large in a way that hands sometimes are on small old men, as though his hands had kept the size they had been at forty and the rest of him had gone on ahead.

He had a pot of apples at his feet and a brown jug on the plank and half a dozen cups in a row, unpainted earthenware, and the cups were exactly the color of strong tea.

Maret drew up. "Perrin."

The old man raised a hand. "Maret Ashvale."

"How's the cider."

"It's cider."

She got down. Davan got down. Maret went directly to the apples, fished out three, and walked them back to Reliable, who received them with the dignity of a statesman accepting a minor petition.

Davan approached the plank. Perrin was watching him the way a man watches a bird — without effort, without particular interest, but without missing anything.

"Cup, young man?"

"Please."

Perrin poured. His hand was steady. The cider was cool and cloudy and tasted of late autumn, which was a neat trick for summer.

Davan paid. Three copper, into a small wooden dish. Perrin slid the dish off the table and into a pocket without looking.

Davan meant to step back. He meant to take his cider and stand beside the wagon and drink it and say nothing. He had decided this on the walk from the wagon. He had been very clear about it.

"May I ask you something."

Perrin waited.

"A pattern I saw somewhere." Davan turned the cup in his hands. "Nested arcs. Several of them, inside each other, resolving to a single mark at the center — a small glyph, I think; I couldn't quite see the shape. Is that — does that mean anything, as writing? Is it any script you've seen?"

He asked it looking at the cider.

Perrin did not answer for a moment. The moment was long enough for Davan to hear the wind in the willow, to hear Reliable crunching apple, to hear Maret murmuring something to Stub about the breadth of his belly.

Then Perrin said, quietly:

"Where did you see that?"

His voice had not changed. It was the same voice he had said *it's cider* in. But the question was a different kind of question, and Davan knew it the way a man knows when the floor of a room has shifted an inch without any sound.

He looked up from the cup.

The old man was looking at him. The look was level, and neutral, and completely awake.

The willow moved in the wind. Davan's cider was still cool in his hand. He had not answered.
