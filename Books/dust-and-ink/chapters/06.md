The road changed in the middle of the afternoon — not with a line, not with a sign, but in the way that light shifts and the fields grow rougher and the marker stones older and the hedges give way to drystone walls, and at some point you realize the country has changed around you without asking.

Maret noticed it the way she noticed everything useful, which was without comment. She named the waystation they'd be stopping at — Brenford, she said, run by a woman named Gram, floor space for four copper, good water. Davan noted it and said nothing and watched the older stones pass.

They had not talked much since morning. The morning's conversation was the kind that needed to settle before anything else could be said about it.

---

Brenford waystation was built from the same gray stone as the walls along the road here, in the same century by the look of it. Swept yard, low door, old smoke. The woman named Gram was perhaps fifty, efficient, not unfriendly, and had the look of a person who had seen every kind of traveler and stopped thinking about it long ago.

"Four copper for two. Seven for the stable. Water's free. Porridge at sundown."

Maret paid. They got the horses settled. Inside, a tinker's family had claimed the warmest corner — three small children already asleep on a pallet. Davan set his satchel between himself and the wall.

Gram brought porridge in clay bowls and settled at the hearthside in the way of keepers who have given up pretending they don't listen.

"You come from south?"

"Vardene," Maret said.

"Thought so." Gram scraped the bottom of her pot. "Had a rider through this morning, asking about a wagon out of Vardene. Two aboard. Merchant and a clerk, he said." She offered this without emphasis, the way she might mention weather.

Davan kept his eyes on the porridge.

"We're for Cressholm," Maret said. "Market there Thursday. You know the road time from the north fork?"

"Half a day, give or take." Gram set the pot down. "Not Aldenmere, then."

"Not this trip."

Gram nodded and said nothing more about it.

---

The fire burned down. The tinker's children slept. The tinker himself nodded off over a cold bowl.

When Gram was across the room banking the coals for the night, Maret spoke. Her voice was quiet enough that Davan felt it more than heard it.

"What did the rider look like?"

Gram answered without turning. "Ordinary. Dark coat, good horse. One of those with a seal ring — right hand. Sat the saddle like a man used to riding."

"Thank you," Maret said.

Gram grunted and went through to the back.

---

Davan lay on his back in the dark and put it together.

The rider had been here in the morning. They had left the Arden Crossroads after midday. The rider could not have overtaken them from behind — the road had been open and straight, the kind of road where you see a horse coming a quarter mile off. The rider had not followed them. The rider had been ahead of them, waiting at the waystation they would reach, asking about a merchant wagon with two aboard.

Either someone knew which road they were on, or there were several of them on several roads, and the net was wider than he had understood.

He stared at the beams above him. A seal ring, right hand. Not Compact civil service — those went on the left. Not any merchant association he knew. Not the Scribes' Register, whose marks were left-hand, middle finger, same as his own had been; he had taken his off the morning after the fire and put it in his satchel without knowing why, and had not put it back.

"Cressholm," he said, very quietly.

"Doesn't exist," Maret said. "Or it does, but the market's not Thursday. Gram won't know. She doesn't go that far."

"But whoever's asking will."

"Whoever's asking will follow a wrong road for half a day and come back."

She said it flat, as information. Not comfort — just the shape of a half a day. Which was apparently what they had.

Davan turned the seal ring in his mind. The doubled V inside a circle on the commission paperwork. A ring on a rider's right hand. He didn't know if they were the same mark; Gram's description told him nothing useful. But there were only so many parties with an interest in a wagon out of Vardene carrying what this wagon carried.

"In the morning," Maret said, "the manifest says pottery and dye-stuffs, bound for Aldenmere, consignment for the usual buyers. My name on the paperwork, your name as clerk."

"That's what it already says."

"I know. I'm saying: that's what it says. That's all it says."

He understood. Nothing else, no matter what anyone asked. Not the document. Not the sigils. Not the fire. Not an old man at a cider stand at the Arden Crossroads. A merchant and a clerk with pottery and dye-stuffs.

Outside, the wind moved along the eaves. One of the tinker's children turned in sleep and was quiet again.

"The ring could be nothing," Davan said.

"Yes," Maret said.

She did not say she believed that. She was either asleep already or a very good impression of it. Davan lay on his back and watched the dark between the beams and thought about the road that was a day and a half long, and the rider who had known to wait at the end of it.

The road went north. There was only one road.
