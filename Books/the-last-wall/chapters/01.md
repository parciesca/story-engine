The last thing Aleksios Doukanas saw before Urban's cannon spoke was a pelican, banking low over the Golden Horn in the grey pre-dawn, indifferent to everything below.

He was standing on the outer peribolos wall, between the third military gate and the Blachernae gate, where the Theodosian triple walls finally gave way to the older Blachernae section — single-thick, higher, and, as Giustiniani had muttered three days running, "the problem." The Genoese commander stood ten feet away, wrapped in a heavy cloak against the April cold, watching the treeline to the west. He had not slept. Aleksios could tell from the way he stood, weight forward, as if sleep were something that happened to other men.

"Tell the kastrophylax his archers need to stop shooting at shadows," Giustiniani said, without turning. His Genoese Italian carried the particular flatness of a man managing his own impatience.

Aleksios put it into Greek and delivered it to the kastrophylax — a heavyset man named Loukas who had commanded this section of wall for eleven years and received this information with the expression of someone being told the price of salt.

"Tell the Frankish lord," Loukas said, "that my archers shot at shadows because the shadows were moving."

Aleksios translated this also. Giustiniani grunted, which was a kind of answer.

This had been Aleksios's work for six days: the narrow space between languages, carrying words across a gap that was not merely linguistic. His father was Genoese by birth, his mother Doukaïna by blood — a name that once meant something in this city, before it meant mostly debt. He had grown up translating at dinner tables, in merchant houses, in the corridors of the Blachernae palace where the Emperor kept his increasingly hypothetical court. He was twenty-three years old and had been, until very recently, good at keeping out of the way of important things.

Then the Ottoman host arrived.

It came at dawn. Not gradually — one moment the western plain was grey and featureless, and then the sun lifted over the city behind them and lit it all at once. Eighty thousand men in ordered camps, cavalry on the flanks, the Janissary corps in their white felt caps massed at the center, and before all of them, dragged by sixty oxen over three weeks of roads, the thing that had made the walls shake two nights ago simply by being moved into position.

The cannon was twenty-seven feet long. Urban of Transylvania had cast it in three months at Edirne, and men said it could throw a stone ball weighing twelve hundred pounds a mile. Aleksios did not know if this was true. He was about to find out.

Giustiniani went very still when he saw it.

"How many men does it take to reload?" he asked.

"Two hundred," said one of his lieutenants. "Perhaps three hours between shots."

"Then we have three hours to repair whatever it does." He turned to Aleksios without turning all the way. "Tell the kastrophylax I want his masons at the breach the moment the dust settles. Not after. The moment."

Aleksios told him. Loukas had already turned and was shouting at someone below.

The cannon fired at the seventh hour.

Aleksios had heard thunder, and ships' guns, and once a powder magazine go up in the harbor at Galata. None of them prepared him for this. The sound was not sound — it was a displacement, a pressure that pushed through his chest and briefly unmade his hearing. He grabbed the merlon in front of him. Beside him, a soldier sat down hard on the wallwalk with blood coming from his ear, looking confused.

The stone ball — he never saw it — hit the lower scarp of the outer wall. The impact threw a column of dust and pulverized limestone forty feet into the air. When it cleared, there was a notch in the outer wall wide enough to drive an ox-cart through.

Three hours later, the masons were in it, passing baskets of rubble hand to hand.

He ran the message to the Venetian bailo at the tenth hour — the Emperor's request, carefully worded so as not to sound like a request, for the Venetian galleys in the harbor to move into position along the sea walls. The bailo, Girolamo Minotto, received it in his counting house with the expression of a man tallying costs. He asked two questions about the cannon's reload time. He said he would send word to the captains by evening prayer.

Aleksios left through the Phanar district, because it was faster, and passed the warehouse his father had leased for thirty years on the waterfront off the Golden Horn. The doors were open. Inside, crates were being stacked onto a handcart.

His father appeared in the doorway as if he had been watching for him.

"The Trevisano galley takes passengers tomorrow night," Paolo Doukanas said. His Greek was accented after thirty years in the city, permanently. "I have arranged for three."

"Three."

"You, your mother, and myself."

Aleksios looked at the handcart. Silver plate, he thought. The good pieces. "Mother won't go."

"Your mother—"

"Won't go." He met his father's eyes. "You know that."

Paolo Doukanas looked at his son for a long moment. He was a careful man, a methodical man, a man who had made a fortune in a dying city by understanding which way things were going before other people did. He did not argue. "The offer holds until tomorrow night," he said, and went back inside.

At vespers, walking back along the land walls, Aleksios saw the Emperor.

Constantine XI was fifty-eight and looked older. He was walking the Blachernae section alone — no guards visible, though they were certainly there — in a plain soldier's cloak, trailing his hand along the stone as he walked. He stopped at the point where the new breach had been mortared over with rubble and fresh limestone, still pale against the old dark stone, and stood looking at it for a time.

Then he walked on.

Aleksios did not approach him. There was nothing to say. The repair would hold until morning. Tomorrow Urban's cannon would fire again.
