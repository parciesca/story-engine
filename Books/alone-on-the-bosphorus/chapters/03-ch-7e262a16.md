The ferry crossing took less than an hour.

Cornelis stood at the bow of the flat-bottomed *vapur* with his horse — *Amsterdam*, who was indifferent to water in a way that suggested good sense — and watched Istanbul recede across the Bosphorus. The city from the water was something else entirely: the domes of Hagia Sophia and the Sultan Ahmet mosque rising in a kind of geological patience above the skyline, the minarets thin and exact as compass needles, the whole of it reflected in the straits with a wobbling, impermanent beauty that he understood he would not see again for some time. He felt something that was not quite homesickness — he had not been home in fourteen months, and home was in the other direction — but was perhaps its cousin. The loss of something known.

Then the Asian shore came up beneath the hull and there was the business of unloading, and the city was behind him.

---

They were seven in the caravan: Yusuf and his nephew Mehmet, who was sixteen and handled the pack animals with a competence that made Cornelis quietly ashamed of his own relationship with *Amsterdam*; two hired guards whose names Cornelis could not yet pronounce reliably and who regarded him with a professional neutrality that was neither welcoming nor hostile; a cloth merchant named Ibrahim who was travelling as far as İznik and seemed to find Cornelis mildly amusing; and a woman of indeterminate age who Ibrahim referred to only as *the widow* and who travelled in a covered cart and spoke to no one.

Yusuf rode at the front and set the pace, which was unhurried. This surprised Cornelis, who had imagined — he saw now that he had imagined the whole enterprise from the outside, as a series of transactions rather than an experience — that a merchant would be anxious about time. But Yusuf seemed to understand something about roads that Cornelis did not yet, which was that the road rewarded patience and punished urgency, that a horse pushed too hard arrived lame, that a man who did not sleep enough made bad decisions in the market.

They rose before light and rode through the cool of the morning. By midday they stopped at whatever shade presented itself — a stand of plane trees, a ruined caravanserai, once simply the shadow side of a long hill — and waited out the worst of the sun. In the late afternoon they rode again, the light going amber and long across the countryside, and stopped for the night at villages when they could, in the open when they couldn't.

He had expected — again, he had expected the thing before the thing, the idea of Asia Minor rather than Asia Minor itself — something barren, or strange, or dramatically foreign. What he found instead in those first days was a countryside that looked, in its rhythms if not its particulars, oddly like the inland parts of any civilized land: cultivated fields coming green in the April warmth, olive trees on south-facing slopes, villages of whitewashed stone where dogs came out to regard the caravan with suspicion and children came out to regard it with uncomplicated delight. Women carried water jars on their heads. Men guided wooden plows behind patient oxen. The smell was thyme and horse dung and somewhere ahead, always, the faint cool promise of water.

What was different was harder to name. A quality of light, perhaps — sharper than Dutch light, which was always softer, filtered through clouds and the particular grey generosity of northern skies. Here the shadows were definite. Things stood out from their backgrounds with an almost aggressive clarity. The white of a minaret against a blue sky was not the white of anything he had seen before.

And the sounds. The call to prayer reaching them across a valley just before dusk, the muezzin's voice wavering and then steadying into something that was not music exactly but was not not music either, and the caravan stopping — not formally, just slowing, the men who prayed moving to the side of the road to do so — and Cornelis sitting on *Amsterdam* and not quite knowing where to put himself.

He wrote in his ledger in the evenings. He had begun, without deciding to, to use the blank pages at the back for something other than accounts. Small observations. The Turkish word Mehmet had taught him that day. The name of a plant Ibrahim had pointed out. The way the road curved south through a valley of fig trees and then climbed sharply and suddenly presented, over a ridge, a view of Bursa's distant plain that made him stop his horse without meaning to.

He was recording something. He was not sure yet what.

---

Yusuf began talking to him on the third day.

Before that there had been functional communication — the accounts Cornelis was supposed to maintain, questions about the pack animals' loads, decisions about the route — but on the third evening, at a *han* outside a village whose name Cornelis could not catch, Yusuf sat down across the fire and asked him, in their shared Italian, what he had been doing in Istanbul.

Cornelis explained. The VOC. The trading voyage. The *Altın Ay* and its consequences.

Yusuf listened without interrupting, which was a quality Cornelis had already noticed in him, and then said: "And before the ship?"

"I was a clerk in Amsterdam. For a merchant house. Wool, mostly."

"Your father?"

"A schoolmaster. In Leiden."

Yusuf considered this. "And you chose trade."

"I was good at numbers," Cornelis said. "And the VOC offered—" he gestured vaguely at the night sky, at the road, at Anatolia in general— "this."

Yusuf made the sound that Cornelis was beginning to recognize as his version of a laugh — not a laugh exactly, more a brief exhalation of acknowledgment. "Yes," he said. "It does offer this."

Silence. The fire. Somewhere in the darkness a nightingale was producing an amount of sound disproportionate to its size.

"You keep notes," Yusuf said. He had seen Cornelis writing.

"A habit."

"What do you write?"

Cornelis thought about how to explain this in Italian. "What I see," he said. "What I don't understand yet."

Yusuf looked at him steadily. In the firelight his face was all shadow and plane, the face of a man who had spent decades reading road and weather and the intentions of other merchants. "That is a good habit," he said. "Most men write only what they already understand."

He stood, picked up his cup, and walked to the other side of the fire to talk to one of the guards, and the conversation was over, though Cornelis continued to think about it for some time.

---

The trade itself he learned in pieces.

Yusuf dealt primarily in what he called *kuru mal* — dry goods, the unglamorous backbone of the caravan trade: beeswax, dried figs, leather, raw wool, blocks of alum from the mines near Bursa that the European cloth merchants needed for their dyes. He had contacts at each stop, men who had known his father and now knew him, relationships maintained across years by the consistent honoring of agreed prices and the occasional small gift brought from the road.

"The Venetians come to Istanbul," he explained one afternoon, riding side by side on a stretch of road wide enough for it, "and they think they are buying from Istanbul. But Istanbul does not make the wax. Istanbul does not grow the figs. Istanbul is just the door." He gestured back, roughly west. "We are what is behind the door."

Cornelis thought about this in accountant's terms: the difference between the price at origin and the price at the door. He had spent his career so far on the Istanbul side of that equation, which meant he had understood only the door.

"How far back does it go?" he asked. "The supply."

Yusuf made his almost-laugh. "How far east do you want to go?"

It was not quite an invitation. Cornelis filed it under *things not yet understood* and wrote it in the back of the ledger that evening.

---

On the eighth day they came over a ridge in the late afternoon and Bursa was below them.

The city lay against the long flank of UludaÄŸ — the Great Mountain, Ibrahim told him, which the ancient Greeks had called Olympus of Bithynia — in a way that suggested it had grown there rather than been built, the minaret clusters rising from the green of fruit orchards and the silver-grey of olive groves, the old city walls still visible, the silk market's *bedesten* somewhere in the center of it all sending up a thin haze of commerce even at this distance. Beyond the city the plain stretched south and west, flatlands going hazy in the late light.

Mehmet said something in Turkish, and even without the words Cornelis understood it as the thing a young man says when he sees home from the road. Yusuf smiled — a real smile, the one he reserved for things that were not transactions.

Cornelis opened his ledger. Looked at the figure at the top of his account column. Looked at Bursa.

He wrote: *Day 8. City of silk and old empire. The road here felt longer than the numbers say.*

He closed the ledger and rode down the hill toward the city.
