The road east of Karapınar was the same road it had been before, which was the strange thing.

The same plateau light, the same wind with its plateau indifference, the same pale thread running southeast through the long grass and occasional stands of poplar that marked where water was underground. *Amsterdam* had his opinions about the wind as always. The pack animals moved at their pace. Mehmet rode at the front with Yusuf and occasionally looked back at Cornelis with an expression that was not quite respect and not quite amusement but occupied the territory between them.

What was different was Cornelis.

He could not have specified it precisely — he was a man who liked to specify things precisely, and the imprecision bothered him mildly in the way that an unbalanced column bothers an accountant even when the sum is close enough for practical purposes. But something had shifted in Karapınar that was not reversible. The body that had been thrown twice and thrown a man once was not the body that had ridden out of Istanbul four months ago, and it was not only the bruises, which had faded to yellow at the edges and would be gone by Kayseri. It was something in the posture. The way he sat *Amsterdam* — Mehmet's corrections had accumulated, the door was becoming a rider. The way he looked at the road rather than across it.

He rode one morning without opening the ledger. This had not happened before.

---

Yusuf noticed. Yusuf noticed most things and said something about perhaps a third of them, which was a ratio Cornelis had come to understand as deliberate.

"You are quieter," Yusuf said, on the third morning out of Karapınar, riding alongside.

"I am thinking," Cornelis said.

"About what?"

Cornelis considered the honest answer. "About the difference between knowing a thing and having done it." He paused. "I knew what the plateau was before we crossed it. I had read accounts. I had seen maps. I thought this was knowledge."

Yusuf said nothing, which was his way of indicating that Cornelis should continue.

"It wasn't knowledge," Cornelis said. "It was the shape of knowledge. The container without the thing inside."

Yusuf rode for a moment. "In Turkish," he said, "we have a word — *tecrübe.* It means experience, but not quite. It means the knowledge that comes through the body, through doing. It is considered a different category from what is learned from books."

"We have no such word in Dutch," Cornelis said.

"I know," said Yusuf.

They rode on. The plateau went about its plateau business around them, enormous and unhurried.

"Yusuf," Cornelis said, after a while.

"Yes."

"The man in Kayseri. The accounts that require care." He had been carrying this since Konya. "Tell me what I'm riding into."

Yusuf was quiet long enough that Cornelis thought he might decline. Then: "His name is Adem Åžahin. He has been trading on the eastern routes for thirty years — beyond Kayseri, into the passes, toward Tabriz when the roads allow and the politics allow, which is not always simultaneously." He paused. "He is very wealthy and not entirely comfortable with what being very wealthy requires him to do. His accounts reflect this discomfort. They are not dishonest — Adem is not dishonest, this is important to understand — but they are the accounts of a man who has made many arrangements that he would prefer not to examine too closely."

"And you want me to examine them."

"I want you to organize them. Make them legible. There is a difference."

Cornelis thought about this. "What is he afraid he'll find?"

Yusuf almost laughed. "He is afraid he will find that he is richer than he thinks, because some of that richness has obligations attached that he has been successfully not thinking about."

"And if he is?"

"Then he will need to decide what to do about the obligations. That is not your problem. Your problem is the ledger." He glanced at Cornelis. "Can you do that? Look at accounts and report what is there, without advising what should be done about it?"

Cornelis thought about the Leiden professor who had taught him to read Rumi. About the Dutch Reformed church's very specific opinions about usury and obligation. About nine coins and a ledger on a quay in Istanbul.

"Yes," he said. "I can do that."

---

Kayseri arrived on the ninth day as a dark mass against the improbable backdrop of Mount Erciyes — a volcanic cone, snow-capped even now in early summer, rising from the plain with the clean geometric confidence of something that had decided to be a mountain and seen no reason to be modest about it. The city at its foot was old in the way that plateau cities were old: not loudly, not ornamentally, but with a settled permanence that made Istanbul feel, by comparison, almost recent.

This had been the Hittites. This had been the Persians. Alexander had passed through. The Romans had fortified it. The Byzantines had held it until they hadn't. The Seljuks, then the Mongols, then the Ottomans — the city had been claimed by so many successive certainties about how the world was organized that it had developed, Cornelis sensed on entering it, a quality of patient scepticism about all of them. History had happened here and would continue to happen here, and Kayseri's job was to outlast it.

The stonework was the dark volcanic basalt of the mountain — whole streets of it, the buildings absorbing the light rather than reflecting it, giving the city a density that Bursa's warm sandstone and Istanbul's imperial marble did not have. The market was smaller than Konya's but more concentrated, the goods from further east present in a way he had not seen before: spices whose names he didn't know, textiles in patterns that did not come from the plateau, a smell in certain alleys that he associated with his reading about the silk road rather than anything he had actually encountered.

He was further from Amsterdam than he had allowed himself to think about directly.

---

Adem Åžahin's house was in the old quarter, built against the city wall — a substantial house, the kind that announced its owner's wealth through quality rather than display. The wooden screens on the upper windows were finely made. The courtyard garden, glimpsed through the gate, had a fountain that worked. These were the details, Cornelis had learned, that meant something on the plateau.

Adem himself was not what Cornelis had constructed from Yusuf's description.

He had expected — the word *uncomfortable* had lodged in his mind — someone visibly burdened, a man with the look of accounts-anxiety that he recognized from certain Amsterdam merchants who had grown faster than their record-keeping. What he found instead was a man of perhaps sixty, lean and upright with a white beard trimmed close and eyes that were simultaneously warm and measuring, who greeted Yusuf with the embrace of a genuine old friendship and turned to Cornelis with an attention that was immediate and specific.

"The Dutchman," he said, in Arabic-inflected Turkish that Cornelis caught most of.

"The factor," Yusuf said, using the word again.

Adem looked at Cornelis for a moment, then said, in careful and surprising Italian: "Can you keep a confidence?"

Cornelis met his eyes. "I keep accounts," he said. "Accounts are by nature confidential."

Something in Adem's expression settled, the way a balance settles when the weights are correctly placed. He nodded once, stepped back, and gestured toward the interior of the house.

"Come then," he said. "We will eat first. The accounts have waited two seasons. They can wait another hour."

---

They ate in the courtyard beside the fountain, the mountain visible above the roofline in the long evening light, the water sound underneath the conversation. The meal was elaborate in a way that Cornelis had not encountered since Istanbul — course following course, each announced by a servant who seemed to take the production personally. Adem ate with the appetite of a man who had been hungry enough times in his life to take abundance seriously.

He asked Cornelis questions through the meal — not about the accounts, not about trade, but about the Netherlands. The sea. The VOC and its methods. The way European merchants organized their companies, their joint-stock arrangements, the separation of investors from operators. He asked with the focused curiosity of a man who had been thinking about these questions for some time and had not previously had a useful interlocutor.

Cornelis answered honestly, including the parts that were unflattering to the VOC, which were several. Adem listened to all of it and said nothing evaluative, only asked the next question.

Yusuf ate and said almost nothing and watched them both.

"Your company left you here," Adem said eventually, not unkindly.

"By accident," Cornelis said.

"Yes." Adem looked at the mountain. "And yet here you are in Kayseri, nine days from Konya, eating my food and about to look at my accounts." He refilled Cornelis's cup himself, which was a gesture of something. "Accident is an interesting word. I have been a merchant for thirty years and I am not sure I believe in it."

Cornelis thought about the *Altın Ay* and the hornpipe and the tide. "What do you believe in instead?"

Adem smiled — the first full smile of the evening. "Readiness," he said. "Some men are ready when the moment comes and some men are not. The moment does not choose. The man chooses, beforehand, by how he has lived." He set down his cup. "You were ready to be left behind. You did not know it. The ship knew."

Cornelis had no answer for this. He was not sure he agreed with it. He was not sure he disagreed.

After dinner Adem led him to a room off the courtyard — a study, the walls lined with the kind of organized disorder that meant a man spent real time here. On the central table were the accounts.

They were considerable. Four bound ledgers and a stack of loose correspondence, the whole thing covering perhaps a decade of trade. Cornelis sat down, looked at the topmost page, and felt the specific settling of attention that good work produces in a person suited to it — not excitement exactly, but a kind of alignment, the right instrument applied to the right problem.

He looked up. Adem was in the doorway.

"How long?" Adem asked.

Cornelis assessed the stack. "A week. Perhaps ten days, if there are things I need to ask you about."

Adem nodded. "You will stay here. Yusuf also." He paused. "Ask me whatever you need to ask. I have decided that I want to know."

He left.

Cornelis turned back to the ledgers. Opened the first one. Found the earliest date — twelve years ago, a caravan manifest from a road east of Kayseri, somewhere called Sivas — and began.

Outside the courtyard fountain maintained its steady account of the water moving through the city, and the mountain held its position above the roofline, and the evening deepened around the house of Adem Åžahin while the Dutchman with the ledger did what he had always been made to do, in a place he had never imagined being, on a night that no one had planned.
