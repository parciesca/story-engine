The Dutch trading house in Galata smelled like home — pine resin, old wool, the faint ghost of Gouda — and Cornelis stood in the doorway for a moment before entering, breathing it in like a man surfacing from water.

The factor who received him was named Hendrick Pauw, a heavyset man in his fifties who had been in Istanbul long enough that his Dutch had taken on a faint Ottoman lilt without him noticing. He listened to Cornelis's account with the expression of a man who has heard variations of this story before — not unsympathetic, but not surprised either. Ships left men behind. The Bosphorus had a long memory for such things.

"The *Zeeland Ster* made the morning tide," Pauw confirmed, not unkindly. "The harbourmaster's log has her cleared before the sixth hour. I'm told there was some confusion about your whereabouts." He paused. "You were at the *Altın Ay*?"

"I was," Cornelis admitted.

Pauw nodded. Said nothing further on the subject.

The news was not good, though it was delivered with the smooth efficiency of a man accustomed to managing disappointment on behalf of the company. The next VOC vessel expected in Istanbul was the *Noord-Holland*, but she was running the Alexandria route first and would not make the Bosphorus before October at the earliest — possibly November, if the winds off Cyprus ran contrary. It was currently late April.

Six months. Perhaps seven.

"We have contacts," Pauw said, producing a small ledger of his own — smaller than Cornelis's, and considerably more worn. "Local merchants who occasionally require a literate hand. Translation work, correspondence in Latin or French. The French ambassador's secretary sometimes has overflow. The pay is—" he found the page, ran a thumb down a column of figures— "adequate. For modest lodgings and one meal a day."

Cornelis looked at the numbers. His face did something careful and controlled.

Pauw read it accurately. "Istanbul is not a cheap city," he said. "But you will survive until November. We've seen men through worse."

That evening Cornelis sat in the small room Pauw had arranged for him — a clean room, a narrow bed, a window that looked onto a courtyard where a fig tree grew — and opened his ledger to the page he had started on the quay. He added two columns: income projected, expenses projected. He ran the sums twice. The arithmetic was unambiguous. He would arrive in Amsterdam, assuming no unexpected costs, with less than he had left with fourteen months ago. Fourteen months of the eastern trade, and he would return poorer in money and possessed of nothing more than a story about sleeping on cobblestones.

He closed the ledger. He looked at the fig tree.

---

He lasted eleven days on Pauw's list of contacts before the caravan merchant found him.

Or rather — before he found the caravan merchant, which was also, in a sense, the city finding him, which was perhaps how it had always been going to work.

The work Pauw had arranged was real and he did it conscientiously — copying correspondence for a Flemish cloth dealer, translating a legal document from Latin into passable Italian for a Greek Orthodox bishop who seemed faintly embarrassed to need the help. But the money was precisely as meager as Pauw had suggested, and Cornelis, who had been trained to notice discrepancies between what a thing was worth and what it was being sold for, could not stop noticing them.

The discrepancy that caught him was in the *bedesten* — the inner market of the grand bazaar, where the serious trading happened, locked behind iron gates each night. He had gone in looking for nothing in particular, which is the only safe way to enter a bazaar, and had found himself watching a transaction that seemed, by his accounting, to make no sense.

A merchant — Anatolian, from the look of him, travelling clothes still dusty from the road — was selling a consignment of beeswax to a Venetian buyer who was not paying nearly enough attention to what he was buying. The price being agreed was the Istanbul price. The wax had come from somewhere east, somewhere the price was considerably lower, and Cornelis did not need to know where exactly to understand that the margin between those two numbers was significant.

He stood there for a moment doing sums in his head.

Then he did what he had been trained to do: he made himself useful.

His Italian was better than the Anatolian merchant's — a low bar, as it turned out — and when the negotiation stumbled on a question of weights and the two men reached for their respective versions of the same gesture of exasperated incomprehension, Cornelis stepped in and said, in his careful trading Italian, "Perhaps I can assist."

The Venetian buyer was annoyed. The Anatolian merchant — whose name was Yusuf, and who had been on the road for nineteen days and was tired in the way that only long travel produces — looked at this pale young foreigner with an expression that was not quite hope but was adjacent to it.

They sorted out the weights. The transaction concluded. The Venetian departed.

Yusuf looked at Cornelis for a long moment, then said something in Turkish. Cornelis spread his hands apologetically. Yusuf switched to his Italian, which was indeed limited but serviceable for the present purpose.

"You know numbers," he said.

"I am a factor," Cornelis said. "Or I was."

Yusuf absorbed this. "I go back east in three days," he said. "To Bursa first, then into the interior. I have goods to sell and goods to buy and—" he searched for the word— "accounts that become confused." He looked at Cornelis with the particular attention of a practical man assessing a practical problem. "You need money. Yes?"

Cornelis thought of the arithmetic in his ledger. "Yes," he said.

"I pay fairly," said Yusuf. "I have a reputation for this."

"How long?" Cornelis asked.

Yusuf considered. "Bursa is ten days. After Bursa—" he made a gesture that suggested the interior of Anatolia, its distances, its open-endedness— "it depends what we find. What the roads are. What the season does." He looked at Cornelis. "You have somewhere you need to be?"

Cornelis thought of the *Noord-Holland*, arriving in November, maybe, if the winds off Cyprus cooperated.

"Not until October," he said. "At the earliest."

Yusuf nodded, as if this settled something. He named a figure — weekly, in silver — and Cornelis, who had been trained never to accept the first number, almost forgot to negotiate. He caught himself, made a mild counter, and Yusuf accepted it so quickly that Cornelis suspected he had not gone nearly high enough.

They shook hands in the Ottoman fashion, which Cornelis got approximately right.

---

He told Pauw the following morning. Pauw received the news with the mild disapproval of a man whose carefully arranged solution was being discarded, but he was too experienced to argue with the arithmetic.

"Yusuf ibn Malik," he said, when Cornelis gave the name. "Yes, I know him. Reliable. Moves between here and Konya, sometimes further east." He studied Cornelis with the look of a man recalibrating. "You'll need a horse."

"Can I—"

"Against your wages," Pauw said, before the question was finished. "We'll sort it. And Bos—" Cornelis was already turning to go— "be back before the *Noord-Holland*. Don't get distracted."

Cornelis said he wouldn't.

He was, even then, looking at the map on Pauw's wall — the interior spreading out east of the Bosphorus, city names he half-recognized from classical history, trade routes marked in faded ink, mountain ranges that looked, on paper, like the wrinkles in an old man's hand. Bursa, which had been the Ottoman capital before Constantinople fell. Konya, where the whirling dervishes kept their house. Beyond that, the map grew quieter and more approximate.

He picked up his ledger from under his arm. Opened it to the arithmetic page. Drew a single line through the column of small Istanbul wages and replaced it with the new figure.

The numbers, for the first time, looked like something.

Three days later, in the early morning, the city still blue-grey and cool, he rode out through the Anatolian side on a compact bay horse he had named — privately, and with some embarrassment at the sentimentality — *Amsterdam*, and followed Yusuf ibn Malik east.
