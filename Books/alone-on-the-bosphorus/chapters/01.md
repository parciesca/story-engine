His name was Cornelis Bos. He had discovered this again some minutes ago, patting his coat for the ledger and finding it still there, his name inked on the inside cover in his own careful hand — *C. Bos, Junior Factor, V.O.C.* — as if he had anticipated needing the reminder. The coat was damp. The cobblestones beneath him were damp. The dog regarding him from a careful distance was, at least, dry.

He stood. This took longer than it should have.

The street that had seemed, in darkness, to be near the waterfront was in fact several streets removed from it, which meant he had walked in the wrong direction at some point in the night, which meant — he turned, got his bearings from the minarets, turned again — the quay was that way. He walked.

Istanbul in the early morning was not yet fully itself. The great covered markets were shut. The water-sellers had not yet taken their positions. But the waterfront never entirely slept — there were always men moving cargo in the grey hours, always small boats crossing to the Asian side, always the smell of fish and tar and the particular mineral cold that came off the Bosphorus regardless of season. Cornelis followed his nose and the sound of water and came out onto the quay at the precise moment the sun cleared the hills of Anatolia and threw its light across the straits.

He stood there and looked at the water for a long time.

There was no ship.

He looked left, where the Bosphorus opened toward the Golden Horn and the crowded harbor beyond. He looked right, where the straits narrowed and curved toward the Black Sea. He looked straight across at the Asian shore, in case he had somehow confused his directions entirely and the *Zeeland Ster* was anchored there for reasons he could not fathom.

There was no ship.

A fisherman was mending a net twenty feet away, paying him no attention. Cornelis walked to him and said, in Dutch, "The ship. The Dutch ship. Was it here?" The fisherman did not look up. Cornelis said it in French, then in the approximate Portuguese he'd learned in Lisbon two years ago. The fisherman continued mending his net with the patience of a man who had seen Europeans make noise on this quay before and expected to see it again.

Cornelis sat down on a bollard.

The *Zeeland Ster* had been bound for Batavia via Alexandria, and then home. The return cargo was already negotiated — he had done some of the negotiating himself, his first real responsibility, and he had been proud of it in the quiet way he allowed himself small prides. His passage home was on that ship. His employment was on that ship. His trunk, containing his spare coat, his books, his letters from his mother, and every guilder he had saved across fourteen months of the eastern trade, was on that ship.

What he had: one ledger. One coat. A small knife he kept from habit. Whatever remained in his purse, which he now counted — eleven silver coins of uncertain denomination, two of which turned out, on closer inspection, to be buttons.

Nine coins, then. And a ledger. And the morning.

The boat was painted red and green, the wood worn smooth. It disappeared around a point of land and was gone.

Cornelis opened the ledger to the first blank page and, from force of habit more than any useful purpose, wrote the date. Then he wrote: *Ship departed. Circumstances unclear.* He stared at this. He added: *Position: the quay below Galata. Assets: as above. Liabilities: considerable.*

He closed the ledger.

Around him the city was waking up properly now — the calls to prayer had come and gone while he slept on the cobblestones, but the streets were filling, the noise building, the particular layered chaos of a city that had been the center of the world for two hundred years and had not forgotten it. A spice merchant was opening his stall twenty yards away, arranging small mountains of color — ochre, crimson, a yellow so bright it almost hurt. Two women passed in conversation, their voices quick and low. A boy led a donkey loaded with copper pots that rang softly against each other with each step.

None of it was for him. He was scenery here. He was a pale, somewhat crumpled young Dutchman sitting on a bollard with a ledger, and the city moved around him like water around a rock, indifferent, unhurried, already full of its own business.

He had two options, as best he could determine. He could make his way to the Dutch trading house in Galata and throw himself on their mercy — they would have heard of the *Zeeland Ster*, might even know what had happened, might arrange passage home eventually. This was the sensible option. This was what he would do.

He did not move.

The spice merchant caught him staring and stared back, not unfriendly, assessing. The man said something in Turkish, then in what Cornelis slowly recognized as a version of Italian, simplified, the trading-port Italian that was nobody's native tongue and everybody's second language along this coast.

"You are lost," the man said. It was not quite a question.

Cornelis considered the various ways this was true.

"I have missed my ship," he said, in the same simplified Italian.

The merchant nodded slowly, as if this were a reasonable thing to have done. "Where was it going?"

"Home," said Cornelis.

The merchant made a small sound — not quite sympathy, not quite amusement — and went back to arranging his spices. But he did not close the conversation. He left it open, the way a door is left open on a warm day, without obligation, just the possibility of air.

Cornelis sat on the bollard a little longer. The sun was fully up now. The Bosphorus glittered. Somewhere in the city, someone was baking bread, and the smell of it reached him on the wind and made his stomach remind him forcefully of its existence.

He stood. He picked up his ledger. He looked once more at the empty water where his ship had been.

Then he walked toward the spice merchant.
