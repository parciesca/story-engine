The market in Sivas opened while the shadows were still cold.

Cornelis had left Kemal's han at first light with a short list in his head and the pleasant, now-familiar sense of a day with a defined shape. Yusuf was with Kemal, arranging the caravan's place in whatever eastward movement was forming. Mehmet was with the horses. That left Cornelis and the list.

The list was sensible. A small copper pot to replace one whose bottom had developed a suspicion in Kayseri. Salt. Dried apricots, which travelled well and made the last hour of a cold day survivable. A measure of lamp oil. A wool blanket, because the passes ahead would be colder than the plateau, and his current blanket had opinions about moisture that it had not previously disclosed. Rope.

He walked out into the market.

Sivas in the morning light was older than Kayseri in a way that took some time to parse. The stone was the same colour roughly — the eastern grey-brown, the Anatolian weather — but the proportions were set to a different rule. The minarets were taller and thinner. The medrese across from the market had a portal the size of a small house, its facade patterned with geometries that Cornelis's eye, trained by four months of carpet borders, now read almost involuntarily. He recognized — or thought he recognized — two motifs that had migrated from eastern looms to stone. The Seljuks had been here first, and the stone remembered them the way Adem's workshop carpets remembered the hands two centuries gone.

The list went well for a while.

The salt seller called salt *tuz* and weighed it by the okka and nothing unexpected happened. The apricot seller produced three grades and Cornelis chose the second without argument. The oil was mild and came in a stoppered clay jar that fitted his palm. The blanket seller was a woman of middle years who tested the wool with practiced fingers and suggested, by lifting her chin at a particular piece, that the one Cornelis had initially pointed at was adequate and the one she now indicated was better. He took hers.

The copper pot required a short walk across to the metalworkers' row. He found one that would do and paid for it without trouble, the merchant young and talkative, the Turkish perfectly legible.

Then the rope.

He found a shop that smelled of hemp and tar and stood in the doorway for a moment, looking at the coils on the walls and the small skeins stacked on a low bench. The proprietor was an old man — seventy perhaps, possibly more, with the deliberate economy of movement of someone who had found in long practice the smallest motion that achieved each necessary thing. He was working a knot when Cornelis came in. He set it down and waited.

"Good morning," Cornelis said. "I need rope. For the Erzurum road."

The old man looked at him.

"*İp,*" he said, testing the word on his tongue the way Yusuf tested a silk thread. "You said *ip.*"

"Yes."

"For what, on the Erzurum road?"

Cornelis began to say what he had been about to say — *for the horses* — and realized, in the moment before the words left him, that the question contained a correction. He stopped. He thought about what he actually needed rope for, which was several different things: to lash packs to the saddles, to lead a horse in difficult terrain, to haul a bundle up from a cart, to tie down a load against wind on an exposed stretch. He had come in with one word for all of these, and the old man had already seen that this would not be sufficient.

"Several things," Cornelis said. "To tie packs. To lead. To bind a load against wind."

The old man nodded, a small concession.

"*Urgan,*" he said, pointing at a thick dark coil on the wall. "*Sicim.*" A smaller, paler skein. "*Kendir.*" A coarse bundle at the end of the bench. "*İp* is what a child holds. A man on the Erzurum road needs three words."

"Three," Cornelis said.

"Sometimes four." The old man produced from under the bench a flat coil of something that looked more like woven webbing than rope. "*Kolan.* For the girths and the saddlebags, when the leather has given up."

Cornelis stood with this for a moment.

He had spent four months in Kayseri believing his Turkish had become functional — believing that the gap between his competence and a native speaker's was a matter of accent and subtlety and the residual formality that never fully left a late-learned language. He had not considered that in moving east he might find himself in a place where the language itself was more granular, where a road that demanded more specific tools had produced a vocabulary with more specific words, where the western plateau's *ip* was the old Sivas merchant's careless shorthand for distinctions that mattered.

It did not feel like a humbling, exactly. It felt like the moment in Bursa when Selim had put silk in his hand and said *read it* and he had realized there were ten things to know before the reading began.

"Show me what I need," he said.

The old man did. He took down the thick *urgan* and measured and cut two lengths; produced a skein of *sicim* for the smaller lashings; suggested a grade of *kendir* that would survive wet weather better than the other coils; added one length of *kolan* because, he said, Cornelis looked like a man whose saddlebags were older than they admitted. The prices were fair. The transaction took somewhat longer than the others had, partly because the old man explained, with the patient economy of a man who found instruction a form of commerce in itself, which rope was for which job and why.

When Cornelis paid, the old man took the coins without looking at them and said: "The road east has patience for men who ask twice. Ask twice."

"I will."

"And learn the words. The Erzurum side has more than four."

Cornelis walked back through the market with the coils slung over one shoulder, conscious of the new weight and of the shape of the four words in his mouth. *Urgan. Sicim. Kendir. Kolan.* He turned them as he walked, testing their edges the way he had turned *dalāl* on the Kayseri workshop floor, not a week ago.

Four words where he had brought one. The gap between what he had thought he knew and what was needed had opened, briefly, and he had seen its shape. The shape was instructive rather than discouraging. It meant there was still a road to walk in the language, and the language was keeping pace with the road.

He stopped at a baker's stall and bought two *pide* warm from the oven and ate one of them walking, because the morning had earned it.

The briefing came after the midday meal.

They sat in a small room off the han's courtyard — Kemal, Yusuf, Cornelis, and a man Cornelis did not know: a broad-shouldered merchant named Rüstem with the bearing of someone who had made this particular road many times and had developed the settled confidence of a man whose concerns had been reduced to a manageable short list. Kemal had pulled him in as the informed voice on current conditions.

The conditions, it turned out, were workable.

The winter had been ordinary. The snow in the higher passes east of Erzincan was heavier than the previous year but not dangerous. The meltwater would be running in the channels for another ten days; after that it would settle. There were no current reports of banditry on the Sivas–Erzurum stretch, though Rüstem said this with the dry qualification of a man who believed absence of reports was a different thing from absence of danger. The caravan forming out of Sivas was of respectable size — some twenty merchants, a small party of pilgrims, a rotation of soldiers bound for the Erzurum garrison, a handful of travellers whose purposes were unspecified and would remain so. Departure was set for the following dawn, two hours before first light, to make the first way-station before dark.

"You have ridden with Yusuf from Istanbul," Rüstem said to Cornelis. "Nine months. That is enough road for a man to know his horse."

"I know my horse," Cornelis said. Which was true.

"On this road you listen to him. He will tell you when something is wrong before you see it."

"I listen."

Rüstem looked at Yusuf, made a small gesture of approval, and addressed the remaining practical questions to him — water at specific hans, the condition of a particular bridge on the fourth day, which villages would sell good fodder and which would not. Cornelis listened and did not speak. He had learned, in Bursa and Kayseri, that the moment to speak in a room like this was when he had something to add, and he had nothing to add yet.

When the meeting broke, Kemal walked Cornelis to the courtyard door and looked at him with the faintly amused assessment of the previous evening, slightly revised.

"You went to the market," he said.

"I had things to buy."

"Halil said a Dutchman came in asking for *ip* and left with four words." Kemal's mouth did something close to a smile. "He told me on his way to his noon prayer. He does not tell me about every customer."

"I asked twice."

"Good," Kemal said. "The road east rewards that."

He went back inside.

Cornelis stood for a moment in the courtyard. The afternoon light was turning the stonework softer than morning had given it. The caravan would form before dawn. The four words were in the back of his ledger now, written in Dutch with their meanings attached, and beside them a small note:

*In Sivas the language is older and has more words for rope. Assume this about everything east of here.*

It was an instructive morning. It had cost him some copper and taught him a principle he suspected he would need for the rest of the road.
