He found Adem in the carpet workshop at first light.

Not unusual — Adem was often there in the early hours, before the weavers arrived, in the particular quiet of a room that spent its days full of rhythmic industry. He was standing at the far end with a lamp, examining a carpet that had come off the loom the previous week, and he did not look up when Cornelis entered, which meant he had heard the footsteps and identified them.

Cornelis stood beside him and looked at the carpet.

It was a large piece — the biggest loom in the workshop, the one reserved for commissions of significance. The ground was a deep indigo that had taken three separate dyeing sessions to achieve, the pattern a medallion design from further east, one of the pieces in Adem's collection that the weavers had been reproducing from careful study across the winter months. The border carried a motif that Cornelis now recognized — had recognized since January, when Adem had shown him the original in the chest in the study — as coming from a workshop tradition east of Tabriz. Someone had made a carpet with this border two hundred years ago and the pattern had traveled west by being copied, generation to generation, the hands teaching the hands.

"It's good," Cornelis said.

"The indigo is slightly uneven in the third quadrant," Adem said. "You have to know to look."

Cornelis looked. Found it — a barely perceptible variation in the depth of color, a place where the dye had not fully penetrated. "I wouldn't have seen that in January," he said.

"No," Adem agreed. "You wouldn't have."

They stood with it for a moment. Outside, Kayseri was doing what Kayseri did at first light — the calls to prayer finished, the city finding its feet, the smell of bread already present in the cold air. The mountain would be clear today. It had been clear for three days, which Adem had said meant the passes east were open, which meant the road to Sivas was ready, which meant this was the last morning.

"The workshop," Cornelis said. "When you're east — who manages it?"

"My sister's husband. He knows the commercial side. The weavers know the rest." Adem set down the lamp on a workbench, looked at the carpet a final time. "The workshop has been here longer than I have. It will be here after."

Cornelis thought about the pattern traveling west from beyond Tabriz, copied by hands that had never seen the original. He thought about what survived and what didn't, and what the difference was between the two.

"Adem," he said.

"Yes."

"The role you have described for me — on the Tabriz journey, and after. It is not a factor's role. Not in the VOC sense."

"No," Adem said. "It is not."

"I am trying to understand what it is."

Adem was quiet for a moment, in the way he was quiet when the question deserved a considered answer rather than a ready one. "In the Persian tradition," he said finally, "there is a word — *dalāl.* A broker. But not simply a man who stands between buyer and seller for a fee. A *dalāl* is a man who knows the value of a thing independently of what either party believes it to be worth. He carries the true price in his head." He looked at Cornelis. "You are not there yet. But you are learning to carry it."

Cornelis said nothing.

"The Tabriz journey will teach you the other end," Adem said. "You already know what silk is worth in Bursa, in Istanbul, in the European market. After Tabriz you will know what it is worth at the source. Very few men hold both numbers simultaneously." He picked up the lamp. "What a man does with that knowledge is his own business."

He walked toward the workshop door, paused. "The horses are ready at the second hour. Eat something first. The road to Sivas does not improve on an empty stomach."

He left.

Cornelis stood alone in the workshop for a moment, the indigo carpet before him in the grey morning light, the slight imperfection in the third quadrant invisible now without the lamp. He thought about *dalāl* — the word, its weight, the shape of what it named. He turned it in his mind the way he turned new Turkish words, testing its edges.

He thought it might be the first word in any language he had encountered that described what he was becoming.

He went to eat something.

---

The departure had the quality of all departures from places that have held you — slightly unreal, the familiar rendered strange by the knowledge that you are about to stop seeing it. The courtyard fountain, uncovered now that the frosts had passed, resuming its account. The study window, the lamp Adem worked by not yet lit at this hour. The smell of the city's bread and basalt and the cold coming off the mountain.

Mehmet had the horses ready in the street. He had grown across the winter — not just in height but in the way a young man grows when the people around him are worth watching: a new attentiveness in his eyes, a quality of assessment borrowed from Yusuf and not yet fully his own but clearly on its way to being so. He handed Cornelis *Amsterdam*'s reins without comment.

Yusuf was already mounted, looking east.

Adem stood in the doorway of the house. He had said what he had to say in the workshop. He raised one hand now — not a wave exactly, something more economical — and Cornelis raised his in return, and that was sufficient.

They rode out through the basalt streets as the sun cleared the mountain's shoulder and threw long shadows west behind them.

---

The plateau in March was a different country from the plateau in April.

The grass was not yet green — it was the color of old straw, flattened by winter, pressed close to the earth as if still not entirely convinced that the cold was finished. The road was soft in places where the snowmelt had not yet drained, *Amsterdam* picking his way with a care he hadn't needed in the drier months. The sky was the high pale blue of early spring, the light strong but not yet warm, the shadows long until midday and long again by afternoon.

There were more travellers than Cornelis expected.

He had imagined the spring opening of the eastern roads as a gradual thing — a trickle becoming a flow. What he found instead was that everyone who had been waiting out the winter had apparently decided simultaneously that March was sufficient. The road to Sivas was populated: merchants, pilgrims, soldiers rotating to eastern postings, a group of dervishes travelling in a silence so complete it seemed to alter the air around them, two Armenian traders from Kayseri who recognized Adem and fell into conversation beside him for the better part of a morning.

Cornelis rode and listened and caught what he could.

His Turkish was good enough now that travel had become aurally rich in a way the early months had not been. He heard a merchant complaining to his companion about prices in the Kayseri spring market — the specific numbers, the comparison to last year, a theory about why the Venetian buyers had been less present than expected. He heard a soldier explaining something about the garrison at Erzurum to a younger man who was going there for the first time. He heard the dervishes not speaking and understood that this too was a kind of communication.

He rode alongside Yusuf when the road allowed it and they talked — more than they had talked since the early days out of Istanbul, the winter in Kayseri having created a new ease between them. Yusuf asked about the winter's work, the accounts, what Adem had taught. Cornelis answered honestly, including the parts he was still working out.

"*Dalāl,*" he said, on the second afternoon. "Adem used this word."

Yusuf rode for a moment. "Yes," he said. "That is what he is making you."

"Is it what you are?"

Yusuf considered. "I am a merchant. I buy and sell. A *dalāl* does not buy and sell — he knows the price and advises. It is a different relationship to the goods." He glanced at Cornelis. "A *dalāl* needs to trust no one's valuation but his own. This requires knowing more than either party in a transaction, which requires—"

"Having been to both ends," Cornelis said.

"Yes."

The road curved through a shallow valley where a stream ran fast with snowmelt, the sound of it loud enough to require a pause in conversation. When they came out the other side Yusuf said: "Adem was a *dalāl* once. Before the capital accumulated and the arrangements became necessary. He misses it, I think."

"He's making me what he was," Cornelis said.

"He is giving you what he had before he had too much," Yusuf said. "There is a difference."

Cornelis thought about the obligations in the ledgers, the arrangements that had required further arrangements, the discomfort Adem had named clearly and carried quietly. The workshop that had been there before him and would be there after. The carpet traveling west by being copied, the pattern surviving because the hands kept teaching the hands.

What survived was the knowledge. Not the capital, which accumulated and created its own problems. The knowledge of value, carried independently.

He wrote it in the back of the ledger that evening, in Dutch, which he was still saving for the things worth keeping:

*A dalāl. A man who carries the true price. Adem was one before he became something larger and more complicated. He is teaching me to be what he was before the complications. I am not sure he knows he is doing this. I think perhaps he does.*

On the fourth day the terrain changed.

The plateau's long swells gave way to something more broken — the land lifting and folding as the road climbed toward the watershed between the Kızılırmak basin and the upper Euphrates country. The horizons that had been the plateau's constant generosity contracted, the road threading through valleys that required attention in a way the open ground had not. The snow on the higher ground was still substantial, the meltwater running in channels beside the road that hadn't been channels in October.

Sivas appeared on the sixth day in the late afternoon, in a valley broad enough to have recalled the plateau's scale — a substantial city, the old Seljuk architecture visible in the tile work of the medrese facades, the minarets different in proportion from the Ottoman style Cornelis had become accustomed to. Older. The Seljuks had built here three hundred years before the Ottomans consolidated the region, and the stonework remembered.

Yusuf's contact in Sivas was a man named Kemal — a lean, sardonic figure of perhaps fifty who ran a *han* and appeared to regard the world with the amusement of someone who had seen enough of it to find its repetitions entertaining. He embraced Yusuf with the warmth of long friendship and assessed Cornelis in approximately four seconds with the accuracy, Cornelis suspected, of a man who had been making such assessments for decades.

"The Dutchman," Kemal said, in Turkish.

"The factor," Yusuf said.

"Adem's work?"

"All winter."

Kemal looked at Cornelis with a slight upward revision of whatever he had initially concluded. "The road east," he said. "You know what you're going into?"

"I'm learning," Cornelis said, in Turkish.

Kemal made a sound that was somewhere between Yusuf's almost-laugh and something more pointed. "Good answer," he said. He switched to the trading Italian of the caravan routes, slower and more deliberate than Yusuf's, shaped by different influences. "Honest men say they are learning. Fools say they know. The road east has no patience for fools."

"What does it have patience for?" Cornelis asked.

Kemal thought about it. "Men who pay attention," he said. "And men who can tell the difference between a problem and a condition."

"What's the difference?"

"A problem can be solved." Kemal turned toward the *han* interior. "Come. Eat. Tomorrow we talk about the road to Erzurum and what the conditions are this season."

Cornelis followed him in, and behind him the door of the *han* swung on its hinge in the Sivas wind, and the road east waited outside it with its conditions and its distances, patient as the plateau, indifferent to whether he was ready or not.

He was, he thought, nearly ready.
