The city had been burning for two hundred years.

Not literally — not always — but Tabriz had the quality of a place that fire understood well, that had been taken and retaken and burned and rebuilt often enough that the architecture had developed a certain philosophical relationship with impermanence. The Mongols had sacked it. Tamerlane had sacked it. The Safavids had made it their capital and the Ottomans had taken it twice and given it back twice and the border between the two empires had moved back and forth across the surrounding plain like a man unable to find a comfortable position in bed.

"It is the most contested city in the world that is still worth contesting," Adem said. He had poured something — not tea this time, a darker liquid that arrived in small glasses and was not discussed — and settled into his chair with the manner of a man who had been waiting for the right evening to tell this particular story. "Which tells you something about what is there."

What was there: the Persian silk trade at its source, before the Ottoman intermediaries took their margin, before the Venetian agents added theirs, before the Istanbul market added the final layer of markup that made Bursa silk expensive and Persian silk — if you could get it directly — the kind of margin that Adem had spent thirty years looking for.

"How direct?" Cornelis asked.

"The workshops are in Gilan, on the Caspian coast — that is another journey entirely, one I have not made. But Tabriz is where the Gilan silk comes to be traded, and Tabriz is reachable." Adem turned his glass in his hands. "In a good year."

"How often is it a good year?"

"Since the last Ottoman-Safavid war ended — perhaps half the years. The Peace of Nasuh Pasha held longer than most expected. It may hold through this spring. It may not." He looked at Cornelis steadily. "This is the nature of that road. Everything east of Erzurum is conditional."

Cornelis processed this. "You have been before."

"Twice. The first time twenty years ago — that was when I understood what the margin could be. The second time eight years ago, when the road opened briefly between conflicts." Adem set down his glass. "Both times I came back with enough to fund the next several years of trade. Both times the road closed again within the season."

"And the accounts — the obligations you mentioned. The arrangements you were not comfortable with in retrospect."

Adem was quiet for a moment. "Financing the Tabriz journey requires capital that one man cannot hold alone. Twenty years ago I borrowed from sources whose terms seemed reasonable in the excitement of the margin." He looked at his hands. "The terms were not reasonable. I paid them — eventually, fully — but the payment required further arrangements that required further arrangements." He gestured at the ledgers on the shelf. "You have read what that produces."

"Yes," Cornelis said.

"This time I want to go differently." Adem looked at him directly. "Smaller. Better capitalized. With a factor who knows the accounts so well that nothing accumulates in the margins without being seen." He paused. "The risk is not primarily financial. It is the road, the politics, the conditional nature of everything east of Erzurum. I cannot eliminate that risk. I can manage the financial structure so that if something goes wrong on the road, the damage is bounded."

Cornelis thought about the dotted line on the map. He thought about what Yusuf had said about danger and care, which were not the same thing. He thought about the wrestling match in Karapınar, the two falls before the throw, the body learning from what the mind could not prepare it for.

"Tell me the road," he said.

---

Adem told it over three evenings.

The first evening: Kayseri to Sivas, six days on a road he knew well, the last substantial Ottoman city before the landscape became serious. Sivas was a junction — the road north to Trabzon and the Black Sea, the road east toward Erzincan and the mountains. It had a *han* Adem trusted, contacts who could be relied upon for information about road conditions further east, a market where final supplies could be acquired at reasonable prices.

The second evening: Sivas to Erzurum, eight days through country that was no longer the plateau. The mountains of eastern Anatolia were different from the Taurus visible from Kayseri — older, less organized, the valleys deep and the passes genuine obstacles rather than the gradual elevations of the western plateau. The Euphrates had its headwaters somewhere in this territory, which Cornelis found difficult to reconcile with the modest river he had encountered on maps labeled as the great boundary of the ancient world. Snow in the passes as late as April. Bandits, occasionally, though Adem said this with the same neutrality he applied to weather — a condition to be managed, not a drama.

"The guards from the Kayseri journey," Cornelis said.

"Not sufficient for that road," Adem said. "We would need to join a larger caravan out of Sivas. There is usually a significant movement east in March — merchants, pilgrims heading eventually toward Mecca via the southern route, soldiers rotating to the eastern garrisons. Safety in numbers is not a metaphor on the Erzurum road. It is the operating principle."

The third evening: Erzurum to Tabriz.

Adem was quieter about this section. Not evasive — Cornelis had learned to distinguish Adem's genuine reticences from his managed ones — but careful, in the way that a man is careful about a country he loves and has complicated feelings about.

"Erzurum is the last Ottoman city," he said. "It feels like a last city — it has that quality. Large garrison, serious walls, the sense of a place that knows it is a boundary and has organized itself around that knowledge." He paused. "East of Erzurum the road crosses into territory that is sometimes Ottoman and sometimes Safavid and sometimes neither, depending on the year and the mood of both empires. There are Kurdish tribal territories whose relationship with both empires is — pragmatic. There are Armenian villages that have survived every successive wave of authority by being useful to whoever is currently in charge."

"And the road itself?"

"Good, in places. Maintained when the Safavids hold it, because the Tabriz trade is important to them. Neglected when the Ottomans hold it, because the Ottomans are less interested in what the road leads to." He turned his glass. "Four days from Erzurum to the border, if border is the right word. Three more to Tabriz, if the roads are open. If they are not—" he made the gesture that Cornelis associated with the open-endedness of the eastern trade— "we wait, or we turn back."

"And in Tabriz itself?"

Adem's expression changed.

It was the change Cornelis had seen in the carpet workshop — the warmth that crossed his face when he spoke about something he genuinely loved. "Tabriz," he said, "is the most alive market I have encountered. More alive than Istanbul, which is larger but older in itself, settled into its own importance. Tabriz knows it might be taken again tomorrow and conducts its commerce accordingly — everything faster, more vivid, the merchants more direct because there is no tradition of permanence to be polite about." He shook his head slowly. "The silk market is in the *qaysariyya* — their word for the covered market. The silk from Gilan arrives in bales and is graded and traded in a single day sometimes, the whole year's production moving in a week's burst of commerce. The prices are—" he stopped. "You would need to see it."

Cornelis looked at the map on the table between them, the dotted line going east from Erzurum, the city of Tabriz marked with a confidence the mapmaker clearly felt and the road clearly didn't always justify.

"The people," Cornelis said. "In Tabriz. Language."

"Persian primarily. Azeri Turkish, which is different enough from Ottoman Turkish to be its own learning. Arabic for trade and religion. Armenian in the merchant quarters." Adem looked at him. "Your Ottoman Turkish will be understood but not native. You would need me to conduct the primary negotiations. Your role would be the accounts and the assessment — the same as here, but faster. In Tabriz you cannot take ten days to organize a decade of records. You have the duration of the market."

"Which is?"

"Two weeks. Perhaps three if we arrive at the right moment in the trading cycle." He paused. "Then the road back, before the politics change."

Cornelis was quiet for a moment, doing the arithmetic he always did — not just the financial arithmetic, though he was doing that too, the margins Adem had described against the costs of the journey, the capital required, the risk-adjusted return. He was doing a different arithmetic as well, harder to express in columns: the weight of what he would see against the weight of what he was moving away from, the April passage against the dotted line, the letter to his father that had not said what was true against the thing that was becoming true.

"The capital," he said. "How are you financing it differently this time?"

"I have enough of my own," Adem said. "For the smaller operation I described. No borrowed money, no arrangements with terms that will require further arrangements." He looked at Cornelis carefully. "What I do not have is a factor I can trust with the full picture. The Tabriz merchants — they are sophisticated, they are fast, they will find any weakness in the accounting structure and exploit it with complete professionalism and no malice. It is simply how the market works." He spread his hands. "I need someone who knows my accounts as well as I do, who can see a proposed transaction in the context of everything else, who will tell me when something is wrong before I have signed it."

"You have described the job I have been doing here all winter," Cornelis said.

"Yes," said Adem. "I have."

Yusuf was in the courtyard when Cornelis came out of the study on the third evening, sitting on the edge of the covered fountain in his winter coat, apparently engaged in the contemplation of nothing in particular.

Cornelis sat beside him.

They were quiet for a while. The mountain was invisible behind the cloud cover but present — you could feel the mass of it even when you couldn't see it.

"You knew," Cornelis said. "When we left Istanbul. Not the details, but the direction."

Yusuf was quiet long enough that Cornelis thought he might not answer. Then: "I knew Adem needed someone. I knew you needed a road." He paused. "I did not know you would be what he needed. I hoped."

"And if I hadn't been?"

"Then you would have organized his accounts for ten days and ridden back to Istanbul with fair wages and a good story." Yusuf almost laughed. "The road provides what it provides. You cannot plan the specifics."

Cornelis thought about this. "Tabriz," he said.

"Yes."

"You've been."

"Once. Twelve years ago." Yusuf looked at the courtyard wall, at something Cornelis couldn't see. "It is a remarkable place. And the road to it—" he paused— "the road to it is the road I have been meaning to take again. There is something further east that I have been meaning to return to."

Cornelis looked at him.

"Further east than Tabriz?" he said.

Yusuf said nothing. Which was, Cornelis had learned, not the same as no.

He sat in the courtyard while the February cold did what February cold did, and felt the spring journey taking shape around him the way the plateau had taken shape on the morning the forest ended — enormous, clear-edged, already decided, waiting only for him to ride into it.

He was not afraid.

He noted this, as he noted things, for the record.
