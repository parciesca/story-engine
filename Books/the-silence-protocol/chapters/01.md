The signal had been arriving for three years, and for three years Yara had assumed it was mathematics.

That was the consensus. Colonial Authority Science Division, forty-two independent research teams across five institutions, the full apparatus of human pattern-recognition turned toward the signal like flowers toward a lamp. Repeating prime sequences. Dimensional notations that mapped to geometric structures no organism on Earth had independently conceived. The signal was clearly intelligent, clearly intentional, clearly a message in the oldest universal language: here is proof that we can count.

Yara had agreed with this, had published two papers confirming it, had let herself feel the particular pride of being right in public. Then, seven weeks ago, she had started filtering for noise.

Not because she thought there was anything in the noise. She was bored. Rotation extension requests required documented ongoing research, and she had run out of documented ongoing research. The noise filter was something to do.

The anomaly she found was not in the noise. It was in the signal itself, braided through the mathematical structures like a second melody played in a key she hadn't been listening for.

She'd spent six weeks convincing herself she was wrong.

This morning, she had stopped trying.

---

The analysis station occupied the full width of Ring C, which meant it was the coldest room on Relay Nine, which meant it was her room in the way that spaces become yours when other people avoid them. Three workstations, two of which she had colonized with secondary displays and a ceramic mug with a hairline fracture she kept meaning to replace. The view through the single viewport was nothing — a suggestion of stars too distant to resolve, the soft blue gradient where the solar wind thinned into the interstellar medium. She had stopped seeing it months ago. Now she saw it again.

The readout on her primary display showed the Tau Ceti signal stripped of its mathematical content. What remained should have been residue — random thermal noise, the cosmic microwave background, the accumulated static of eleven point nine light-years of travel. Instead, the remaining structure held a pattern that repeated in intervals of exactly 73.4 seconds. Not prime. Not geometric. Not anything in the signals' established vocabulary.

Sable had flagged a sub-frequency anomaly four months before her noise filter. She'd logged it and moved on. She looked at that log entry now, six words of automated notation, and felt something unpleasant in her sternum.

She pulled up the emotional-encoding literature — not because she expected to find anything, but because she needed to be able to say she'd checked. Signal analysis theory had a long, embarrassing history of researchers projecting meaning onto pattern. The field had developed frameworks for exactly this kind of confirmation bias. She read through three papers and found what she already knew: the frameworks were designed for human communication. No one had seriously applied them to xenosignals because no one had had a xenosignal worth applying them to.

No one had, until now, needed a framework for alien grief.

She sat with that sentence for a while.

The 73.4-second pattern, when she mapped it against the parametric space used to model human emotional signal in neurolinguistic research, produced a distribution she recognized. Not intellectually. Somewhere lower than that. She had heard this shape before, in a different form, in the months after her mother died — in the particular rhythm of her own breathing at three in the morning when sleep stopped working. Repetitive. Searching. Landing on something over and over that wasn't there.

She was aware this was not evidence. She was also aware she wasn't going to be able to un-see it.

---

Commander Drov found her in Ring C forty minutes later, which meant he'd been notified by Sable that she'd been in there for eleven hours and had not eaten. This was a standing arrangement she had agreed to and occasionally resented.

"Your coffee is in the corridor," he said, from the doorway. He never came all the way in.

"I'll get it."

"That's what you said at oh-eight-hundred." He leaned against the frame, arms crossed, doing the thing he did where he looked at the displays before he looked at her. "You find something?"

She had prepared three different ways to answer this question. All three involved starting with the methodology, the peer literature, the many reasons she might be wrong. She had learned, with Drov, that he heard what she framed first and treated everything else as qualification.

"I think so," she said.

"In the signal."

"In the signal. Not the mathematical layer. Something underneath it." She turned from the display and looked at him directly, which was the only way she had of communicating urgency without sounding alarmed. "I need another forty-eight hours before I'm ready to write it up. But I didn't want to sit on it for forty-eight hours without you knowing something was there."

He looked at the display again. He had the signal readout memorized by this point — everyone on Relay Nine did, the way you memorized the faces of the people you lived with, not through intention but through exposure. He studied the secondary overlay she'd left up. Whatever he saw in it, he didn't share.

"How confident?" he said.

This was the question she had the least patience for, in general, because confidence was a state she approached asymptotically and never achieved. But she knew what he was actually asking: *is this real, or is this three years of isolation finding shapes in static?*

She turned back to the display. The 73.4-second pattern cycled through its interval and landed, again, on its absent destination.

"Confident enough," she said, "that I needed to tell you first."

She heard him push off the doorframe. He didn't say anything else, and she didn't turn around. Outside the viewport, the stars held their positions with perfect indifference, and the signal continued its long transmission from somewhere eleven point nine light-years away, saying, over and over and over, the same thing it had been saying for three years.

She was only just now learning how to hear it.
