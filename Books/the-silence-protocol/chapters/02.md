She stopped him before he cleared the threshold.

"Drov."

He came back to the doorframe. Didn't come in. He had a policy about Ring C that he'd never articulated — some professional superstition about not crossing into a researcher's active workspace — and she had never asked him to break it until now.

"Sit down," she said.

He looked at the chair she'd indicated, the second workstation, cleared of her secondary displays. Then he came in and sat.

She turned to face him fully. Not the display. Him.

"I said forty-eight hours," she said. "But you'd spend the next two days running the same hypothesis I just ran, and you'd end up here anyway. So."

"So."

"The pattern in the secondary layer — the 73.4-second interval." She went back to the primary display and pulled up the parametric distribution she'd been staring at for six weeks. "I ran it against the neurolinguistic emotional-encoding framework. Szorenyi's parametric model, specifically, which was designed for human signal analysis, which means it's the wrong tool." She paused. "Except it isn't."

He was looking at the display the way he always did — processing before responding, not revealing what he was processing. She had found this trait both useful and maddening over four years.

"The distribution," she said. "When I map the 73.4-second pattern against the parametric space, it produces a signature consistent with sustained, low-amplitude, repetitive emotional signal. The closest human analogue, in the literature, is prolonged grief response." She said the next part carefully, because saying it was still strange. "The signal isn't broadcasting grief. It's — the signal *is* grief. In structure. In the kind of repetition that characterizes grief. The way it cycles and returns and doesn't find resolution."

Silence.

"I know how that sounds," she said.

"I know how it sounds too," Drov said. He said it without inflection, which was either nothing or everything.

"There are three alternative explanations I've been working through. All three require the secondary layer to be: one, a random structural coincidence — possible, low probability; two, a projection artifact in my analytical framework — except I've run three independent frameworks and gotten the same result; or three, a known physical phenomenon that produces this signature — I've checked the literature. Nothing." She pulled up the comparison matrix. "The most parsimonious explanation is that the secondary layer is an intentional emotional encoding and that encoding maps most closely to grief."

He looked at the matrix for a long time.

"You said Sable flagged a sub-frequency anomaly," he said finally.

"Four months before my noise filter. I logged it and moved on."

"Before or after the math-layer consensus papers?"

She thought about this. "After. I'd submitted the second one maybe six weeks before Sable's flag."

He nodded, once, the way he did when he was filing something and not yet ready to say what he'd filed it as.

"Who else knows," he said. Not a question.

"Nobody. Sable's logs, which I can't modify, and you." She watched him. "I was going to write it up in forty-eight hours and send it to Command. That's still the plan. I wanted you to know first."

"I appreciate that."

"Drov." She waited until he looked at her, not the display. "I need to know what Command's classified position is on the signal. The containment pressure you're under — that's not standard first-contact procedure, because there isn't one. Someone upstream made a decision about how this was going to be handled. I want to know what they decided."

He was very still.

"I know you can't tell me," she said, "if there's a classification order. I know how it works. I'm not asking you to break protocol." A pause. "I'm asking you to tell me whether there's protocol to break."

He looked at her for a moment. She had known Aleksei Drov for four years. She had seen him manage a coolant failure, an incapacitating infection that took out three of eleven crew, and the communication blackout of 2285, which had lasted forty-two days. She had never seen him look like he was making a calculation in real time.

"File your write-up," he said at last.

"That's not—"

"File your write-up, document everything, flag it appropriately." He stood, smoothing his jacket — a habit she associated with decisions he hadn't entirely made peace with. "Make sure your methodology is airtight. Peer-reviewable."

"Why."

"Because," he said, from the doorway again, at the threshold he apparently needed between himself and this room, "what you just told me is the kind of thing that benefits from a record that exists before anyone has a reason to want it not to."

She let that sit.

Outside the viewport, the signal continued its 73.4-second cycle, patient and unresolved.

"One more thing," she said.

He waited.

"Sable flagged the anomaly before I did." She looked at the six-word log entry still open on her secondary display. "I want to formally query Sable on the sub-frequency data. Get its full analysis on record."

Something crossed his face that wasn't quite the thing he'd said when she told him about grief, but was in the same family. "That's a reasonable next step," he said.

She turned back to her work.

She heard him leave. She heard him stop just outside the door, in the corridor where her coffee had long gone cold, and stand there for a beat longer than was necessary — not quite going, not quite coming back.

Then she heard him walk away.
