The write-up took her fourteen hours.

Not because the analysis was incomplete — she'd been refining it mentally for six weeks, and the intellectual architecture was solid enough to walk. What took time was the language. A document of this kind lived or died in its framing: too hedged and it read as uncertain; too declarative and it invited the wrong kind of scrutiny. She needed it to be exactly the kind of thing a researcher sent to colleagues for review — not the kind of thing that flagged for classification.

She wrote "emotional encoding analog" four times and deleted it three. Wrote "parametric resonance with known neurolinguistic grief-response signatures" and left it. Accurate. Clinical. Impossible to misread and also, she hoped, impossible to quickly dismiss.

Drov had not come back.

She sent the document at 0317 station time, flagged for Science Division review through the standard colonial research protocol channel, with a copy to the heliopause monitoring consortium and to her own institutional archive at the University of Cape Town. The archive copy was Drov's idea without his having said so. She kept a local encrypted copy as well.

Then she went to sleep, for the first time in thirty-one hours, on the cot in the observation alcove off Ring C, because she didn't want to be far from the display when the replies started coming in.

---

She woke at 1604 to Sable's notification tone — not an alarm, just the soft single chime that meant something had arrived for her attention. She'd been expecting Science Division acknowledgment. Their standard response window was eight to twelve hours; she was still within range.

The message in her queue was not from Science Division.

The sender identifier read: *CAOSI-7 / Colonial Authority Office of Strategic Information.*

She sat up. Read it once. Read it again.

*Dr. Osei — Your submission to Science Division Research Archive has been received and forwarded to this office pursuant to CA Directive 7-R (Restricted Classification Protocols, Tier Two: Extraterrestrial Signal Intelligence). Effective immediately, all materials, methodologies, and derived analyses referenced in your submission are designated provisional classification SENTINEL. Distribution is suspended pending formal review. You are directed to retain all original data in secure station storage and to avoid further distribution or publication pending outcome of review. A Colonial Authority Scientific Liaison will contact Relay Station Nine within the 72-hour review window.*

*Pursuant to Directive 7-R: this communication and its contents are classified. Confirmation of receipt required.*

*—Adjunct Director P. Vasquez, CAOSI-7*

She read it a third time.

The submission had been sent at 0317. The message from CAOSI-7 had arrived at 1547 — exactly sixteen hours and thirty minutes after she'd sent it. Almost exactly one-way communication lag from Earth. Someone had been waiting for something from Relay Station Nine. They had read it, classified it, and responded within minutes of receipt.

That was not how Science Division worked. Science Division took weeks.

She sat with the ceramic mug — the one with the hairline fracture, still unfilled from the day before — and thought about who had seen her document first and how long they'd been watching for it.

The notification tone sounded again. A new message, same sender: *Confirmation of receipt required within 6 hours. Non-response will be construed as non-compliance.*

She set the mug down.

---

Drov was in the commissary when she found him — not eating, just sitting with his hands around a cup, looking at nothing in particular. He looked up when she came in and she watched his face carefully. He didn't ask what was wrong. He asked: "Which channel?"

"CAOSI-7," she said. "Adjunct Director Vasquez."

He looked at the table. A muscle in his jaw moved once.

"You knew this was a possible response," she said.

"I knew there was a Tier Two classification protocol." He didn't look up. "I didn't know who'd send it or how fast."

"Sixteen hours. They responded in sixteen hours, Drov. They had someone waiting."

"I know."

"Directive 7-R. SENTINEL classification. All materials suspended."

"I know." He said it the same way both times.

She sat across from him. The commissary was empty; most of the crew was on sleep rotation. The station's background hum — ventilation, power cycling, the systems that kept eleven people alive at the edge of the solar system — felt louder than usual.

"They're not responding to what I found," she said. "They're responding to the fact that I found something and wrote it down."

He looked at her then. His expression was the careful, contained thing it always was, except underneath it — if you'd been watching his face for four years, which she had — there was something that wasn't quite fear but was closely related to it.

"Yes," he said.

"Which means they already had a position."

"Yes."

"Before I submitted. Before I started writing. Before—" She stopped. "How long, Drov?"

He looked at his cup. "I genuinely don't know."

She believed him. She wasn't sure that made it better.

Outside the commissary's small viewport — a rectangle of nothing, a few distant unresolvable stars — the signal from Tau Ceti continued its measured 73.4-second rhythm, classified now, sealed under a directive neither of them had chosen and couldn't avoid, saying the same thing it had always been saying.

Whatever it was, it had been saying it since 2284.

And someone on Earth had known to watch for the day someone finally heard it.
