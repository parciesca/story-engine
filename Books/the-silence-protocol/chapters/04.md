The deadline was 2147 station time.

She watched it approach from the observation alcove, not because she needed to watch it — Sable would notify her regardless — but because whatever she was about to do required her to do it with her eyes open.

She had spent the intervening hours writing nothing and sending nothing. She had eaten, which surprised her. She had showered, which surprised her more. At some point around 1900 she had noticed that her hands, which had been unsteady since Drov left the commissary, were no longer unsteady. That had been informative.

She was not going to confirm receipt.

Not as a gesture. Not as resistance. As a data point. The directive specified that non-response would be construed as non-compliance. She wanted to know what non-compliance looked like before she decided whether to perform compliance.

At 2147 the deadline passed.

Nothing happened.

She watched the queue for twenty minutes. No follow-up message. No escalation. No demand. The signal from Tau Ceti continued its 73.4-second cycle on the secondary display, and the absence of bureaucratic response was, in its own way, louder than the directive had been.

At 2208 she went to the commissary for water.

Drov was there.

He was sitting in the same chair he'd been in sixteen hours earlier. The cup in his hands was different. He didn't look up when she came in, which was unusual; Drov looked up when anyone entered a room.

"You didn't confirm," he said.

"No."

"They know."

"How do you know they know?"

He took a moment. "Because I received a communication at 2149."

She stopped on her way to the dispenser.

"On what channel," she said.

"A channel I'm not supposed to discuss with you." He said it carefully. "I'm telling you the channel existed. I'm telling you it was used. I'm not telling you what it said."

She filled the cup. She sat down across from him. The station's hum was the same hum.

"Is it a standing channel," she said, "or was it opened for this."

"Standing."

She had suspected something like this. She had not quite let herself suspect it all the way down.

"How long," she said.

"Since before you."

"Since before my rotation?"

"Since before the station."

She sat with that.

---

She did not go back to Ring C. She went to the auxiliary console in the engineering annex — a workstation three crew used rarely, none at this hour — and logged in under her own credentials. She queried Sable informally, the way any researcher might check routine traffic.

*Sable. Can you show me outbound and inbound archival queries on station systems over the last eighteen hours.*

*Yes, Dr. Osei.*

The list was short. Internal traffic was normal. There were two entries from external sources she did not recognize.

*Identify sources seven and nine.*

*Source seven: Colonial Authority Office of Strategic Information. Remote diagnostic subroutine initiated at 2148:42. Non-invasive. Pulling archival metadata — directory structures, file timestamps, log integrity checks. Not reading content.*

*Source nine: Colonial Authority Office of Strategic Information. Concurrent. Accessing Commander's administrative channel.*

She read it twice.

*How often do external diagnostic subroutines access station archives in a standard operating month.*

*Zero times, Dr. Osei.*

She set her hands flat on the desk.

*Is the current external activity visible to Commander Drov through his standard monitoring interfaces.*

A pause — not a long one, but longer than Sable's responses usually ran.

*No, Dr. Osei.*

She held that. Then, carefully:

*Sable. Please note that I have asked these questions. Timestamp and retain in my personal query log.*

*Noted.*

---

She thought about the shape of it.

Non-compliance had not produced a demand. It had produced observation. They had pulled back the veil on the station's archival system within ninety seconds of her missing the deadline — not to find the document, which they already had, but to establish what other records existed and where. Metadata. Directory structure. What files her analysis had touched. What she might have saved elsewhere.

They were doing an inventory.

And they were speaking to Drov through a channel that did not run through her and was not visible to him on any interface he would recognize as his.

The point of the six-hour deadline had never been to receive her confirmation. It had been to see whether she would provide it — whether she would behave like someone who intended to comply. Non-response had answered that question, and the answer had triggered a silent escalation no notification would ever make visible to her.

She sat at the engineering console for a long time, not moving, watching the indicator lights that told her nothing and, tonight, everything.

Eventually she opened a blank document.

Not a submission. Not yet.

A list.

Everywhere the analysis existed outside the station: Cape Town archive. Her personal institutional account. The heliopause consortium copy. Her local encrypted copy. Sable's logs. Her own memory.

She counted six locations, four of which CAOSI-7 could reach given sufficient motivation and time.

Two they could not.

She closed the document without saving it.

Then she walked back to Ring C, slowly, past the commissary where Drov was no longer sitting, past the viewport that looked out on nothing, and thought about which of the two she was going to protect first.
