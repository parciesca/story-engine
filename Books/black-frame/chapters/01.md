The frost had not yet broken when Reigh rode into Halworth. He came down the high lane from his father's old farmstead at a walk, the gelding picking its way over ruts of black ice, and the town below him still lay under a lid of smoke that had not been there a year ago. Three new chimneys above the river. He counted them again, as he always did, and did not like the sum.

The boy from Pemberton's mill was waiting at the bridge, hopping on his cold heels. Mr. Pickford had sent him up at four. The magistrate himself, the boy said, was abed with a quinsy and not for rising; would the captain be so good as to attend.

"What is it then," Reigh said.

"A drowning, sir."

"At the mill?"

"In the race, sir. Mr. Pemberton would have her out by now if Mr. Pickford had not sent word."

Her. So a woman. Reigh dismounted at the mill gate and gave the reins to the boy and walked the cobbled yard with his old stiffness in his right knee. The wheel was halted; he could hear the silence of it before he saw it, an absence in the bones of the building that he did not at once recognise. Frost rimed the dressed stone of the millrace. Two soldiers of the Cumberland militia, sent up out of Manchester last autumn, stood at the head of the tail-race with their hands tucked into their tunics and their breath going up in plumes. They straightened when they saw him, which surprised him; he had not yet got used to being saluted as a magistrate's man rather than as a captain.

"Sergeant Daws," he said, recognising the elder.

"Sir." Daws nodded down into the race. "She'll have come in above the wheel and gone under it. She's caught on the gratings now."

Reigh leaned over. The body lay face down in the slack water below the stilled wheel, dark hair fanned out and snared in the iron grating that kept the larger refuse from the works. A grey skirt, sodden black. One hand visible, very white, fingers curled.

"How long?" he said.

"Master found her at first light. He's in the counting-house."

"Has anyone been into the water?"

"No, sir. Mr. Pemberton said leave her be 'til you came."

Reigh looked again. The wheel had been stopped. That was what the silence was. A mill in March had no business being stopped at six in the morning unless something had broken in it, and he could see from the dressed timbers that nothing had. Pemberton had stopped his own wheel. That alone was worth a guinea of evidence, though he could not yet say of what.

"Get her out," he said. "Carefully. Lay her on the cobbles and cover her with something decent. Not your tunic, sergeant — your greatcoat. I'll be a quarter of an hour."

He crossed the yard to the counting-house. Josiah Pemberton was at his desk with a black coat buttoned to the throat and a glass of something colourless at his elbow that was not, Reigh thought, water. He rose at once, and his rising was a fraction too quick.

"Captain Reigh. I had hoped Mr. Pickford—"

"Mr. Pickford is unwell. You'll have me. Who is she?"

Pemberton sat again, very deliberately, and made a steeple of his fingers. He was a man of perhaps fifty, broad, with the high colour of a man who eats well and sleeps badly. "Esther Holt. A winder, second floor. She lodges — lodged — with her sister in Brigg Row."

"How does a winder come to be in your tail-race before the wheel is set?"

"That, captain, is what I cannot tell you. The race is fenced. Whoever opened the gate to the yard was none of mine."

"Your gateman?"

"Asleep. He has been thrashed for it. He swears to God the gate was locked at midnight and locked at five when he opened for the cardroom hands."

"Then she came over the wall."

"Or she did not come over it living."

The two men looked at each other. Pemberton's face did not change but the colour deepened a shade further along his cheekbones.

"You are too quick to say so," Reigh said.

"I am too slow if I do not. There is talk in this town, captain. You have been in the south, you do not — forgive me — you do not entirely know what is here now. Last month at Westhoughton they fired the Wroe mill. Three weeks ago in Middleton they shot a man who looked like me from the front of his own counting-house. I keep a girl on the second floor who is found in my race at dawn with the wheel stopped, and I am to say what?"

Reigh let it sit. Outside, the wheel still did not turn. Somewhere above them a child coughed, the dry rattling cough of the cardroom that Reigh remembered from his father's smaller place, a sound he had not heard in seven years and recognised at once with a small inward turning of the stomach.

"You stopped the wheel yourself," he said.

"I did."

"Why."

Pemberton considered. He took up the colourless glass and set it down again without drinking. "Because if she had gone through it, captain, there would have been less of her to ask questions of. And I want the questions asked."

Reigh did not answer. He went out again into the yard, where Daws and the younger soldier had Esther Holt laid on her back on the frosted cobbles with the greatcoat drawn up to her chin. He knelt at her side and drew the coat down, and was very still for a long moment.

There was a thin dark line about her throat that the water had not made, and the bruise of a thumb beneath her left ear.

He drew the coat up again, gently, and stood.

"Sergeant. Send word to the parsonage. The Reverend Brearley is to come at his earliest, and he is to bring the elder Miss Holt with him. And keep this yard shut. No hand of this mill leaves the gate until I have spoken to him."

Daws did not move at once. "Sir. The hands in the cardroom — there's near sixty of them. They've families—"

"I know what they have, sergeant. Shut the gate."

He stood a moment longer beside her. The frost was burning off the cobbles around her where the body's small heat had not yet given out altogether, a faint paler ring at her shoulders and hip. Above the mill the three new chimneys at last began to draw, the smoke going up grey into a sky that had not decided whether to give them weather or not.

"God help us," he said, very quietly, and not to anyone in particular. Then he went to find the gateman.
