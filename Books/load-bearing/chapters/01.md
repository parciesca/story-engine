The lock on the access door had been cut. Not pried — cut, with something precise enough to leave a clean shear rather than the mangled twist she usually found, which meant either a professional or someone who had taken their time. Vera photographed it, added a note to the field tablet, and filed it under *site security concerns* where it would eventually become a memo no one read.

The building was six stories of reinforced concrete block, 1963 vintage, the kind of construction that had looked progressive once and now mostly looked tired. The city had condemned it in August after a fourth-floor fire spread moisture damage through the east wall and a follow-up inspection found carbonation in the concrete columns — a slow alkaline rot that could work for decades without announcing itself. Vera's job was to quantify the damage. Document the load-path failures, grade the structural members, sign the sheet that said *yes, this building has become a liability, you may proceed.* It was the third one she'd assessed this quarter. They had a rhythm by now.

The lobby was dim. Power had been cut in September, and the October morning outside was flat and overcast, so the light came only through the glass panels flanking the entrance — milky, even, enough. She had a headlamp clipped to her vest but didn't reach for it yet. She let her eyes adjust.

The smell was the usual: damp concrete, old carpet adhesive, something underneath that was harder to name. Not rot, exactly. More like the scent of a space that had been waiting a long time for something to happen.

She started at the base of the east wall and worked methodically north, tapping with the handle of her inspection hammer every eighteen inches, listening. A solid thud meant intact concrete. The hollow pop that told you the carbonation front had reached the rebar — that you were past the carbonation depth and into compromised material — she'd developed an ear for over years. You could feel it in the grip of the tool, a slight give, like tapping a wall and finding it hollow behind the plaster.

She found it at 2.4 meters up. Then again at 2.9. She photographed the surfaces, measured, noted the pattern. The crack propagation above the third-floor header was consistent with what the initial inspection had found: moisture moving laterally through the block course, expanding and contracting through freeze cycles, pushing the concrete apart along its own aggregate boundaries. Patient destruction. The building had been killing itself for years before anyone thought to look.

She took her notes. She moved to the stairwell.

The stairs were poured concrete with a steel-pipe handrail, coated in fine grey dust the way every surface was after months of abandonment. The dust settled uniformly in undisturbed spaces, a thin continuous layer that registered the smallest perturbations. Vera paused on the third step and crouched. The dust on the riser above had been scuffed — not a clean footprint, but an oblong smear, the kind a boot toe left when you placed weight and shifted. Then another on the fifth step. Then a stretch of unmarked risers, then another smear near the landing.

Someone had been careful. Not careful enough, but careful.

She noted it under *site access concerns*, same category as the lock. Took a photo for the record. There were sometimes kids, in these buildings — teenagers who found condemned structures irresistible for the obvious reasons, who left their evidence in beer cans and spray paint. She'd stopped finding it remarkable years ago.

The second floor was standard. Horizontal cracking in the corridor wall, four centimeters of spalling above the window lintels, a section of the ceiling near the elevator shaft that had delaminated and dropped to the floor. She measured, photographed, moved on.

On the third floor she noticed the door to apartment 3C was open.

Not ajar — not the kind of open that came from a latch giving up over months. Fully open, swung back flat against the inside wall. She stood in the corridor a moment. There was nothing to hear. She pushed the door wider with her knuckle and stepped in.

The light was better here. The apartment faced east and the windows were intact — all of them, which was itself unusual — so the grey morning came in without obstruction. Two rooms: a main space that had been living room and kitchen combined, and a bedroom beyond an open doorway. She did the living room first.

It had been used. That was plain. Against the wall beside the kitchen counter: a sleeping bag rolled and cinched at both ends, set on a closed plastic bin, not on the floor. Deliberate. On the counter itself: a camp stove, the kind that used compressed-gas canisters, with a small aluminum pot nested over the burner. Two food-storage canisters, both sealed. A water container — five-liter, collapsible — flattened and folded. More than a single night. More than a few nights.

She stood there for a moment. She photographed it.

The bedroom was empty except for a cardboard box and a canvas duffel bag pushed under what had once been a closet shelf. Clothes in the duffel, she could tell from the weight distribution, though she didn't open it. The cardboard box held books — paperbacks, a worn hardcover, a Bible without a cover. She didn't touch those either.

Back in the main room she went to the window to measure the spalling on the lintel, and that was when she saw the mug on the sill. Enamel, dark blue, a white chip on the rim. She picked it up without thinking.

The tea inside was warm.

Not hot — not recently-made warm — but warm in the way a mug stays warm when it has been held for a while and then set down. Twenty minutes, maybe. Less.

Vera put the mug back on the sill with care, handling the rim where she'd touched it.

From the floor above came the unmistakable creak of someone shifting their weight.

She didn't move. The sound settled. The building was silent again around it — the particular silence of a place where someone is trying not to breathe.
