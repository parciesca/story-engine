The radiator above Reardon's desk had been banging for three winters. He had asked his landlord twice to fix it. The landlord had said the same thing both times — that they were lucky to have heat at all, considering the price of oil — and then had gone back downstairs to the lunch counter to argue with a Polish butcher about hockey.

Reardon had given up on the radiator. He had given up, by that spring, on a number of things.

It was a quarter past nine on a Thursday morning in April, and the sky over Cass Avenue was the color of an old ashtray. He was reading the *Free Press* and not really reading it, the way a man reads a paper when he's waiting for the coffee to be cool enough to drink. Coleman Young was in the news again. Coleman Young was always in the news. The new mayor had told the suburbs over the weekend that the criminals could keep moving, just in a different direction this time. Reardon had laughed when he read it the first time on Sunday. He read it again now and didn't laugh.

The knock came at the door.

It was a polite knock, and that surprised him more than the hour. People who came up the stairs to his office were almost never polite. They had usually been crying for a while, or they had been sent by somebody angry, or they were drunk and looking for the dentist on the second floor who had gone out of business in 1971.

He folded the paper. "It's open."

The woman who came in was carrying her coat over her arm, though it was forty degrees outside, and she was holding it the way a person holds a coat that has been newly cleaned. Twenty-five, he guessed. Twenty-six. A pale-blue dress that had been ironed that morning. A small black handbag. Nothing about her looked accidental.

"Mr. Reardon."

"Have a seat."

She sat. She did not look around the office, which most people did, looking for something to be reassured by. There was nothing in his office that reassured. A green metal filing cabinet. A steel desk. A calendar from a body shop in Hamtramck still turned to February.

"My name is Loretta Mills," she said. "I was told you used to be honest."

"That's a hell of a way to start a conversation."

"It's why I came." Her hands were folded over the coat in her lap. "Do you want me to start over?"

"No," Reardon said. "You did all right."

She told it without flourish. Her brother Marcus, twenty-eight, worked the trim line at Dodge Main in Hamtramck. He lived alone in a two-room walk-up over a barbershop on Mack. He came to her apartment for Sunday dinner most weeks. Two Sundays ago, he hadn't come. She had called the next day. The phone rang and was not picked up. She had taken the bus over on Tuesday and let herself in with the key he had given her. The apartment was tidy. His coat was on the hook. His wallet was on the dresser. His Buick was not in the alley.

She had filed the missing-persons report on Wednesday. She had gone back this Tuesday — eight days later — to ask whether anything had come of it. The desk sergeant had not been able to find the report. The detective who had taken it from her was on vacation. The desk sergeant had told her, in a voice she described to Reardon now without inflection, that grown men sometimes went where they wanted to go.

"And what did you say to him?" Reardon asked.

"I thanked him for his time."

He watched her over the rim of the coffee cup. "Why?"

"Because I wasn't there to fight with the desk sergeant."

"Fair," he said.

She set three twenties on the desk. Then a fourth. "I'm told you charge forty an hour. I have more if it runs longer."

He looked at the money. He looked at her.

"Tell me about your brother," he said.

She did. Marcus read books. Had finished high school, was thinking, on and off, about going back for a community college degree. He had served two years at Fort Hood and had come home in '69 and had said almost nothing about it since. He worked the line. He didn't drink to speak of. There had been some trouble at the plant lately — a caucus he'd joined, words he wouldn't repeat in front of her, a foreman who had it in for him. None of which she thought was the kind of trouble that kept a man from coming home.

"I'll tell you what I think it is," she said.

"Go on."

"Marcus was supposed to take my daughter Tasha to the zoo on Belle Isle two Saturdays ago. He hadn't been to a zoo since he was eight years old. He bought her a stuffed giraffe ahead of time. He had it in a paper bag on his kitchen table. I saw it sitting there. He doesn't miss things like that, Mr. Reardon. He drove me to a doctor's appointment once with a hundred-and-three fever. He's the kind of man who doesn't miss."

She had said *was*, then *doesn't*. He noticed both. He didn't bring either up.

"All right."

"All right?"

"I'll look around."

She was quiet a moment. "What does that mean?"

"It means three days. I ask in some places. Then I tell you what I think. It might not be what you think it is. You should know that going in."

"I know," she said.

"Most likely it's something you've already thought of. I'm not going to lie to you about that."

"I know," she said again.

He took two of the twenties and pushed the other two back across the desk. "I take half up front. Down payment on telling it gently."

For the first time since she'd walked in, she almost smiled. It was a small thing. It went away again.

"What's the foreman's name?"

"Wilcox. Tom Wilcox."

"And the caucus?"

"DRUM."

He held her eyes for a second. He set his pen down on the open notebook.

"All right, Mrs. Mills."

"Miss."

"All right, Miss Mills."

She rose. He rose. She put on her coat without hurry, with a kind of quiet dignity that he didn't see often in this office and that he found, in a tired way, hard to look at directly. She offered her hand. He took it. She left. He sat down again and listened to her footsteps going down the stairs and out into the morning.

He sat there a long time after she was gone.

Then he picked up the phone. He thought a minute. Then he dialed a number he had not dialed in three years.
