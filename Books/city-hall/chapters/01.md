The Millhaven Municipal Building had columns. Four of them, limestone, flanking a set of double doors that hadn't opened evenly since the Carter administration. The left door stuck unless you lifted and pushed at the same time, a motion so specific that every regular visitor performed it without thinking and every new one stood outside looking confused until someone took pity.

Janet Roby had been taking pity on confused visitors for twenty-three years. She arrived at 7:40 every morning, fifteen minutes before she needed to, because she liked the building when it was quiet. She'd unlock the front door — the right side only, the left stayed latched until Roger got around to looking at it, which he'd been meaning to do since 2019 — brew the first pot of coffee in the break room, and sit at her desk in the front office with the overhead lights still off, reading the Millhaven Courier on her phone.

The Courier came out twice a week and could be read in about four minutes, but Janet liked to take her time with it.

By eight, the building began to fill, which was a generous way to describe five people arriving in ones and twos. Dale Kessler, the town manager, came through the right door at 8:02 with a travel mug in one hand and his phone pressed between his ear and his shoulder, already mid-conversation with someone about drainage.

"No, I understand that, but the culvert has been there since — yes. Since before the Hendersons moved in. The water has to go somewhere, Phil." He waved at Janet as he passed. She waved back without looking up. They'd been doing this for eleven years.

Dale's office was at the end of the hall, past the assessor's window and the door to the meeting room that doubled as a storage closet whenever the Historical Society dropped off another box of photographs nobody had time to catalog. He hung his jacket on the hook behind his door, set his mug on the coaster his daughter had made at camp — it said WORLD'S OKAYEST DAD, and he'd never once considered replacing it — and continued explaining to Phil Henderson that a forty-year-old drainage easement was, in fact, still an easement.

Bev Castillo arrived at 8:10, which she considered early and Dale considered late, a disagreement neither of them had ever spoken aloud but which expressed itself in small, persistent ways. Bev handled permits, zoning, and code enforcement for the town of Millhaven, a portfolio that sounded impressive until you learned that Millhaven had a population of 4,200 and that most code enforcement amounted to reminding Art Pollard that he couldn't store a boat on his front lawn.

She could, in theory, fine Art. She had the authority. But Art's wife had died two years ago and the boat was the last thing they'd bought together, and so Bev simply called him every few months and asked how he was doing, and mentioned the boat, and Art would say he was going to move it, and Bev would say that sounded good, and the boat stayed where it was.

This was, if you asked Bev, governing.

The third arrival was Marcus Webb, who handled public works — which in Millhaven meant he was the person you called when a pothole appeared, a streetlight went out, or a tree fell on something it shouldn't have. Marcus was twenty-eight and had taken the job three years ago because it was the first one that called him back after college, and had kept it because it turned out he was good at it and because the town had a way of becoming yours if you stayed long enough.

He came in through the side entrance, which was technically for employees only but which half the town used because parking was easier on that side. He was carrying a box of donuts, as he did every Tuesday, a tradition he'd started accidentally and now couldn't stop without feeling like he'd betrayed something.

"Glazed and two chocolate," he announced to Janet's office as he passed.

"You're a good man, Marcus Webb."

"I know it."

The last regular was Elena Marsh, the town clerk, who arrived at exactly 8:15 every day not because she was precise about time but because she dropped her son at school at 7:55 and the drive took exactly twenty minutes and she never once varied her route. Elena kept the minutes, managed the records, and served as the institutional memory that no filing system could replicate. If you needed to know when the water main on Birch Street was last replaced, you could search the records for an hour or you could ask Elena, who would tell you it was 2014, that it was Corrigan Plumbing who did the work, and that they'd come in $600 under bid, which she still found suspicious.

"Morning," she said, setting her bag down and reaching for the coffee pot, which was already half empty. She looked at it. She looked at Dale's closed office door. She poured what was left.

These five — Janet at the front desk, Dale in the corner office, Bev with her permits, Marcus with his potholes, Elena with her minutes — were the full-time staff of the Millhaven Municipal Building. There was also Roger, the maintenance contractor, who came on Mondays and Thursdays unless his back was acting up, and Linda from accounting who worked remotely three days a week from her kitchen table and appeared in person only for meetings and to use the color printer.

But the core was the five of them, and the building was theirs in the way that a workplace becomes yours when you've spent enough years learning its rhythms. They knew which radiator clanked, which window leaked, which ceiling tile was stained from the roof repair that hadn't quite taken. They knew that the meeting room smelled like old paper and that the break room microwave ran hot on the left side. They knew each other's coffee orders, parking spots, and the particular sound each of them made coming down the hall.

It was a Tuesday in early April, and nothing in particular was happening, which in Millhaven was most days and suited everyone fine.

Dale finished his call with Phil Henderson. Bev opened her laptop and began reviewing a fence permit. Marcus ate a donut over the sink in the break room so Janet wouldn't find crumbs. Elena started the minutes template for Thursday's council meeting, even though she already knew nothing would be on the agenda worth recording.

The building settled into its morning, warm and unhurried, like a dog turning twice before lying down.
