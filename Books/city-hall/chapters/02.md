The first visitor of the morning arrived at nine-fifteen and performed the stuck door in the usual way: two firm pulls, a pause of betrayal, then a look around for someone to explain.

Janet was already on her feet.

"Lift and push," she said through the glass, making the motion with one hand. The woman on the other side — mid-thirties, yellow rain jacket, a manila folder tucked under her arm — tried it, and the door came open with its familiar complaint.

"Is it always like that?"

"Since 1979, best we can tell." Janet held the door while the woman stepped inside and shook the drizzle off her jacket. Millhaven had been threatening weather all week and finally delivered something cold and discouraging just before nine, which the locals were receiving with the particular aggrievement of people who felt they'd already done their time with winter.

"I'm here about a business license," the woman said. "Or maybe a zoning question. I'm honestly not sure which one I need."

This was, in Janet's experience, the most honest thing anyone had said at the front desk in some time. Most people arrived with either too much certainty or too much documentation, sometimes both. This woman apparently knew she needed something and had the good sense to admit she wasn't sure what.

"Both, probably," Janet said. "Second door on the left."

---

Bev looked up from a fence permit she'd been staring at for twenty minutes trying to determine whether a proposed six-foot stockade on 14 Alder constituted a sight-line obstruction under the 2011 amendment, which it might, depending on how you felt about corner lots.

"Help you?"

The woman introduced herself: Nora Vance. She was interested in the vacant space on the corner of Maple and Third — the old brick building with the covered transom windows, the one that had been empty for three years.

"The Keller hardware," Bev said.

"I keep hearing that."

"Forty years. The family sold after Gerald retired." Bev set down the fence permit without regret. "What are you thinking about doing with it?"

A ceramics studio. Wheel-throwing classes, a small retail shelf. Maybe twelve students a week to start, once she had the equipment in.

Bev considered this. The building was C-1 commercial, which was clear. But instruction use — organized, tuition-based, recurring classes — sometimes required a different reading depending on occupancy count and the 2008 zoning amendment, which Bev had always felt contained language that could be read two ways on a cloudy day. She needed to pull the file.

---

Elena had it. Of course she had it.

"Keller building," Bev said from the records room doorway, and Elena was up before the sentence finished, moving toward the second bank of cabinets with the unhurried confidence of someone who already knew where she was going.

"Zoning application," Elena said, handing the folder over. Then she paused. "Hm."

"What."

"The 2001 survey is filed correctly." She sounded personally offended by this. "Someone knew what they were doing."

The 2008 amendment was in there too, and as Bev had suspected, the language around instructional occupancy was genuinely ambiguous — not wrong, just written the way municipal language sometimes got written when a subcommittee argued for three meetings and nobody wanted to lose. She'd need to do a formal reading and put it in writing. A week, possibly two.

She walked back to where Nora Vance was waiting.

"The good news is the space is commercially zoned with no obvious barriers. The complication is a 2008 amendment I want to read carefully before I tell you it's fine. That's a formality, not a roadblock — I just don't like telling people things are fine until I'm sure they're fine."

Nora Vance said she appreciated that. She left her card.

---

Marcus got a call just after eleven about a sinkhole on Birch Street, which turned out to be a subsidence — a slow collapse in the soil above what he was fairly sure was the old water main from the 2014 replacement. He stood on the shoulder and looked at the depression for a while, took two photographs, and called it in to the county maintenance line, where he left a message that was unlikely to be returned before Thursday.

He drove back with mud on his boots and a work order half-formed in his head.

---

By twelve-thirty the donuts were gone, which was faster than usual because Roger had shown up unexpectedly — his back was better, he said, and he'd come to look at the meeting room ceiling — and Roger had eaten two glazed without asking. Nobody said anything.

Dale had a call with Phil Henderson at one that went better than expected, because Phil had apparently spoken to his neighbor Gary, and Gary had told Phil the same thing Dale had been telling Phil for three months, but in slightly different words, and now Phil was prepared to accept it. Dale added Gary's name to a mental list of people he'd never met and already liked.

Elena updated the Keller building file with a note about the inquiry and put it back. Then she paused with her hand on the drawer. There was an older folder behind it — not misfiled, just tucked back — a variance application from 1994 for the building next door, which she'd never seen before, which meant it hadn't been indexed. She pulled it out, looked at it, and set it on her to-do stack. She'd get to it before Thursday. She always got to things before Thursday.

---

By four-thirty the drizzle had let up and the building had settled into the quiet of late afternoon, which was a different quiet from the morning quiet — warmer, more lived-in. Dale put his jacket on his hook because he'd never taken it home and wasn't about to start. Bev closed the fence permit with a decision she was prepared to defend. Marcus left a Post-it on Dale's door: *Birch/water main — worth a look* and a question mark.

Janet turned off the overhead light and left only the lamp on her desk, because the overhead made the room feel like a waiting room, and it wasn't a waiting room. It was where she worked.

Nothing in particular had happened. A door had stuck. A visitor had asked a reasonable question. A street had subsided two inches in a way nobody would probably notice for another week.

It had been a full day.
