The smell hit her before she was out of the car — wet ash and brine and something synthetic underneath both that she filed away without naming yet. The ocean was fifty meters west. Everything in Aldersham smelled like the ocean eventually, which was going to complicate things.

Nora pulled on her coveralls in the parking lot of the cannery while two town cops watched from their cruiser and the fire marshal's pickup idled near the loading doors, still running after however long he'd been here. She checked her camera battery, her evidence bags, her sample vials. Her kit was the same kit she'd carried for eleven years, different bag but the same contents in the same pockets. She'd tried once to explain this to her ex-husband and watched his face do the thing where he was being polite.

The Aldersham Fish and Processing Company — the sign was still legible, metal letters on the face of the surviving block wall — had been operating in some form since 1947. The original structure had been added to three times. The claim was $2.3 million, which was why she was here. Most of the roof was down. The processing floor was buried under it.

"Vass." Denton Ayres was picking his way out of the structure as she approached, booties over his boots, his turnout jacket open. He was close to sixty, big through the shoulders, the kind of man who had been formidable once and was still used to rooms accommodating him. "Flew you all the way up from Portland for this one."

"Drove," she said. "Four hours."

"Right." He had a folder under one arm. "Electrical, near as I can tell. Distribution panel on the east wall, old wiring throughout. The whole building was a violation waiting for someone to file it."

"When's your report due?"

"End of week." He handed her a diagram — floor plan with his suspected ignition point marked, fire spread pattern sketched in with a felt-tip. "I can walk you through it."

"I'll do a solo pass first," she said. "I'll come find you."

He shrugged the way people shrugged when they wanted you to know the shrug meant something. She went in.

The structure was three layers of reality stacked on top of each other: the pre-fire building, the fire itself as it had moved through it, and the suppression work, the water damage and the disturbance of the hose lines. Reading a scene like this was separating the layers back out. Nora moved slowly, photographing before she touched anything, stepping around debris rather than over it. She was looking at floor burn, at the V-patterns on the surviving walls, at the low burn indicators — floor joists, the bottom edges of metal equipment — that mapped where fire had pooled and for how long.

Ayres's ignition point was at the east wall, at the panel. It was plausible. The char was deep there, the wall materials had burned down to their fasteners. But thirty feet south, on the processing floor, there was a char pattern that didn't originate from there. It had its own V. It pointed at a drain in the floor. A confluence drain where the wash-down lines met.

She photographed it carefully, four angles, then crouched and held the back of her wrist over the debris without touching. The synthetic smell was stronger here. Not much. Plausibly nothing — processing plants used all kinds of cleaning chemicals, solvents, industrial lubricants. She pulled a sample vial and took material from the char line at the drain's edge and capped it and marked it and put it in her bag.

She didn't say anything to Ayres when she came back out.

"What'd you find?" he said.

"Nothing that changes your picture," she said, which was true. "I'll do a second pass this afternoon."

The owner was standing near the loading doors with a man in a jacket who had lawyer written in his posture and his shoes. Soledad Reyes was maybe fifty, square-jawed, her dark hair pulled back. Third-generation Aldersham. The company had been her grandfather's, then her mother's, then hers. She'd given a statement to the fire department, Nora had the copy, seventy-two words long.

Ayres handled the introduction. Reyes shook Nora's hand and said she understood there was an investigation, the company would cooperate fully, her attorney would be in touch. Her attorney nodded at this like it had been rehearsed.

"I just have a few questions," Nora said.

"Of course," Reyes said. "Though we'd prefer to coordinate through—"

"Were you on site last night?"

A pause. "No."

"What time did you hear about the fire?"

"A little after two. The night watchman called."

"And you came out?"

"At two-thirty, yes."

"Okay," Nora said. "That's all for now." She thanked them and walked back toward her car.

She sat in the rental for a while with the engine off and went through the photos. The drain char pattern. The samples. She was building a theory she didn't like building because it was based on almost nothing and she'd been wrong before and it cost things when you were wrong. She'd been wrong once, seven years ago, badly wrong, and the case had been reviewed and she'd been cleared and she still thought about it more than made sense.

She stopped on a photo of Reyes. Shot from across the lot, both of them in the frame, Nora's angle catching Reyes in three-quarter profile as she turned back toward her attorney.

The sleeve of her blouse had ridden up.

Nora zoomed in. The skin on Reyes's right forearm, just above the wrist, was mottled — pink and darker at the edges, the kind of mark that happened when something hot spattered. Could be old. Could be a cooking burn, a steam burn, a hundred things. Could be from last night.

She set the phone down on the passenger seat and looked at what was left of the Aldersham cannery and thought: it doesn't mean anything yet.

She said it out loud so she'd hear herself say it.
